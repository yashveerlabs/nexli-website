## Executive Summary

SWOT analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) grounds strategic planning in reality. Schools that conduct regular SWOTs report 65% better strategic decisions and faster response to market changes. This article teaches how to conduct a thorough SWOT and use it to strategize.

---

## The Four Areas

### Strengths (Internal, Positive)
- Strong faculty in Science/Math
- New infrastructure
- High parent satisfaction
- Strong sports/arts programs
- Strong board support
- Financial stability
- Brand reputation

**How to identify:** Ask staff, parents, students: "What are we good at?"

### Weaknesses (Internal, Negative)
- Weak English performance
- High staff turnover
- Aging infrastructure
- Limited tech adoption
- Low enrollment growth
- Poor transport system

**How to identify:** Where do complaints come from? Where do we struggle?

### Opportunities (External, Positive)
- Growing middle-class population (more school demand)
- NEP 2020 opening doors for innovation
- Online education demand (hybrid model)
- Emerging market for coding/skills
- Government schemes (RTE) creating access
- New residential area development nearby

**How to identify:** What's changing in your market? What could we capitalize on?

### Threats (External, Negative)
- Competitor school opening nearby
- Fee regulation (government caps)
- Declining birth rate in some areas
- Economic downturn affecting enrollment
- Regulatory changes (more compliance, less autonomy)
- Hybrid/online school options

**How to identify:** What worries you? What could hurt enrollment or finances?

## Conducting a SWOT

### Step 1: Gather Data
- Performance metrics (academics, enrollment, finances)
- Staff surveys
- Parent surveys
- Student surveys
- Competitive landscape (who are our competitors? What do they offer?)
- Market trends

### Step 2: Facilitate Workshop
- Bring together: Principal, VP, HODs, senior teachers
- Ask: "What are our strengths?" Brainstorm (no judging)
- Move to weaknesses, opportunities, threats

### Step 3: Evaluate & Prioritize
- Not all strengths matter equally. Which 3–4 are real differentiators?
- Not all weaknesses matter. Which affect student outcomes or enrollment?
- Not all opportunities are realistic. Which can we actually pursue?
- Which threats are most likely and most damaging?

### Step 4: Strategic Implications
- **Strengths + Opportunities = Growth Areas** (build on these)
- **Weaknesses + Threats = Risk Areas** (address urgently)
- **Strengths to Leverage = Differentiation** (how are we unique?)
- **Weaknesses to Address = Priority Improvement Areas**

## Real Example

| Area | Key Factors |
|---|---|
| **Strengths** | Strong Science faculty; new labs; high parent satisfaction; good sports; stable board |
| **Weaknesses** | Weak English performance; low tech adoption; some staff turnover; outdated office systems |
| **Opportunities** | Growing population nearby; demand for coding; hybrid model potential; RTE expansion |
| **Threats** | Two new schools opening in 2 years; economy slowing (fewer admissions); fee caps possible |

**Strategic implications:**
- **Leverage:** Science + New labs → Market as Science-focused school
- **Build:** Tech adoption + Coding trend → Launch coding program (differentiate)
- **Address:** English weakness → Improve urgently before new schools exploit it
- **Defend:** Anticipate new schools → Strengthen parent loyalty, expand programs
