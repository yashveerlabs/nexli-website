## The Problem: Student Assessment Strategies

Assessment is an annual summative event: board exam. Teachers rarely check understanding during the year. A weak student doesn't know they're weak until exam day.
            No opportunity to correct course. Results show 30% failures in math; school doesn't know why or what to do. Parents dispute grades with no evidence. No benchmark to assess
            if school is better than peer schools.

---

## The Consequences of Inaction

- Late intervention when student is already far behind
- No time to improve before annual exam
- Teacher assumptions about student understanding (often wrong)
- Unfair grading if not systematized
- Inability to show progress or growth

---

## Core Solutions

1. Implement formative assessment: quizzes, observations, projects throughout the term
2. Set grading criteria and rubrics so assessment is objective
3. Provide feedback, not just marks: what went well, what to improve
4. Use diverse assessment types: tests, projects, presentations, portfolios
5. Compare class performance to school/national benchmarks

---

## Best Practices for Success

- Assess weekly or bi-weekly, not just at term-end
- Provide feedback within 48 hours of assessment
- Separate formative (learning) assessment from summative (grading) assessment
- Use rubrics so students know criteria for success
- Show growth: compare student's performance this month vs. last month

## FAQ


Q: Should we have board exams every term or just at the end of year?
A: Board exams are year-end. Within year, use formative assessments (quizzes, projects, observations). Avoid mid-term board-style exams—they just stress students.

Q: How much should formative vs. summative assessment count?
A: Formative (practice) shouldn't carry grades. Summative (graded tests, exams) should count. Typical: 30% formative feedback + 70% summative grades.

Q: Can we use peer assessment?
A: Yes, for low-stakes work (essays, projects). Teach rubrics to students so they understand quality. Don't use peer grades for official marks.

Q: How do we handle cheating in assessments?
A: Clear consequences: first time = retest + discussion; repeated = parent meeting + reflection. Document everything.
