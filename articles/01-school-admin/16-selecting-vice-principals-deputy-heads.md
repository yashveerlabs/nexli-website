## Selecting and Developing Vice Principals and Deputy Heads

The Vice Principal is the principal's most critical hire. An outstanding VP can make a school thrive; a mediocre VP will slow it down. This article teaches principals how to evaluate candidates for VP roles, whether promoting internally or hiring externally, and how to develop new VPs to succeed.

---

## The Problem: Wrong VP Selection Cascades Into Failure

Choosing the wrong VP creates years of difficulty:

- **Poor Academic Leadership:** VP doesn't have pedagogy expertise. Curriculum isn't rigorous. Board exam results decline.
- **Staff Misalignment:** VP is liked by some teachers but not trusted by others. School splits into factions.
- **Bottleneck persists:** VP still refers every decision to principal. Delegation isn't working. Burnout continues.
- **Succession failure:** When principal leaves, school flounders because VP can't step up.
- **High VP turnover:** Frustrated VP leaves after 2 years. You repeat recruitment cycle, starting over each time.

Conversely, an excellent VP transforms the school. Teachers get consistent academic support. Curriculum is rigorous. Students thrive. Principal can focus on strategy instead of firefighting.

## VP Selection Criteria: What to Look For

### 1. Pedagogical Excellence
The VP must understand teaching and learning deeply. Red flags:

- Never observed their teaching (ask to sit in on a class)
- Can't articulate their teaching philosophy or approach
- Previous students' results didn't improve with them as teacher

Green flags:

- Students speak positively about their classes
- Their classes are orderly and engaging
- They've led professional development for other teachers
- They've achieved strong exam results in their subject

**Assessment method:** Observe 1-2 of their classes unannounced. Interview 5 current students about learning experience.

### 2. Leadership Experience
Look for evidence of leading others:

- Managed a department (coordinated curriculum, supported colleagues)
- Mentored new teachers or coached struggling students
- Led a school committee or project
- Took initiative fixing a school problem, not waiting for permission

**Red flags:** Only technical skills (strong teacher), no leadership experience

**Green flags:** Led initiatives, managed people, solved problems without being asked

**Assessment method:** Ask: "Tell me about a time you led a group or project. What challenges arose? How did you handle conflict?"

### 3. Emotional Intelligence
VP will interact with frustrated parents, struggling teachers, and difficult situations daily. EI is critical.

**Green flags:**
- Listens more than talks
- Shows empathy ("I understand why you're frustrated")
- Doesn't blame others when things go wrong
- Admits mistakes, learns from them
- Stays calm under pressure

**Red flags:**
- Blames teachers/students when results are weak
- Talks over others, doesn't listen
- Defensive when challenged
- Loses temper in stressful situations

**Assessment method:** Behavioral interview. Scenario-based questions: "A parent complained their child got a low mark they feel is unfair. The teacher says the mark is correct. How would you handle it?" Observe how candidate thinks through the situation.

### 4. Systems & Process Thinking
A good VP creates systems that scale, not chaos.

**Green flags:**
- Organizes work logically (timetable creation, curriculum tracking)
- Uses tools/technology effectively
- Asks "How do we prevent this problem next time?"
- Documents processes so others can follow them

**Red flags:**
- Disorganized, things fall through cracks
- Relies on memory instead of documented processes
- Problem-solving is reactive, not preventive

**Assessment method:** Ask about a project they managed. How did they organize it? Did they document processes? Did it run smoothly?

### 5. Alignment with School Culture & Vision
VP represents school values. If your school values innovation and VP is traditional, constant friction.

**Assessment method:** Explain school vision, values, strategic plan. Do they resonate? Do they ask questions showing understanding? Or do they nod without engagement?

## Internal Promotion vs. External Hire: Decision Framework

### Promote Internally If:
- You have a strong teacher (8+ years, excellent pedagogy, leadership potential)
- They've expressed interest in leadership
- Staff respect them (peer credibility)
- They understand your school's culture and systems
- You're willing to invest in their development (coaching, training)

**Advantages:** Lower cost, known quantity, maintains culture continuity, faster onboarding

**Disadvantages:** May lack formal leadership training, peer relationships can complicate authority, internal resentment from other candidates

### Hire Externally If:
- No strong internal candidate
- You need specialized expertise (IB curriculum, vocational education) that internal teacher lacks
- You want fresh perspective on school improvement
- Internal promotion would demoralize other teachers (not a good choice reasons)

**Advantages:** New ideas, leadership experience, no internal complications

**Disadvantages:** Higher cost, unknown quantity, slower integration into culture, need robust onboarding

## The VP Selection Process: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Define Role & Competencies (2 weeks before search)
Write detailed job description (see previous article). Identify 5-7 critical competencies:

1. Pedagogical leadership
2. Curriculum expertise
3. Staff management & coaching
4. Academic accountability (results orientation)
5. Strategic thinking
6. Communication (with teachers, parents, board)
7. Emotional intelligence

### Step 2: Create Assessment Rubric
Rate each candidate on competencies (scale 1-5):

```
COMPETENCY               IMPORTANCE   Internal Candidate   External Candidate A   External Candidate B
Pedagogical Leadership   Critical     4                    5                      4
Curriculum Expertise     Critical     4                    3                      4
Staff Coaching           Important    3                    5                      4
Results Orientation      Critical     4                    4                      4
Strategic Thinking       Important    3                    4                      3
Communication            Important    4                    4                      5
Emotional Intelligence   Important    4                    4                      4
TOTAL SCORE              (out of 35)  30                   33                     32
```

### Step 3: Multiple Assessment Methods
Use more than just interview (interviews are biased):

1. **Written assessment:** "Write a 2-page plan for how you'd improve board exam results." Assesses strategic thinking and writing clarity.
2. **Teaching demo:** Ask candidate to teach a sample class (45 min) to current Grade 8/9 class. Observe pedagogy live.
3. **Panel interview:** 3 people interview (principal, board member, peer academic leader from another school). Different perspectives catch blind spots.
4. **Reference checks:** Call 2-3 previous employers/colleagues. "How did they handle conflict? Did they hit targets? Would you hire them again?"
5. **Psychometric test:** Optional, but helpful. EQ assessment or leadership style assessment reveals emotional intelligence and leadership approach.

### Step 4: Final Decision & Offer
Candidate with highest total score becomes offer. Negotiate:

- **Salary:** VP should earn 30-40% more than senior teacher. Non-negotiable—underpaying VP signals you don't value role.
- **Title:** "Vice Principal" or "Academic Head"—define clearly
- **Reporting:** Reports directly to Principal, not board
- **Authority:** "Approved to make curriculum changes, teacher assignments, academic schedule without principal approval"
- **Evaluation:** Annual review against KPIs (board results, curriculum coverage, staff satisfaction)

## Onboarding the New VP: First 90 Days

### Month 1: Understanding (Week 1-4)
- **School immersion:** Meet all teachers, observe classes, understand current processes
- **Results analysis:** Review board exam results, identify weak subjects, see intervention programs
- **Curriculum review:** What's the current curriculum? Board alignment? Gaps?
- **One-on-ones:** Talk with each department head, understand strengths and challenges
- **Read:** School handbook, policies, prior year strategic plans, board meeting minutes

**Deliverable:** "100-day overview" presentation to board: "Here's what I found, here's what I'm seeing as priorities"

### Month 2: Assessment & Planning (Week 5-8)
- **First cycle:** Lead your first key activity (e.g., timetable creation, pre-board exam schedule)
- **Shadow principal:** Attend board meetings, budget reviews, key decisions
- **Team meetings:** Facilitate first department head meeting; practice leadership
- **Quick wins:** Identify 1-2 low-hanging fruit improvements (e.g., "Curriculum wasn't tracked; I created a tracking sheet")

**Deliverable:** 90-day plan with priorities for first year

### Month 3: Ownership (Week 9-12)
- **Full responsibility:** VP now owns academic function; principal oversight only
- **Monthly review:** Principal and VP review progress, discuss challenges, provide coaching
- **External support:** Connect with peer VPs at other schools for peer learning

**Deliverable:** First semester report card: academic results, staff feedback, implementation progress

## Developing Your VP: Ongoing Growth

### Invest in Development
A good VP can grow into Principal. Invest:

1. **Executive coaching:** 1-on-1 coaching (monthly) with external coach for first year. Focuses on leadership challenges, decision-making, staff management.
2. **Training programs:** "Effective School Leadership" (LEAD India, NASSCOM), "Curriculum Leadership," "Data-Driven Decision Making"
3. **Peer learning:** Monthly breakfast with Academic Heads from peer schools. Share challenges, learn from peers.
4. **Reading:** Assign books on leadership, pedagogy, school improvement
5. **Stretch assignments:** Give VP opportunity to lead school-wide initiatives (e.g., NEP 2020 curriculum redesign), not just daily operations

**Cost:** ₹2-3L annually. ROI is high—skilled VP improves school performance significantly.

### Regular Feedback & Evaluation
Monthly 1-on-1 with principal: "How's it going? What support do you need? Where can we improve?"

Quarterly formal review against KPIs:
- Board exam results on target
- Curriculum coverage on track
- Staff development happening
- Parent satisfaction with academics
- No significant conflicts unresolved

Annual comprehensive evaluation:
- 360-degree feedback (principal, department heads, sample of teachers, board member)
- Self-assessment
- Goal review (did VP hit targets?)
- Development plan for next year

## Real-World Example

**St. Xavier's School** (380 students, Bangalore): Principal Sarah needed a VP. She had strong teacher Priya (8 years, Grade 10 Math lead, respected) but also external candidate Arjun (10 years experience at peer school, MBA).

**Assessment process:**
- **Rubric scoring:** Priya scored 30/35 (strong pedagogy, culture fit, but lower strategic thinking). Arjun scored 33/35 (strong strategy and communication, but less curriculum depth).
- **Teaching demo:** Both excellent. Priya's class was warm and supportive; Arjun's was structured and rigorous.
- **Written assessment:** Arjun's plan was more strategic. Priya's was practical.
- **Panel interview:** Peer VP from another school preferred Priya (culture fit matters); board member preferred Arjun (new perspectives).

**Decision:** Sarah promoted Priya internally, with condition: Arjun mentors her for first 2 years on strategic planning. Hybrid approach: leverage Priya's culture fit, fill her strategic gap with external coaching.

**Result:** By Year 2, Priya became confident, capable VP. Board results improved. Teacher satisfaction with academic support rose. Priya could eventually step into principal role.

## FAQ

**Q: Is it better to promote internally or hire a VP from outside?**
A: If you have a strong internal teacher with leadership potential, promote internally (lower cost, better culture fit). If no strong candidate internally, hire externally (brings fresh perspective). Don't settle for mediocre internal candidate just because they're internal.

**Q: What should the salary range be for a VP?**
A: Typically 30-50% above senior teacher salary. If senior teacher earns ₹10L annually, VP earns ₹13-15L. Varies by city (Mumbai higher than tier-2 cities) and school size.

**Q: How do I handle other teachers who wanted the VP role but didn't get it?**
A: Be transparent. "We looked for strategic thinking, leadership experience, and pedagogy expertise. Priya had the strongest combination of these. Other candidates, you have strengths we'll develop in your growth plans." Offer runner-up training/mentoring to build for future leadership roles.

**Q: Can I have a VP focused only on Academics and hire someone else for Operations?**
A: Yes, if school is large (400+ students). VP handles academics; Operations Manager handles facilities, transport, canteen. But ensure clear coordination—VP shouldn't be surprised by operational issues.

**Q: How long should a VP stay in role before considering principal promotion?**
A: Minimum 3-4 years as VP to prove capability. After that, if they show strategic thinking and have developed strong team, they're principal-ready. Don't promote VP to principal too quickly—different skill set; give time to demonstrate.
