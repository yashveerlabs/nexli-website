## AI in Schools: Separating What Works from What Does Not

Artificial intelligence has entered education at every level, from marketing pitches on EdTech brochures to actual tools teachers use daily. School leaders need a clear picture of what AI can reliably do in educational settings today, what remains experimental, and what is being oversold.

This article does not argue for or against AI in education. It tries to give an honest account of where AI adds real value and where the claims outpace the evidence.

### What AI Does Well in Schools Right Now

**Multiple-choice and fill-in-the-blank grading:** This is the clearest win. A system that grades a 50-question MCQ paper is fast, accurate, and completely consistent. It does not make arithmetic errors. It does not have a bad day. The moment a student submits, the score is calculated. Teachers get class-wide performance reports showing which questions most students got wrong, which points to gaps in teaching or poorly written questions. This works today, at scale, reliably.

**Plagiarism and copy detection:** Comparing submitted text against a database of prior submissions and web content is a practical AI application with a decade of use. It is not perfect but it surfaces obvious copy-paste cases quickly. Teachers can investigate flagged submissions rather than reviewing every paper manually.

**Anomaly detection in operational data:** AI patterns can flag unusual behavior in school data. A student whose attendance drops sharply from one month to the next. A teacher whose mark-entry pattern differs significantly from historical norms. Fee payment patterns that suggest errors in account mapping. These are statistical anomalies, not complex human judgments, and they are exactly what machine learning handles well.

**Content recommendation in learning apps:** Apps that serve practice problems at the right difficulty level based on a student's recent performance are applying AI in a well-understood way. This is not sentient teaching. It is an algorithm that says: "This student got 8 of 10 easy problems right, show them medium problems." This works.

### What Is Experimental or Overpromised

**Subjective essay grading:** Natural language processing can produce a rough score for an essay based on sentence complexity, vocabulary range, and structural features. What it cannot do reliably is evaluate whether an argument is logically sound, whether the student understood the question, or whether a creative piece achieves its intended effect. AI essay grading is useful as a first-pass tool, not as a replacement for teacher evaluation.

**Adaptive learning at the curriculum level:** The claim that an AI can personalize an entire curriculum for each student in real time is far ahead of what current systems actually deliver. What is currently available is difficulty adjustment within a specific subject's practice problems. Full curriculum adaptation involving sequencing topics, adjusting pedagogy, and evaluating conceptual understanding remains an open research problem.

**Emotion and engagement detection:** Some products claim to detect student engagement or emotional state through webcam analysis. The accuracy of these systems in real classroom conditions is poor, and the privacy implications of monitoring student facial expressions are significant. Schools should be very cautious about any product making these claims.

**Chatbots for complex parent queries:** A chatbot can answer "What time does school start?" or "What is the fee due date?" from a database. It cannot handle nuanced questions about a student's discipline record, a fee dispute, or a learning difficulty. Chatbots that overpromise human-level understanding of complex school situations create frustration when they fail to deliver.

### How to Evaluate AI Claims from EdTech Vendors

When a vendor says their product uses AI, ask these specific questions:

What data does the AI train on, and how was that data collected? AI systems trained on data from schools in other countries or contexts may not work well for Indian classrooms.

What is the accuracy rate, and how was it measured? A vendor claiming "95% accuracy" without explaining what that means (95% of what? Compared to what baseline?) is not giving you useful information.

What happens when the AI is wrong? Every AI system makes errors. The important question is how the error is surfaced, who reviews it, and how it is corrected.

Does the AI output go directly into student records, or does a teacher review it first? Any AI system making consequential decisions about students (marks, flags, recommendations) should have human review in the loop.

### A Framework for Schools Approaching AI

Treat AI as a tool that assists humans, not one that replaces human judgment on consequential decisions about students. Start with operational uses (attendance anomaly alerts, fee default flags, MCQ scoring) where the stakes of an individual error are low and the volume benefits are high. Introduce AI for academic assessment only as a support tool with teacher override.

Build staff capacity to interpret AI outputs critically. A system that flags a student as at-risk is the beginning of a conversation, not the end of one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should Indian schools start adopting AI tools now?**
A: For operational tools (MCQ grading, anomaly detection, chatbot FAQs for simple queries), yes, where the tools are proven and the stakes of individual errors are manageable. For high-stakes academic decisions (final grades, promotion, exclusion), AI should assist human decision-makers, not replace them. Indian schools should also ensure any AI tool handling student data is DPDP Act 2023 compliant.

**Q: Is adaptive learning AI suitable for Indian classrooms?**
A: Current adaptive learning tools work best for practice and revision in subjects like mathematics and languages where right/wrong answers are clear. They are less suited to project-based learning, discussions, or subjects requiring judgment. Schools should evaluate specific tools in their context rather than relying on general claims.

**Q: What data does an AI system need to work effectively in a school?**
A: AI systems need clean, consistent, historical data. A school that has been recording attendance, marks, and behavior accurately in a digital system for two or more years has the data needed for meaningful AI analysis. A school migrating from paper cannot expect useful AI insights immediately because historical data is missing or incomplete.

**Q: What are the DPDP implications of using AI with student data?**
A: Using student data to train or run AI models is processing personal data under DPDP Act 2023. Schools need consent for this use, must limit data use to stated purposes, and should not share student data with AI vendors without a data processing agreement. This applies whether the AI is in the school's own system or a third-party EdTech product.

**Q: Will AI replace teachers?**
A: No. AI handles pattern recognition, repetitive tasks, and data analysis. Teaching involves human relationships, mentorship, contextual judgment, and the ability to adapt to the unpredictable needs of specific children in specific moments. These are not things AI systems can replicate. The more accurate frame is that AI can reduce the administrative burden on teachers so they spend more time teaching.
