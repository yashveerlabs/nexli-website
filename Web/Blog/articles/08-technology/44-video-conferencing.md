## Why Schools Are Moving Parent-Teacher Meetings to Video

Video conferencing for parent-teacher meetings has grown significantly in Indian schools, particularly since parents demonstrated willingness to engage online during the COVID-19 period. For many parents, a 20-minute video call at 7 pm is more accessible than taking half a day off work to visit the school campus during hours when they cannot easily leave the office.

This is a genuine improvement in parent engagement for working parents, parents who commute long distances, and parents who feel intimidated by the physical school environment. The challenge is implementing it with the right tools, proper scheduling, and appropriate consent procedures.

## Choosing a Video Conferencing Tool

Several tools work well for school parent-teacher meetings. The choice depends on what the school already uses and what parents can access:

**Google Meet:** Free with Google Workspace for Education. No download required for parents; they join through a browser link. Teachers can schedule directly from Google Calendar and send the link to parents via email or the school's parent portal. Suitable for most Indian schools already using Google Workspace.

**Zoom:** Widely familiar and reliable. Free for 40-minute meetings with up to 100 participants (sufficient for one-on-one PTMs). Paid plans remove time limits. Parents need to download the app or join through a browser. Zoom provides good controls for the host (teacher): mute, waiting room, recording control.

**Microsoft Teams:** Suitable for schools in the Microsoft ecosystem. Meetings can be scheduled from Teams and the link shared with parents. Parents join without a Teams account via a browser.

**WhatsApp Video Call:** Simple, familiar, and works on all smartphones. Suitable for brief informal check-ins but lacks scheduling integration, recording capability, and attendance tracking. Not recommended as the primary tool for formal PTMs.

When choosing, consider:
- **Parent device accessibility:** Can parents join without downloading an app?
- **Bandwidth:** Low-bandwidth mode availability matters for parents in areas with slow internet.
- **Scheduling integration:** Can the link be sent through the school's communication system automatically?

## Scheduling Video PTMs Efficiently

Ad hoc scheduling of individual video calls creates administrative chaos. A structured approach:

**Define time slots.** Offer fixed time slots throughout the PTM day (e.g., every 15 or 20 minutes per teacher). Parents book their slot in advance.

**Use the school's parent portal or a booking tool.** Parents should be able to see available slots and book without calling the school office. Simple online booking tools (Calendly, Google Forms with a spreadsheet backend, or the school's ERP parent portal if it includes appointment booking) work well.

**Send calendar invites with the meeting link automatically.** When a parent books a slot, they should receive a calendar invite with the video meeting link immediately. This avoids last-minute link sharing on the day.

**Build in buffer time.** Allow 2-3 minutes between each meeting slot for the teacher to make notes from the previous meeting and prepare for the next.

**Maintain a waitlist.** If the PTM day includes slots across the week, last-minute cancellations can be filled from a waitlist.

## Accessibility Considerations

Not all parents have equal access to video conferencing:

**Bandwidth limitations:** Parents in rural areas or those on limited data plans may struggle with video. Provide an option for audio-only calls and ensure teachers can conduct the meeting effectively without seeing the parent.

**Device availability:** Some parents share a single phone across multiple family members. Offer evening and weekend slots to accommodate parents who cannot join during working hours.

**Language:** In multi-language schools, consider whether the teacher and parent share a common language for the video call and arrange an interpreter if needed.

**Disability access:** Parents or teachers with hearing impairment need a text-based alternative. Provide an option for the PTM to be conducted partly via typed chat in the video platform.

## Recording Consent Under the DPDP Act 2023

Recording a video PTM session involves processing personal data, including the visual image and voice of the parent, potentially sensitive information about the student shared during the discussion, and the teacher's personal data.

Under the DPDP Act 2023:

- **Consent is required before recording.** Both parties (the teacher and the parent) must be informed that the session will be recorded and must consent before the recording begins.
- **Purpose must be stated.** If the school records PTM sessions for quality assurance, training, or dispute resolution, this purpose must be stated at the time consent is sought.
- **Retention period must be defined.** Recordings should not be retained indefinitely. Define when they will be deleted (e.g., one term after the meeting date).
- **Access must be restricted.** Recordings of individual parent-teacher discussions are highly sensitive. Access should be limited to the teacher, the relevant administrator (in case of escalation), and the parent who participated.

Best practice: do not record PTM sessions unless there is a specific, documented reason. For general PTM purposes, recording is unnecessary and creates more data protection obligations than it resolves.

If a recording is needed (for example, a parent has requested a record of an agreement reached), inform all parties before the session begins, obtain verbal or written consent, and store the recording with restricted access and a defined deletion date.

## Making Video PTMs Effective

**Prepare in advance.** The teacher should review the student's attendance record, recent marks, and any notable events before the meeting. A video call with no preparation is as unproductive as an unprepared in-person meeting.

**Be structured but human.** 15-20 minutes is brief. Begin with something positive, then address concerns, then agree on a clear action. Ending with "what can we do together to support [student name] in the next term?" is more productive than a list of problems.

**Document outcomes immediately.** After each call, the teacher should note the key points discussed and any agreements made. This record is useful if the discussion is contested later and allows the school to track whether agreed actions are followed up.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do parents in India prefer video PTMs or in-person meetings?**
A: Preference varies by parent demographic. Working parents and those with long commutes typically prefer video. Parents who want a stronger personal connection or whose children have significant issues to discuss often prefer in-person. Offering both options is the most inclusive approach.

**Q: How long should a video PTM session be?**
A: 15-20 minutes per student is standard. This is enough time for a substantive conversation without running over schedule. For students with significant issues (academic, pastoral, or disciplinary), schedule a dedicated longer session separately.

**Q: Can a school record PTM sessions without telling parents?**
A: No. Recording without consent is not permissible under the DPDP Act 2023 and is ethically inappropriate. All recording must be disclosed in advance with consent obtained before the session begins.

**Q: What if a parent misses their booked PTM slot?**
A: Define a no-show policy in advance: missed slots are not automatically rescheduled unless cancelled with at least 2 hours notice. Provide one opportunity to reschedule within the same PTM period.

**Q: Is Google Meet safe for discussing student data with parents?**
A: Google Meet uses end-to-end encryption for video and audio. As long as the meeting is private (not a public link posted anywhere), it is appropriate for discussing student matters with parents. The key risk is not the platform security but improper access to the meeting link: links should be sent to the correct parent only.
