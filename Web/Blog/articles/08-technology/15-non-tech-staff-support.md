## How Do You Support Non-Technical School Staff During an ERP Rollout?

Supporting non-technical school staff during ERP adoption requires four things: simplified interfaces that show only the features they need, printed quick-reference guides for the 5 tasks they perform daily, a buddy system pairing each non-tech user with a more confident colleague, and realistic patience during the 6-8 week adjustment period. Office staff, accountants, and transport managers have distinct workflows; their support plans should be equally distinct.

## Who Are Non-Technical School Staff?

In a typical Indian school, the following staff categories often have lower technology exposure than teachers and are critical ERP users:

- **Office administrators:** Admission clerks, front desk staff, document management. They process student records and parent queries.
- **Accounts staff:** Fee collectors, daily receipt generators, monthly collection reconcilers, payroll processors.
- **Transport managers/wardens:** Route assignment, vehicle coordination, student boarding lists.
- **Hostel wardens:** Room allocation, nightly roll call, student welfare records.
- **Lab/workshop assistants:** Equipment inventory, usage logs.
- **Library staff:** Book catalog, issue and return.

These staff are often the most intensive users of the ERP in terms of daily transaction volume. An accounts staff member may process 30-60 fee transactions in a single day. Getting their support right is essential for operational continuity.

## Understanding Their Technology Starting Point

Before designing support, assess what each group currently uses:

- Do they use a smartphone for personal banking (PhonePe, GPay)? If yes, they can likely learn ERP payment workflows more easily than you think.
- Do they use Excel or any computer-based system currently? If yes, they have basic data entry habits.
- Are they comfortable with a web browser (can they open a URL and navigate)? If not, this is a skill to build before ERP training.

Tailor the starting point of training to where each person actually is, not where you assume they are.

## Simplified Interfaces Reduce Overwhelm

One of the most effective support mechanisms for non-tech staff is not training at all — it is ensuring they only see the parts of the ERP relevant to their job.

A fee collection clerk does not need to see the HR module, the timetable module, or the library system. If she opens the ERP and sees 12 menu items when her job requires 3, she is immediately overwhelmed and will call for help for tasks she could otherwise handle herself.

Work with your ERP vendor to configure role-based access so that each non-tech staff member's dashboard shows only their modules. An accounts clerk's landing page should show: today's fee collection, pending invoices, and the fee report. Nothing else.

This is not about restricting access — it is about reducing visual noise for users who are still building confidence.

## Printed Quick-Reference Guides

A printed, laminated quick-reference guide is one of the most effective low-cost training tools for non-tech staff.

**Design principles for a quick-reference guide:**
- One page, printed in large enough font to read comfortably.
- Laminated and attached to the workstation or kept on the desk.
- Covers exactly five tasks: the five things this person does most frequently.
- Step-by-step with numbered steps (not paragraphs of explanation).
- Include screenshots of the actual ERP screens, labeled with arrows.
- Include the change champion's name and phone number at the bottom.

**Sample quick-reference guide for a fee collection clerk:**
1. How to open the fee collection screen.
2. How to search for a student by name or admission number.
3. How to generate a fee invoice.
4. How to record a cash payment and print the receipt.
5. How to record a UPI payment and verify the transaction.

That's it. When the fee clerk needs to generate the monthly outstanding report, the quick guide doesn't cover it — she calls the change champion. The guide handles 80% of her daily work without any help needed.

## The Buddy System

Pair each non-tech staff member with a buddy: a colleague who is slightly more technology-comfortable and whose workstation is nearby.

The buddy relationship is informal but structured:
- The buddy is available for "quick questions" during working hours.
- The buddy does not do the work for the non-tech staff member — they sit alongside and explain.
- The buddy relationship typically runs for 6-8 weeks until the non-tech staff member is handling daily tasks independently.

Choose buddies carefully. The best buddy is someone in the same department (an accounts buddy for accounts staff, a senior teacher as a buddy for a junior teacher), not necessarily the most technical person in the school. Proximity matters: a buddy who sits 5 meters away is more useful than a technically expert colleague on another floor.

## Training Adaptations for Non-Tech Users

Standard 90-minute group training sessions may not be enough for genuinely non-tech staff. Plan for additional support:

**Practice sessions:** After the initial training, schedule 2-3 short practice sessions of 30-45 minutes where non-tech staff work through real tasks (creating a test invoice, processing a test payment) with the change champion watching.

**Slow-paced walkthrough:** Non-tech users often need the same process explained 3-4 times before it becomes comfortable. Avoid showing impatience. The third explanation takes 5 minutes; a staff member who never learns the system properly costs far more.

**Relate to familiar experiences:** Connect ERP tasks to things the staff member already does. "Generating an invoice in the ERP is like filling out a fee receipt, except the system fills in the amounts for you and the number is automatic." "Searching for a student works like searching in Google — type the name, tap the student's name in the results."

## What to Do When Non-Tech Staff Are Stuck

Establish a clear escalation path that non-tech staff can use without feeling judged for needing help:

1. Try the quick-reference guide for the task.
2. Ask the buddy.
3. Call the change champion.
4. The change champion escalates to vendor support if needed.

Make step 1 and 2 feel completely safe: asking for help is not a sign of failure. Non-tech staff who feel judged for asking questions will avoid using the ERP at all rather than risk feeling incompetent.

## Patience: The 6-8 Week Rule

For genuinely non-tech staff, 6-8 weeks of daily use is the typical timeline before tasks feel routine. During this period:
- Expect a higher volume of questions and mistakes.
- Expect some regression (going back to paper for a task they haven't done in the ERP yet).
- Expect to hear "the old way was easier" from staff who are still in the learning curve.

Patience is not the same as accepting non-use. The expectation remains that ERP use is required. The patience is in allowing the learning curve without punitive pressure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What if an accounts staff member processes a fee payment incorrectly in the ERP?**
A: Show her how to correct it before go-live, not after the first mistake occurs. Most fee errors in an ERP can be reversed or corrected with an edit or a reversal entry. Demonstrating this process in training dramatically reduces anxiety about mistakes.

**Q: Should we hire tech-comfortable staff to handle ERP-heavy roles?**
A: For new hires in data-intensive roles, ERP familiarity is a reasonable hiring preference. For existing staff, invest in support before assuming replacement is needed. Most non-tech staff become competent users within one term with proper support.

**Q: What if an older accounts staff member simply cannot use the ERP after 3 months of support?**
A: After 3 months of structured support (training, buddy system, practice sessions), a performance gap must be addressed formally. Consider whether the role can be modified (e.g., she handles physical receipt filing while a colleague handles ERP entry) or whether redeployment is appropriate.

**Q: Is a printed quick-reference guide still relevant when the ERP has built-in help?**
A: Yes. A non-tech staff member who is stuck at 3 PM will not navigate to the ERP's help section. She will look at the laminated card on her desk. In-app help is a supplement, not a replacement, for printed guides with non-tech users.

**Q: How do we measure if non-tech staff support is working?**
A: Track the number of ERP transactions processed by each non-tech staff member per week. Compare to the expected volume (e.g., a fee clerk should be processing 20-40 transactions/day at full adoption). Rising transaction counts indicate growing competence and confidence.
