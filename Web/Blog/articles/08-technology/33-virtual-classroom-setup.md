## What a Virtual Classroom Actually Requires

Setting up a virtual classroom for schools is more than installing a video conferencing app. A functioning virtual classroom needs: a reliable video platform that teachers can use without technical support, a scheduling system that students and parents can follow, a way to track attendance, a policy on recording, and strategies to keep students engaged when the teacher cannot see their faces.

Schools that treat virtual classrooms as just "Zoom meetings" typically encounter problems within the first few weeks: students join from different devices with audio issues, attendance is informal and unverifiable, and teachers have no record of who participated.

## Choosing a Video Platform

The video platform is the foundation. The main options for Indian schools are:

**Google Meet:** Included with Google Workspace for Education. Free for qualifying schools. Integrates directly with Google Classroom for scheduling. Students can join from any browser without downloading an app. Very suitable for K-12 schools already using Google Workspace.

**Zoom Education:** A paid product with features specifically designed for classrooms, including waiting rooms, breakout rooms, teacher controls for microphone and camera, and attendance reporting. The desktop app offers better stability than browser-based alternatives on low-bandwidth connections.

**Microsoft Teams:** Suitable for schools in the Microsoft ecosystem. Class teams can schedule meetings directly, share class materials, and track assignment completion alongside video calls.

**Jitsi Meet:** Open-source and free. Can be self-hosted. No account required for participants. Suitable for privacy-conscious schools that want to avoid commercial platforms, but requires IT support for self-hosting and has fewer classroom-specific features.

When choosing, consider:
- Device compatibility: Will students access from phones, tablets, or laptops?
- Bandwidth: Low-bandwidth modes (audio-only or low-resolution video) matter in areas with slow internet.
- Teacher comfort: The best platform is the one teachers will use consistently.

## Scheduling Virtual Classes

Random scheduling creates confusion. Students need a predictable structure:

**Use the school timetable.** Virtual classes should follow the same timetable as physical classes wherever possible. This gives students and parents a consistent routine.

**Send calendar invites or links in advance.** A recurring meeting link that is the same each week for each subject reduces the chaos of sharing links through WhatsApp before every class.

**Post reminders through the school communication channel.** A reminder the evening before and 10 minutes before class start improves attendance. The school ERP or parent portal can send automated reminders if configured.

**Build in buffer time.** Starting a virtual class takes longer than starting a physical class. Students join late, test their audio, and wait for the teacher to admit them from the waiting room. Build a 5-minute buffer into the schedule.

## Tracking Attendance in Virtual Classes

Attendance in a virtual classroom is less formal than physical attendance but still needs to be recorded. Options:

**Platform-level attendance reports:** Zoom and Google Meet both generate participation reports showing who joined and for how long. Download these after each session and attach them to the class record.

**Manual roll call:** The teacher calls names at the start of class. Simple but time-consuming for large classes.

**In-session poll or form:** A short Google Form or poll posted in the first 5 minutes that students must complete. This doubles as engagement and attendance.

**LMS submission as attendance proxy:** Requiring a brief comment or response in the LMS within 30 minutes of class end can serve as a participation record.

Attendance data captured in a virtual class should be entered into the school ERP just like physical attendance, so the official record remains complete.

## Recording Policies

Recording virtual classes creates significant benefits (students can re-watch, absent students can catch up) and significant risks (recordings include student faces and voices, which are personal data under the DPDP Act 2023).

Before recording any class:

- Obtain informed consent from parents, especially for students under 18.
- Store recordings in a system with access controls (not an open YouTube channel or unsecured Google Drive folder).
- Set a retention period (most schools delete recordings after one term).
- Define who can access recordings: the teacher and enrolled students only.
- Communicate the recording policy in writing to parents at the start of the year.

## Keeping Students Engaged

Engagement in virtual classrooms declines faster than in physical classrooms. Practical strategies that work:

**Keep sessions to 30-45 minutes maximum.** Attention spans online are shorter. Break a 90-minute period into two 40-minute sessions with a break.

**Use polls and quizzes.** Platforms like Mentimeter, Kahoot, and built-in Zoom polls add interaction without requiring the teacher to stop and call on individuals.

**Enable breakout rooms for group work.** Small group discussions break the monotony of one teacher talking to a grid of faces.

**Assign brief tasks mid-session.** "Open your textbook and copy the definition in the chat box." This keeps students active.

**Check comprehension verbally, not just in chat.** Ask a question and unmute students randomly to respond. This maintains accountability.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can a school run virtual classes without paying for any software?**
A: Yes. Google Workspace for Education Fundamentals (which includes Google Meet and Google Classroom) is free for qualifying schools. Jitsi Meet is also free and open-source. Both can support virtual classroom delivery at no software cost.

**Q: What internet speed is needed for a virtual class?**
A: A stable connection of at least 1.5 Mbps per participant for video. For schools with shared bandwidth, audio-only or low-resolution video modes reduce requirements significantly. Advise students on low-bandwidth households to use audio-only.

**Q: How do teachers handle students who join late or leave early?**
A: Define a late-join cutoff (e.g., no attendance credit for joining more than 10 minutes late). Communicate this policy in writing to parents before the term starts. Most platforms show join and leave times in their attendance report.

**Q: Are recordings of student video classes legal in India?**
A: Recording is permissible with proper consent under the DPDP Act 2023. Schools must inform parents that classes will be recorded, explain how recordings will be used and stored, and obtain consent before recording begins.

**Q: What if a student has no internet access for virtual classes?**
A: This is an equity issue that virtual classroom policy must address. Schools should identify students without internet access at the start of the year and provide alternatives: printed materials, recorded sessions on a shared device, or access from the school library or computer lab.
