## What Should Indian Schools Negotiate in an ERP Contract?

An ERP contract is a multi-year financial and operational commitment. The key terms to negotiate are: uptime SLA with penalties, complete data ownership and export rights, lock-in period and early exit clauses, support hours and response times, customization scope and pricing, and annual price escalation caps. Schools that sign without reviewing these terms often face expensive surprises in year two and difficulty leaving a vendor whose system isn't working.

## Why Contract Negotiation Matters for Schools

School ERP vendors have standard contracts drafted to protect the vendor's interests. These contracts are legal documents, and schools that sign without reviewing them have limited recourse when things go wrong.

A school trust signing a 3-year ERP contract is committing potentially ₹15-20 lakh or more. This deserves the same review as any major capital expenditure. Involve your school's legal advisor or finance committee chair in contract review, not just the IT coordinator.

## Term 1: Service Level Agreement (SLA) for Uptime

The SLA specifies how reliably the system must be available. This matters because fee collection, attendance, and exam records depend on the system being accessible.

**What to look for:**
- Uptime guarantee as a percentage: 99.5% means approximately 44 hours of unplanned downtime per year. 99.9% means approximately 9 hours per year. Anything below 99% is concerning for a school that runs daily operations.
- Measurement period: Monthly or annually? Monthly measurement catches recent degradation that annual measurement hides.
- Planned maintenance exclusions: Vendors typically exclude scheduled maintenance windows from uptime calculations. Ensure maintenance is limited to nights and weekends, with advance notice of at least 48 hours.
- Remedies for SLA breach: What happens if the vendor fails to meet the SLA? Common remedies include service credits (a percentage of monthly fees). Ensure the remedy is meaningful, not a ₹500 credit on a ₹40,000/month contract.
- Critical period exclusions: Negotiate that certain periods (exam season, fee collection days at the start of each term) have enhanced SLA commitments or on-call support.

**Negotiation position:** Request 99.5% or higher uptime SLA with service credits of 10% of monthly fee for each percentage point of monthly uptime below the guaranteed level.

## Term 2: Data Ownership and Export Rights

This is the most commonly neglected and most important clause in ERP contracts.

**Non-negotiable positions:**
- The school owns all data uploaded to and generated in the ERP. The vendor has no right to use your data for any purpose other than operating the service.
- The school can export a complete copy of all data at any time, on demand, at no additional cost.
- Data must be exportable in a standard, human-readable format: CSV, JSON, or Excel. "Proprietary format available on request" is not acceptable.
- Upon contract termination, the vendor must provide the school with a complete data export within 30 days and must delete all school data within 90 days (with written confirmation of deletion).

**Warning signs:**
- Contract states that data "may be used for service improvement purposes" without clear limitations.
- No export functionality described in the contract.
- Export is listed as a professional services item with a fee.
- No termination data return clause.

Schools that don't secure data portability find themselves trapped: leaving the vendor means losing access to years of student records and fee history.

## Term 3: Lock-In Period and Early Exit

Most ERP vendors offer 1-year, 2-year, or 3-year contracts. Longer commitments typically come with better pricing.

**What to negotiate:**
- A performance-based exit clause: If the vendor fails to meet the SLA for 3 consecutive months, the school has the right to exit the contract without penalty.
- If the vendor is acquired by another company, the school has the right to renegotiate or exit within 60 days.
- Early exit penalty: If you must exit before the contract end, what is the penalty? A common formula is 30-50% of remaining contract value. Negotiate this down or add conditions that waive it for vendor breach.
- For first-time ERP implementations, negotiate a 1-year initial term rather than 3-year, even if it costs more. This limits your exposure if the implementation fails.

## Term 4: Support Hours and Response Times

Generic "24/7 support" claims need to be quantified in the contract.

**What the contract should specify:**
- Support hours: Standard business hours (9 AM - 6 PM, Mon-Sat) vs. extended hours vs. 24/7 for critical issues.
- Response time by severity:
  - Critical (system down, fee collection inaccessible): Response within 2 hours, resolution within 8 hours.
  - High (a module is non-functional): Response within 4 hours, resolution within 24 hours.
  - Medium (functional issue with workaround): Response within 8 hours, resolution within 48 hours.
  - Low (cosmetic issues, non-urgent questions): Response within 24 hours, resolution within 5 business days.
- Support channels: Email only, phone, and WhatsApp/chat. Confirm which channels apply to which severity levels.
- Dedicated contact: Is there a named account manager or does all support go to a shared ticket queue?
- Language support: Hindi or regional language support for non-English-speaking staff.

## Term 5: Customization Costs and Scope

Many ERP vendors demonstrate features during sales that turn out to require paid customization. Protect against this with clear contract language.

**What to clarify before signing:**
- List the specific reports, formats, and workflows that the vendor demonstrated and ensure these are described as "included in the standard subscription" in the contract.
- Define the scope of included customization: Are minor changes to fee receipt format included? What about adding a custom field to the student record?
- Customization fee structure: If customization is billed separately, at what rate and with what approval process?
- Any state board or CBSE format requirements: Confirm these are included in standard scope, not custom development.

**Red flag:** A vendor who says "we'll take care of any customizations you need" without specifying cost in the contract is setting up a future billing dispute.

## Term 6: Price Escalation

ERP subscriptions are recurring costs. Without a price escalation cap, your ₹4 lakh/year contract can become ₹7 lakh/year by year three.

**Negotiate:**
- Maximum annual price increase cap: 10% is common; 5% is achievable for multi-year contracts.
- Price increase linked to CPI or another index, not arbitrary vendor decision.
- If price increases exceed the cap, the school has the right to exit without penalty with 90 days notice.

## Term 7: Implementation Timeline and Milestones

If the vendor delays implementation, the school suffers: staff can't use the system, data migration is incomplete, the go-live date slips.

**Add to the contract:**
- Implementation milestones with dates (system setup complete by X, training complete by Y, go-live by Z).
- Consequences for vendor-caused delays: service credits or extended subscription period at no cost.
- School-caused delays: Clarify what happens if the school is responsible for delay (most contracts have "reasonable extension" language; ensure "reasonable" is defined).

## How to Use These Terms in a Negotiation

Vendors expect negotiation. Approach it professionally:

1. List your non-negotiables (data ownership, export rights) and your "nice to have" items (lower price escalation cap, named account manager).
2. Ask for the vendor's best offer on all items before revealing your priorities.
3. Trade lower-priority items for higher-priority wins.
4. Get all agreed terms in writing as a contract amendment before signing. Verbal commitments don't count.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should a school hire a lawyer to review an ERP contract?**
A: For contracts above ₹5 lakh total commitment or for 3+ year terms, yes. Legal review of the data ownership clause and termination terms alone is worth the cost. Many school trusts have a panel advocate who can review commercial contracts affordably.

**Q: What is a typical ERP contract length for Indian schools?**
A: 1-year, 2-year, and 3-year contracts are all common. First-time ERP implementations are better served by a 1-year initial term with a renewal option. Switching an ERP after a failed implementation on a 3-year contract is very expensive.

**Q: Can we get out of an ERP contract if the system doesn't work as promised?**
A: Only if your contract includes a performance-exit clause. Without it, you are liable for the remaining contract value. This is why negotiating exit clauses before signing is critical.

**Q: What happens to student data if the ERP vendor goes out of business?**
A: Your contract should include a data escrow clause: the vendor deposits current code and data with a neutral third party, which releases it to you if the vendor ceases operations. This is rare in practice but worth negotiating for smaller vendors.

**Q: Is it reasonable to ask for a free pilot period before signing?**
A: Yes. A 30-60 day pilot with a limited number of users and modules before full contract commitment is a reasonable request for a first-time ERP relationship. Vendors confident in their product will agree.
