## Managing E-Books in School Libraries: What You Need to Know Before You Start

E-books management in school libraries is more complex than it appears. The challenge is not the technology itself: most e-book platforms are straightforward to access. The challenge is licensing, access control, device requirements, and the question of how you prove to yourself and your management that the digital resources are actually being used.

Before a school invests in e-books, these four questions need clear answers.

### Licensing Models for School E-Books

Unlike a physical book, where the school owns the copy outright after purchase, digital book licenses come in several forms with different implications for how many students can read the book simultaneously and for how long:

**Simultaneous user license:** The school pays for a defined number of simultaneous users (for example, 10 users at once). When the 11th student tries to access the title, they join a queue or see "currently unavailable." This model is common for academic publishers and is similar to how a physical library works (limited copies). Cost is typically per-title, per-user-limit.

**Unlimited access license:** The school pays a higher fee but any number of students can access the title simultaneously. This makes sense for reference books, textbooks used by all students in a class, or titles assigned as curriculum reading.

**Subscription collection:** The school pays an annual subscription for access to a collection of titles (rather than individual titles). Access is available for all enrolled students during the subscription period. When the subscription ends, access ends. Common for general reading libraries from providers like Epic!, Oxford Reading Cloud, or similar platforms.

**Per-title perpetual ownership:** Rare in digital, but some publishers offer a digital purchase where the school owns the file permanently. Less common because publishers prefer subscription revenue.

### Access Control

For a school, "access control" means two things: ensuring that only enrolled students and staff can access the paid resources, and ensuring that grade-appropriate content is available to the right students (a Class 3 reader should not be accessing Class 11 reference texts, and vice versa).

Access control implementation options:

**Username/password per student:** Each student gets a login to the digital library platform. The school manages the user list. When a student leaves, the account is deactivated. Most institutions manage this through the ERP's student database, which feeds into the platform's user management.

**IP-based access:** The platform grants access to anyone connecting from the school's network IP address range. This works for on-campus access but not for home access, which limits the usefulness of the digital library for homework research.

**SSO (Single Sign-On):** Students log into the school's ERP and access the digital library within the same session, without a separate login. This is the best experience but requires technical integration between the ERP and the digital library platform.

**Grade-based content filtering:** Some platforms allow content to be organized by reading level or grade, with different student groups accessing different collections. This is configured during platform setup.

### Student Device Requirements

A digital library is only useful if students have devices to read on. The realistic device landscape in Indian schools:

**School-owned tablets or computer labs:** If the school has a computer lab or issued tablets, the digital library can be accessed from those devices. This means the digital library is only available during scheduled lab time or library periods, which limits independent access.

**Students' own smartphones:** Many students above Class 5 have access to a smartphone (parent's or their own). Most e-book platforms have mobile apps. Ensuring compatibility with older Android devices (Android 8 and above for most platforms) is important.

**Home computers:** Students accessing from home require either IP-based authentication (does not work at home) or username/password access. Schools enabling home access should verify the platform's bandwidth requirements.

**BYOD (Bring Your Own Device) programs:** Schools with BYOD policies have the best conditions for digital library adoption. Students bring their device, connect to school Wi-Fi, and access the digital library using the school's authentication.

For schools without widespread student device access, physical books supplemented with a limited set of digital resources for curriculum-specific research is the realistic starting point.

### Tracking Usage

A key advantage of digital resources over physical books is that usage data is available: which titles were accessed, by which users, for how long, and whether the student completed the book. Physical books provide no equivalent data.

Useful digital library usage metrics:

- **Titles accessed per student per month:** Is the digital library being used? A subscription to 500 titles with 10 accesses per month suggests the students do not know about the resource or cannot access it easily.
- **Most accessed titles:** Which titles are students choosing? This informs future purchasing decisions.
- **Access by time of day:** Students accessing during library periods suggests the teacher is directing them; access after school hours suggests independent use.
- **Non-completion rate:** Students who open a book and close it within 2 minutes are likely not finding it useful. This signals catalog issues (wrong level) or access friction (difficult to read on the device).

Usage data is only available if the platform provides it. Before subscribing, confirm what usage reporting the platform offers and whether it exports data or requires manual review within the platform's own dashboard.

### Common Mistakes

**Buying a subscription without a distribution plan:** Purchasing 500 e-books and not telling teachers or students is a common failure. Digital resources need the same promotion that new physical books receive.

**Choosing a platform incompatible with students' devices:** A platform that requires a high-end device or Windows app will not be used by students with budget Android phones.

**Ignoring the license expiry:** When a subscription lapses, access disappears immediately. Students in the middle of a research project lose access. Track renewal dates and budget accordingly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Which e-book platforms work best with school ERPs like Nexli?**
A: Platforms that support standard authentication protocols (OAuth, SAML, or simple CSV user import) can be linked to student databases. Common platforms used by Indian schools include Oxford Reading Cloud, Readonn, and Epic! (for primary). Evaluate based on your curriculum alignment and device requirements.

**Q: Can e-books replace physical books in a school library?**
A: For most Indian schools, digital and physical collections are complementary. Physical books are better for long-form reading (novels, textbooks). Digital resources work well for reference access, research, and supplementary reading.

**Q: What happens to students' reading progress if the subscription lapses?**
A: Most platforms do not preserve reading history after subscription ends. Before subscribing, check the platform's data export options so student reading records can be downloaded if you switch providers.

**Q: Do e-books count toward reading program targets?**
A: This depends on your reading program design. If the program tracks books read (not just physical books borrowed), then yes, e-books accessed and completed count toward the target. Nexli's reading program tracker can be configured to include digital titles.

**Q: Is there a government scheme for e-books in Indian schools?**
A: DIKSHA (Digital Infrastructure for Knowledge Sharing) provides free digital content aligned with NCERT curriculum for Classes 1-12. DIKSHA content is accessible via app or browser and does not require a subscription. Schools can direct students to DIKSHA alongside their paid digital library resources.
