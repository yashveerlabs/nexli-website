## How Do You Diagnose and Manage Technology Resistance in School Staff?

Technology resistance in school staff takes three distinct forms: fear-based resistance (afraid of making mistakes or being judged), training-gap resistance (genuinely doesn't know how to use the system), and usability resistance (the system is genuinely hard to use). Each type requires a different response. Applying the same intervention to all three wastes time and erodes trust. Diagnose the specific cause before designing a response.

## Why Treating All Resistance the Same Way Fails

A common mistake in school ERP implementations is applying the same response to all resistant users: more training. If a teacher is resistant because she is afraid of making mistakes, more training doesn't help. If a transport manager is resistant because the vehicle assignment screen requires 12 taps to complete a task that took 3 steps on paper, more training doesn't help, fixing the interface does.

Understanding what is actually causing the resistance is the most important step.

## Diagnostic Method: The Three-Question Conversation

Before labeling a staff member as "resistant," have a direct 10-minute conversation with the change champion or the staff member's line manager.

**Three diagnostic questions:**
1. "Can you show me how you would complete [specific task, e.g., marking attendance] right now?"
2. "What's the hardest part about this process?"
3. "What would make it easier for you?"

**Interpreting the answers:**

If the staff member can demonstrate the task but chooses not to in practice: fear-based or motivational resistance.

If the staff member attempts the task and makes errors or gets lost: training-gap resistance.

If the staff member completes the task correctly but takes significantly longer than the paper alternative, or expresses genuine frustration with specific steps: usability resistance.

## Type 1: Fear-Based Resistance

Fear-based resistance appears as avoidance. The staff member finds reasons not to use the system, delays entry "until later," or does the minimum required when observed but reverts to paper when unobserved.

**Root causes:**
- Fear of making a visible mistake (wrong mark entered, wrong fee amount processed, wrong attendance marked).
- Fear of being monitored and judged on previously invisible behaviors (how quickly they complete tasks, when in the day they finish mandatory processes).
- Fear of being exposed as less capable than colleagues who adopted more easily.

**Response strategy:**

Demonstrate error correction, not just task completion. In a private session, walk the resistant staff member through: "Watch. I'm going to deliberately enter the wrong mark for this test student. Now watch how I correct it." Seeing the correction is as important as seeing the task.

Create a private practice environment. Give resistant staff access to a sandbox environment with test data. Let them make all the mistakes they want without consequence before working on live data.

Explicitly state what is and is not being monitored. If the principal is reviewing attendance completion rates by class but not individual timestamp data, say so. Clarity reduces fear.

Pair with a non-judgmental buddy. Choose a peer champion who the resistant staff member trusts and who will not make them feel judged. Avoid pairing a resistant senior teacher with a highly tech-confident junior colleague; the power dynamic adds to the anxiety.

## Type 2: Training-Gap Resistance

Training-gap resistance appears as inability, not unwillingness. The staff member wants to use the system but doesn't know how. They often blame themselves ("I'm just bad with technology") and may have stopped trying after a confusing experience.

**Root causes:**
- The initial training session moved too fast.
- The session covered too much at once and specific daily workflows weren't reinforced.
- The staff member missed the training session and has had no catch-up support.
- The staff member's general technology experience is lower than the training assumed.

**Response strategy:**

Start from the beginning with this staff member, one-on-one. Don't refer to what was covered in the group training, just start with the task they need to do today.

Use their own data. Training is more effective when it uses the actual students in their class, the actual fees due, or the actual tasks from their daily workflow. Abstract examples don't stick; familiar names and numbers do.

Break the task down further than you think necessary. If attendance marking has 5 steps in your training, there might be a problem at step 1 (opening the browser, navigating to the URL). Go back to that step.

Set micro-goals. After this session, your goal is to complete attendance marking once, with me watching. That's it. After today, the goal is to do it independently for three consecutive days. Success builds confidence.

## Type 3: Usability Resistance

Usability resistance is genuine product feedback disguised as personal resistance. The staff member has tried the system, found specific workflows difficult or inefficient, and has stopped using it because the old method is faster.

**How to identify it:** Multiple staff members independently complain about the same feature or workflow. The resistant staff member can explain specifically what is harder about the ERP than the old method. The complaints are about a specific task, not the system in general.

**Response strategy:**

This is the most important type to identify correctly because the response is different: the ERP needs to improve, not the training.

Document the specific issue: which screen, which workflow, what specifically is harder. Reproduce the problem yourself. If you can confirm the issue, escalate to the vendor as a usability bug or improvement request.

Distinguish between "different" and "worse." Some resistance is "this is different from what I'm used to," not "this is actually harder." Test: can an objective observer complete the task faster in the ERP than in the old paper method? If yes, the issue is adjustment, not usability. If no, the issue is the product.

In the interim, acknowledge the problem to the resistant staff member: "You're right that this step is awkward. I've reported it to the vendor. In the meantime, here is the workaround: [specific alternative path]." Acknowledgment reduces frustration even when the fix takes time.

## Structuring a Resistance Response Program

For a school of 60 staff, a practical resistance management program looks like this:

**Week 2 after go-live:** The change champion and line managers identify staff who are not using the ERP as expected. No action yet, just observation.

**Week 3:** For each non-using staff member, one of the three diagnostic questions is asked in a natural conversation. Categorize each person: fear-based, training-gap, or usability.

**Weeks 4-6:** Targeted interventions based on category. Fear-based: private error-correction demonstration and sandbox practice. Training-gap: one-on-one session starting from scratch. Usability: document and report to vendor.

**Week 8:** Re-evaluate. Most fear-based and training-gap resistance should be resolving. Persistent non-use at week 8 requires formal management conversation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do we tell the difference between stubbornness and legitimate usability complaint?**
A: Test the complaint yourself. Complete the same task the resistant staff member says is too hard. If you find it genuinely slower or more complex than the old method, it's a usability issue. If you find it manageable after adjustment, it's likely an adjustment/fear issue.

**Q: Should resistance management be part of the change champion's role or the manager's role?**
A: Both. The change champion handles day-to-day support and training-gap interventions. The manager handles formal performance conversations when resistance persists beyond 6-8 weeks.

**Q: What if multiple staff members all resist the same feature?**
A: This is a signal, not a coincidence. Document the specific feature and the specific complaints and escalate to the vendor. Group resistance to a specific workflow indicates a product problem.

**Q: Is it ever right to let a resistant staff member continue using paper?**
A: Only during the pilot/parallel-running period (weeks 1-6). After that, allowing permanent exemptions creates inequity ("why do they get to use paper when I have to use the ERP?") and data integrity problems.

**Q: How do we handle a senior staff member with high status who is resistant?**
A: Senior staff resistance has an outsized cultural effect: if the head of accounts uses paper, other accounts staff feel the ERP isn't really mandatory. Engage the principal to have a direct conversation with the senior staff member. This is a situation where top-down authority is appropriate.
