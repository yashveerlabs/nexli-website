## What a School Intranet Is and Whether You Need One

A school intranet is an internal digital platform accessible only to school staff (and sometimes students) that serves as a central hub for documents, policies, announcements, and communication. Unlike the school's public website (which faces parents and the community) or the school ERP (which manages operations and data), an intranet is primarily for day-to-day staff information sharing.

Not every school needs a dedicated intranet. A small school with 30 staff members may manage internal communication perfectly well through a shared Google Drive and a WhatsApp group. A school with 150 staff members across multiple departments and campuses will find a shared drive unwieldy and WhatsApp too informal for policy documentation.

This guide helps schools decide whether they need an intranet, what to put in it, and what mistakes to avoid.

## What a School Intranet Typically Contains

**Document library and policy repository:** This is the highest-value use of a school intranet. Staff policies, procedures, templates, and forms should be in one searchable location rather than distributed across email threads. When the leave application form is updated, a single update to the intranet replaces the need to email the new version to all staff.

Examples of what belongs here:
- Staff handbook and code of conduct
- Leave application forms and procedures
- Emergency procedures and evacuation plans
- DPDP Act consent and data handling procedures
- Examination timetables and moderation procedures
- Procurement request forms

**Staff directory:** A searchable staff directory with names, departments, roles, extension numbers, and email addresses. Useful in larger schools where staff do not know everyone personally.

**Internal announcements and notices:** A noticeboard for internal communications: upcoming school events that require staff coordination, training session reminders, maintenance notices, and meeting schedules. This is different from the parent-facing communication channel.

**Committee and department workspaces:** Larger schools with academic departments, prefect committees, or working groups benefit from dedicated spaces where each group can maintain its own meeting notes, action items, and shared documents.

**Academic calendar and event schedule:** A shared calendar showing school events, exam dates, staff meeting schedules, and public holidays, visible to all staff.

## What to Avoid Putting on the Intranet

**Student personal data:** Student records, fee details, health information, and similar personal data should live in the school ERP with proper access controls, not in an intranet where access is typically less tightly controlled.

**Financial data:** Budget spreadsheets, salary details, and vendor contracts should not be on a broadly accessible intranet. These belong in the ERP or in a restricted shared drive with defined access.

**Operational decision-making:** If the intranet becomes the place where operational decisions happen (leave approvals, purchasing decisions, complaint resolution), it creates confusion between the intranet and the ERP workflow. Operational decisions should happen in the ERP.

## Common Reasons Intranets Fail

The most common fate of a school intranet is that it becomes a graveyard: documents are uploaded and never updated, staff stop visiting because the information is outdated, and it reverts to being decorative infrastructure.

To avoid this:

**Assign content owners.** Each section of the intranet should have a named person responsible for keeping it current. The HR officer owns the staff policy section. The academic coordinator owns the curriculum section.

**Schedule regular reviews.** Set a quarterly reminder for each content owner to review and update their section.

**Keep it searchable.** Staff will not browse an intranet. They will search for what they need. Good search functionality is essential.

**Ensure mobile access.** Staff increasingly use mobile phones for quick information lookups. An intranet that only works well on a desktop computer will be underused.

**Integrate with existing workflows.** If the intranet requires staff to go to a separate platform they don't already use, adoption will be low. Intranets hosted within an existing platform staff already use (Google Sites within Google Workspace, or SharePoint within Microsoft 365) have much higher adoption than standalone systems.

## Intranet Platform Options for Schools

**Google Sites:** Free with Google Workspace. Easy for non-technical staff to update. Pages embed Google Docs, Sheets, and Forms natively. Suitable for most K-12 schools already using Google Workspace.

**SharePoint:** Available within Microsoft 365. More powerful than Google Sites but requires more technical knowledge to configure well. Better suited to schools with an IT team.

**Notion:** A flexible document and knowledge base platform that some schools use for internal documentation. Works well for structured content but may feel unfamiliar to less technology-confident staff.

**Dedicated intranet software:** Products like Staffbase, LumApps, or Simpplr are purpose-built intranets with features like engagement tracking and automated onboarding. These are typically sized for larger organisations and may be more than a school needs.

For most Indian schools, Google Sites (if in the Google ecosystem) or SharePoint (if in the Microsoft ecosystem) is the practical choice, since staff already have accounts for those platforms.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does a small school need a formal intranet?**
A: Not necessarily. A school with fewer than 50 staff can often manage internal communication effectively with a shared Google Drive folder and a staff email group. An intranet adds value at scale, when finding documents and communicating across multiple departments becomes complex.

**Q: Can the school intranet be used to communicate with parents?**
A: No. An intranet is for internal staff use. Parent communication should go through the school's parent portal, email, or communication platform. Mixing internal and parent-facing content creates access control challenges.

**Q: What is the minimum content a school intranet should have at launch?**
A: Staff directory, staff handbook, leave application form, academic calendar, and a contact list for key departments. Launch with a small, useful set of content and expand it. An empty or content-poor intranet discourages adoption.

**Q: How do you enforce that staff use the intranet rather than emailing attachments?**
A: Stop sending policy documents via email. When staff ask for a form, direct them to the intranet link. After a few experiences of finding what they need there quickly, habit forms. Leadership visible use of the intranet also encourages adoption.

**Q: Who should maintain the school intranet?**
A: Maintenance should be distributed. Designate a content owner for each section. The IT coordinator or administrative manager oversees the overall platform. Central ownership by one person is a single point of failure.
