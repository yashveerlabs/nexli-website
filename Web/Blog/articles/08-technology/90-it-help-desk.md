## Running a School IT Help Desk: Structure, Priority, and Response

A school IT help desk is the organized system through which staff report technology problems and receive help. Without a structured help desk, problems are reported verbally, forgotten, or deprioritized based on who asked loudest rather than which issue matters most. A structured approach ensures no request is lost and that the most impactful issues get addressed first.

### Why Schools Need a Help Desk System

In a school with 80 staff members and 200 computing devices, the IT coordinator receives support requests constantly. Some come by phone, some by in-person interruption, some by email, some via WhatsApp. Without a system to capture and track all of these, some will inevitably fall through.

A help desk system:

Creates a record of every request, whether it was resolved, and how long it took.

Allows the IT coordinator to see the full queue, not just the most recent request.

Surfaces recurring problems. If three different teachers report problems with the same projector in the same week, the ticket history makes this visible.

Provides data for conversations with school leadership about resource needs. If the IT coordinator is closing 15 tickets per week but new tickets are being opened faster than they are being closed, that is evidence for the need for additional support capacity.

### Ticketing System Options

Schools do not need enterprise helpdesk software. The minimum viable approach:

**Shared email inbox:** An email address like it@schoolname.edu.in that all staff can send support requests to. The IT coordinator monitors this inbox, responds, and manually tracks resolution. Works for very small volumes (under 10 tickets per week) but has no automatic tracking.

**Google Forms plus Sheets:** A form where staff submit support requests, automatically captured in a spreadsheet. The IT coordinator manually updates status columns. Simple, free, and adequate for small schools.

**Free helpdesk tools:** Products like Freshdesk, Zoho Desk, and osTicket have free tiers that provide actual ticket tracking, status updates, and assignment. These are worth using for schools with volumes above 15-20 tickets per week.

**Paid helpdesk tools:** For schools with dedicated IT teams, commercial helpdesk tools with automation, SLA tracking, and reporting are worth the cost. They save more time in management than they cost.

The most important thing is not which tool is used, but that a system exists and that all requests go through it. A WhatsApp message to the IT coordinator is not a ticket. It is a conversation that may or may not be acted on.

### Priority Levels That Make Sense for Schools

The right priority scheme is based on the impact on school operations, not on who asks.

**Priority 1: Class or School-Wide Impact**

A teacher cannot deliver a lesson because a technology failure has made the classroom non-functional. A projector that does not display, a computer lab where all machines are offline, a WiFi failure affecting an entire building, or the school ERP being unavailable during attendance-taking time are all Priority 1 situations.

Target response: Acknowledgment within 15 minutes. IT coordinator physically present or remotely resolving within 1 hour. Alternative provided (another room, workaround for attendance) if resolution takes longer.

**Priority 2: Administrative Operation Impacted**

The accounts office cannot process fee payments because the payment system is down. The principal cannot access student records needed for a parent meeting in two hours. Critical print jobs failing before a morning assembly. These affect operations but do not directly stop classroom learning.

Target response: Acknowledgment within 30 minutes. Resolution within 4 hours or before the next school day if the issue arises in the afternoon.

**Priority 3: Individual Inconvenience**

A teacher's account password has expired and needs a reset. One computer in a 30-computer lab is running slowly. A printer in one office is not working but the adjacent office's printer is available.

Target response: Acknowledgment within 2 hours. Resolution within 2 business days.

**Priority 4: Improvement and Enhancement Requests**

Can we install a specific piece of software? Can this printer be moved to a different room? Can we add a new printer near the library?

Target response: Logged and assessed. Addressed in the next scheduled maintenance window (typically monthly).

### Building a Knowledge Base for Common Issues

A knowledge base is a collection of documented solutions to common problems. Its value: staff can solve their own common issues without waiting for the IT coordinator, and new IT coordinators can resolve issues faster without reinventing solutions from scratch.

**Start with the most frequent tickets.** Look at the last six months of tickets. The top five or ten most common issues are the knowledge base candidates.

**Document the solution, not just the problem.** "The projector is not displaying" is a problem. "Check that the HDMI cable is fully seated in both the laptop and the projector. If still not displaying, press Windows+P on the keyboard and select Extend or Duplicate. If the projector has a Source button, press it to cycle through inputs." That is a solution.

**Make it accessible.** A document in a Google Drive folder that most staff do not know about has limited value. Post the most common knowledge base articles on the school intranet or in the staffroom. QR codes that staff can scan to access the relevant knowledge base article directly from the classroom are useful.

**Keep it current.** When the solution to a problem changes (because software was updated or procedures changed), update the knowledge base article. Outdated instructions are worse than no instructions.

### SLA Reporting and Accountability

The help desk generates data. Use it. A monthly summary to the IT coordinator's manager should include:

Total tickets opened and closed in the period.

Average response time and resolution time by priority level.

Tickets that breached SLA (took longer than the target response or resolution time) and why.

Most common issue types.

Any trends: is a particular piece of equipment generating a disproportionate number of tickets? Is the same issue recurring because a permanent fix has not been implemented?

This reporting makes IT support visible as an organizational function, not just an informal service. It builds the case for resources when workload genuinely exceeds capacity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can a teacher mark a problem as Priority 1 themselves?**
A: Teachers should be able to indicate urgency when submitting a ticket, but the IT coordinator should confirm priority classification based on actual impact. Teachers naturally rate their own problems as high priority. The IT coordinator needs to assess whether a class is actually blocked or whether an alternative is available.

**Q: What should the IT coordinator do when two Priority 1 issues occur simultaneously?**
A: Triage based on scale of impact. A projector failure affecting one teacher in one class is a smaller impact than a WiFi failure affecting an entire building. Address the larger impact first and provide an immediate workaround (for example, moving the single affected class to the lab) while the smaller problem waits.

**Q: How should schools handle IT issues that arise outside support hours?**
A: Define out-of-hours support clearly in the IT support policy. For most schools, genuine Priority 1 issues outside of school hours (during evening events, parent meetings, board presentations) need a direct emergency contact for the IT coordinator with an understanding of what qualifies as a genuine emergency versus something that can wait until morning.

**Q: Should the knowledge base be accessible to parents?**
A: Parts of it can be. Articles explaining how to access the parent portal, how to reset a forgotten password, or how to view fee receipts are legitimately useful to parents. These can be published on the school website. Internal troubleshooting procedures (how to reconfigure the core switch) are not for external publication.

**Q: How do you measure whether the help desk is performing well?**
A: Key metrics: percentage of tickets resolved within SLA, first-contact resolution rate (issues resolved without escalation or revisiting), and ticket reopening rate (issues marked resolved but reported again within 48 hours). Staff satisfaction with IT support, gauged via a brief annual survey, is also a useful indicator.
