## How Should Schools Train Staff on a New ERP System?

ERP training for school staff is more than watching tutorial videos. Effective training is role-specific, practically focused on the five tasks each person does daily, and supported by printed reference materials for the first 60 days. Schools that run a single all-staff session get poor adoption. Schools that invest in structured role-based training see the ERP used consistently within four to six weeks.

## Why Generic Training Fails

Many schools book a full-day "ERP training" for all staff. The principal sits next to the librarian. The accountant is in the same session as the transport manager. The trainer covers every module because they don't know who needs what.

Three hours in, everyone is overwhelmed and remembers nothing specific to their job.

Generic training fails because adults learn by doing tasks relevant to their own work. A teacher who learns to mark attendance during training will remember it. The same teacher sitting through a payroll tutorial she will never use is wasting her time and building resentment toward the new system.

## The Role-Based Training Model

Design separate training sessions of 90-120 minutes for each staff group. Each session covers only the tasks that group performs daily.

**Teachers (90 minutes):**
- Marking daily attendance for each class period.
- Entering marks and grades after assessments.
- Viewing the timetable and checking class assignments.
- Accessing the homework/assignment module.
- Receiving and acknowledging school announcements.

**Office/Admin Staff (2 hours):**
- Creating new student admissions records.
- Generating fee invoices and recording payments (cash, cheque, UPI).
- Printing fee receipts and running daily collection reports.
- Managing parent contact updates.
- Running standard reports for principal review.

**Accounts Staff (2 hours):**
- Fee ledger management and reconciliation.
- Generating monthly collection reports and outstanding fee lists.
- Processing payroll runs.
- Expense recording and voucher management.
- Running audit-ready financial summaries.

**Transport and Hostel Managers (60 minutes):**
- Updating route and vehicle assignments.
- Viewing student transport lists by route.
- Managing hostel room assignments and roll calls.

## The Train-the-Trainer Approach

For schools with more than 30 staff, the train-the-trainer model works better than the vendor training everyone directly.

**How it works:**
1. Select one "change champion" per department (academic, finance, operations).
2. The ERP vendor trains the change champions intensively: 4-6 hours over 2-3 sessions, covering both tasks and troubleshooting.
3. The change champions then train their own departments using their own department's daily workflow as the example.
4. Change champions answer day-to-day questions, escalating only complex issues to the vendor.

This model has two advantages. First, training feels relevant because it uses the school's own data and processes as examples. Second, ongoing help is available from a colleague who sits 10 meters away, not from a vendor support queue.

## Quick Reference Cards

Print a one-page, laminated quick reference card for each role. Stick it to staff desks or workstations.

A quick reference card for a class teacher should have exactly five items:
1. How to log in (URL + first-time password reset instructions).
2. How to mark attendance (step 1, step 2, step 3 with screenshots).
3. How to enter marks after an exam.
4. What to do if a student's name is missing from the class list.
5. Who to call for help (change champion's name and extension).

Cards longer than one page do not get used. Keep them focused.

## Addressing Teacher Resistance

Some teachers, particularly those with 20+ years of service, will resist the ERP. The resistance is rarely about the technology itself; it is about fear of making mistakes publicly, extra work during a busy term, and distrust that the system will actually work.

**Strategies that work:**

Show time savings, not features. Teachers do not care about "cloud-based infrastructure." They care that marking 40 attendance records takes 3 minutes instead of 15 minutes with a paper register.

Start with willing teachers. Do not force resistant teachers in the first month. Let the willing early adopters demonstrate results publicly. Peer influence is more effective than management pressure.

Guarantee that mistakes are fixable. Many teachers are afraid of entering a wrong mark and having it sent to parents permanently. Clarify early: data can be corrected, approvals can be reversed, and nothing is sent to parents without a staff action.

Make training optional attendance but required competency. Teachers who miss training sessions still need to demonstrate they can mark attendance and enter grades before the first assessment date. This frames it as a skill requirement, not as punishment.

## Video Tutorials for Reference After Training

Short (2-5 minute) screen-recorded videos for the most common tasks work well as refreshers. Staff who forget how to generate a fee report at 4 PM on a Friday do not want to call support; they want to watch a 3-minute video.

Key videos to create or request from your vendor:
- How to mark attendance.
- How to enter marks for a class.
- How to generate a fee receipt.
- How to reset a forgotten password.
- How to view the timetable.

Store these videos somewhere accessible from the school intranet or a shared drive, not just the vendor's support portal.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does ERP training take for school staff?**
A: Per-role training of 90-120 minutes is enough for most staff to handle their daily tasks. Budget an additional 2-3 hours over the first week for questions and practice. Full competency usually develops within 3-4 weeks of daily use.

**Q: Should we train all staff before go-live or train as we go?**
A: Train role-specific groups 1-2 weeks before their module goes live. Training too far in advance means staff forget before they start using the system. Training too late means staff go live without preparation.

**Q: What do we do if a teacher refuses to use the ERP at all?**
A: First, diagnose the resistance. Is it lack of training, fear of judgment, or distrust of technology? Address the specific cause. If resistance continues after targeted support, escalate to management as a performance issue. An ERP that some staff use and others don't creates more confusion than no ERP at all.

**Q: Can non-English-speaking staff use an ERP?**
A: Most Indian school ERPs offer Hindi and regional language interfaces. Confirm language support during vendor evaluation if this applies to your support staff.

**Q: Who should we contact when something goes wrong after training?**
A: The internal change champion should be the first contact. For issues the champion cannot resolve within one working day, escalate to the vendor support. Establish a clear SLA with the vendor for response time.
