## How to Organize IT Support in a School

A school that invests in technology without investing in support is setting itself up for frustrated teachers, unused systems, and technology that gets blamed when the problem was actually inadequate maintenance. IT support is not a luxury for large schools. It is a basic requirement for any school where technology is part of daily operations.

### The IT Coordinator Role

The foundation of school IT support is a designated IT coordinator. In small schools, this may be a teacher with IT responsibility allocated as part of their duties. In medium and large schools, this should be a dedicated staff position.

The IT coordinator's responsibilities include:

**Day-to-day troubleshooting:** Being the first person a teacher calls when the projector in classroom 7 stops working, when a teacher cannot log in to the ERP, or when the printer in the office is offline.

**Device and network maintenance:** Regular checks on network switches, access points, computers, and AV equipment. Not waiting for things to break, but proactively replacing consumables, updating firmware, and identifying devices approaching end-of-life.

**Vendor coordination:** Being the point of contact for software vendors, internet service providers, hardware vendors, and any contracted IT service providers. Managing support tickets with vendors and escalating when response times exceed agreed SLAs.

**Staff training support:** Running brief refresher sessions when software updates change workflows, creating quick-reference guides for common tasks, and supporting staff who are less confident with technology.

**Inventory and asset management:** Keeping an up-to-date register of all devices, software licenses, warranty expiry dates, and service contracts.

In schools without a dedicated IT coordinator, the role often falls informally to whoever is most technically confident. This creates risk. When that person leaves, the informal knowledge leaves with them.

### Structuring the Help Desk

A help desk is a structured system for logging, tracking, and resolving support requests. In a school context, this does not require enterprise-grade software. A ticketing system can be as simple as a shared email inbox or a Google Form that creates a logged record of each request.

What matters is that requests are captured, assigned, tracked, and closed. When there is no logging system, the IT coordinator manages everything in their head, issues get forgotten, and recurring problems are not visible as patterns.

**Priority levels** are important for managing workload when multiple requests arrive simultaneously:

Priority 1 (Classroom impacted): A teacher cannot deliver a lesson because of a technical problem. A class of 30 students is sitting with nothing to do. This needs same-day resolution, within hours if possible.

Priority 2 (Operational process impacted): The fee collection software is down and the accounts team cannot process payments. Important, but students are not directly affected in the moment. Resolve within one business day.

Priority 3 (Individual inconvenience): A teacher's password reset, a single computer running slowly, a request to install a new piece of software. Resolve within two to three business days.

Priority 4 (Improvement request): New functionality requested, a suggestion to change a system configuration. Assess and address in the next scheduled maintenance window.

**Response time** and **resolution time** are different metrics. Response time is how quickly the IT coordinator acknowledges the request. Resolution time is how long it takes to fix the problem. A good help desk system tracks both and surfaces requests that have been open too long.

### Escalation Paths

Not all problems can be resolved by the school's IT coordinator. Some issues require vendor involvement. Some require physical hardware replacement. Some require a specialist.

A clear escalation path prevents problems from getting stuck. The IT coordinator should know:

Which issues to escalate to the ERP vendor versus the network hardware vendor versus the internet service provider.

What the vendor's support contact details are and what the SLA response times are.

What to do when a vendor is not responding within SLA, including escalation contacts within the vendor organization.

When to call in a third-party IT service company for on-site work the school's coordinator cannot do alone.

### Vendor SLAs

Service Level Agreements with technology vendors define what response and resolution times the vendor commits to for different types of issues. When evaluating ERP and infrastructure vendors, schools should review SLAs carefully.

Questions to ask:

What is the committed response time for a critical system outage (system down, users cannot access)? For school ERP systems, anything longer than four hours for critical issues is a concern.

What is the resolution time commitment for non-critical bugs?

What uptime percentage does the vendor guarantee, and what is the credit mechanism when uptime falls below that guarantee?

Is support available only during business hours, or 24/7? Schools that run boarding operations or after-hours activities need support availability beyond 9-to-5.

What is the vendor's process for planned maintenance (software updates)? Do they notify customers in advance? Do they schedule maintenance during low-usage periods?

### Self-Service Resources

Staff who can solve their own common problems reduce the demand on the IT coordinator and feel more confident with technology. A small library of self-service resources goes a long way:

A printed quick-reference card for the most common tasks (how to log in to the ERP, how to mark attendance, how to generate a report) posted in the staffroom.

A short video showing how to troubleshoot common problems (projector not displaying, printer offline, browser login issues) accessible on the school intranet.

An FAQ document for new staff covering the most common questions during the first month.

These resources do not replace support. They reduce the routine load so the IT coordinator's time is available for harder problems.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does a small school with 300 students need a dedicated IT coordinator?**
A: A full-time dedicated IT coordinator may not be justified for a 300-student school, but someone must have the responsibility. A teacher with IT responsibility allocated as a defined part of their role (and compensated accordingly) works. What does not work is having no designated owner and expecting problems to somehow get resolved.

**Q: What should a school ask about in a vendor SLA before signing?**
A: Ask specifically about critical outage response times, planned maintenance notification processes, uptime guarantees, support hours, escalation contacts for when first-level support fails, and the credit or remedy process if SLA commitments are not met. Get these in writing in the contract, not just as verbal assurances.

**Q: How should the school handle a vendor that consistently misses SLA commitments?**
A: Document every missed SLA with timestamps. Raise with the vendor's account manager first. If that does not produce improvement, escalate formally in writing citing the contract terms. Persistent SLA failures are grounds for contract termination in most commercial agreements. Schools should include this provision explicitly in contracts.

**Q: What is the right ratio of devices to IT support staff in a school?**
A: Industry benchmarks for corporate IT suggest roughly 1:100 (one support person per 100 devices) for environments without dedicated user-level support. Schools with more devices, less technically confident users, or more intensive device use may need more support capacity. A school with 200 devices and no dedicated IT person will have problems.

**Q: Should schools use ticketing software or can a shared email inbox work?**
A: A shared email inbox works for small volumes (under 20 tickets per week). As volume grows, a simple ticketing tool (many free or low-cost options exist) makes it easier to track open issues, spot recurring problems, and report on response times. The ticket history also helps new IT coordinators understand the school's common issues when they start.
