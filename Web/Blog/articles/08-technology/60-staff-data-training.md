## Training School Staff to Use Data: What Works and What Doesn't

School staff data training is the step that most ERP implementations skip or underinvest in. The software gets installed, staff get a login walkthrough, and then the expectation is set that "the system will take care of it." Six months later, the principal complains that reports are not being used, and the ERP vendor hears that the software is not helpful. The problem is usually not the software. It is that staff were trained to enter data but never trained to read it.

Data entry and data use are different skills. Teaching a teacher to mark attendance on an app takes 15 minutes. Teaching a class teacher to read her class's cumulative attendance report and identify the three students approaching the 75% boundary takes a different kind of training.

### The Gap Between Data Entry and Data Use

In most schools, data flows in one direction: staff enter it, administrators use it. Class teachers mark attendance; the principal reads the summary. The bursar posts payments; the accountant runs the collection report.

This structure has a critical weakness: the people closest to the data (class teachers who know why a student is absent, subject teachers who can explain a sudden drop in marks) are not in the habit of reading it. They generate the data and move on. The administrator who reads the summary is two steps removed from the context that makes the data meaningful.

Data literacy training changes this by building a habit among operational staff of reading the data they create, not just entering it. A class teacher who checks her section's cumulative attendance weekly will notice that one student's count has been declining for three weeks. She does not need to wait for the principal to flag it.

### What Basic Data Literacy Looks Like for Teachers

Basic data literacy for school teachers does not require statistics knowledge. It requires three things:

**Reading a report without misinterpreting it:**

The most common misinterpretation: confusing an average with a distribution. A class average of 78% in mathematics does not mean the class is doing well. If the distribution shows 12 students below 50%, those students are at risk regardless of what the average says. Training should include walking through real examples from the school's own data and pointing out what the number does and does not tell you.

**Knowing the difference between a data point and a trend:**

One absence is a data point. Five absences over four weeks is a trend. One low test score is a data point. Three declining test scores in the same subject is a trend. Training should explicitly cover how to look at date-ordered records rather than single snapshots.

**Knowing when to escalate:**

Data literacy is not about giving teachers decision authority they do not have. It is about training them to recognize when a data pattern warrants bringing to the counsellor, the academic coordinator, or the principal. The class teacher's job is to notice and report; the counsellor's job is to investigate and act.

### Practical Training Approaches

**Use real school data in training:**

Generic examples of "a student with declining attendance" are abstract. Training sessions that open the school's actual Nexli dashboard and walk through real report screens make the learning stick. Staff recognize the interface and relate to the data.

**Short regular practice beats one long session:**

A 3-hour data literacy workshop followed by nothing produces little change. A 20-minute monthly session where the academic coordinator reviews the at-risk report with class teachers builds the habit over a term.

**Assign specific reports to specific roles:**

Each role should have 2-3 reports they own. The class teacher owns the weekly attendance summary for her section. The subject teacher owns the marks distribution report for her subject after each assessment. Ownership creates accountability; accountability creates habit.

**Answer "so what?" for every report:**

During training, after showing any report, always ask: "What would you do if this number changed?" If the answer is unclear, the report needs either better design or clearer protocols. Staff who know what action a report is supposed to prompt will look at it more often than staff who see it as an information archive.

### Common Training Mistakes

**Training on features, not on decisions:**

Feature training ("here is how to filter by date") is necessary but insufficient. Decision training ("here is what to do when attendance drops below 80%") is what changes behaviour.

**Training only senior staff:**

If only the principal and accounts head are trained to read reports, the school is dependent on two people to monitor all data. Distributing basic data reading skills to class teachers, subject heads, and section coordinators multiplies the monitoring capacity.

**Not revisiting training after data quality improves:**

In the first term after an ERP implementation, data is incomplete because not all staff have consistently entered records. Reading reports from incomplete data is discouraging and teaches nothing. Plan a second training round at the end of Term 1, when data coverage is higher and patterns are starting to be meaningful.

**Q: Does Nexli provide training as part of onboarding?**

**Q: How long does it take for staff to become comfortable with data use?**
A: Most school teams reach consistent data-use habits within one full term (roughly 4 months) of regular practice. The first month is the steepest learning curve.

**Q: Can we train staff ourselves once the initial onboarding is done?**
A: Yes. Nexli provides documentation and video walkthroughs that school IT coordinators or ERP champions can use to run internal training sessions.

**Q: What if some teachers are resistant to using digital reports?**
A: The most effective approach is starting with the reports that directly help the resistant teacher: "This report shows you which students are approaching the attendance limit so you can call their parents before the principal asks you." Self-interest is a reliable motivator.

**Q: How do we measure whether the data training has worked?**
A: Track report access logs (Nexli shows which users accessed which reports and when), and measure whether at-risk interventions happen earlier in the term rather than only at the end of the term after exam results are in.
