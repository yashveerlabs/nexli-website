## Facial Recognition in Schools: A Technology That Demands Careful Scrutiny

Facial recognition in schools is an increasingly discussed technology in India. Vendors promise contactless attendance, enhanced security at school gates, and automated identification of students. The appeal is real: no cards to lose, no fingers to place on scanners, no proxy attendance.

The risks are equally real, and in many cases significantly outweigh the convenience benefits. This article explains how the technology works, what the DPDP Act 2023 requires, why regulators and privacy advocates are concerned specifically about deploying it for children, and what schools should consider before any implementation.

This technology is not included in Nexli. This article is provided to help school administrators make informed decisions about a technology they may encounter in the market.

## How Facial Recognition Works

A facial recognition system processes video or still images from a camera to identify or verify an individual. The process involves:

1. **Detection:** The system detects that a human face is present in the camera frame.
2. **Feature extraction:** The system maps facial features (distance between eyes, shape of the jawline, nose geometry) and converts them into a numerical template called a faceprint.
3. **Matching:** The faceprint is compared against a database of enrolled faceprints. If a match above a confidence threshold is found, the person is identified.
4. **Action:** The system logs the identification event (time, person, location) and triggers any configured action (opening a gate, recording attendance, sending an alert).

The key technical challenges are accuracy under varying conditions (different lighting, angles, partial occlusion, ageing) and the fundamental fact that faceprints, once extracted, cannot be changed if the data is compromised.

## Accuracy and Algorithmic Bias

Facial recognition accuracy is not uniform across demographics. Academic studies and audits by organisations including MIT Media Lab have documented higher error rates for:

- Darker skin tones compared to lighter skin tones.
- Female faces compared to male faces.
- Younger faces (children and adolescents).

In a school context, this means a system that performs well for adult males may have significantly higher false rejection rates for female students or students with darker complexions. A student who is repeatedly failed by the scanner at the school gate experiences both friction and a form of algorithmic discrimination.

When a school's system returns a false positive (incorrectly matching a student to the wrong identity), the attendance record is corrupted, which may have downstream consequences for the student.

## The DPDP Act 2023 and Biometric Data of Minors

Facial biometric data (faceprints) is a category of sensitive personal data under Indian privacy law. The DPDP Act 2023 and earlier SPDI Rules impose heightened obligations on organisations that collect this data.

For schools specifically, the combination of biometric data and minors creates the most stringent requirements:

**Consent for minors:** The DPDP Act requires verifiable parental consent for processing personal data of children (under 18). Biometric data, as a sensitive category, requires clear, specific, and informed consent. A generic enrollment form acknowledging terms and conditions does not constitute adequate consent for facial biometric collection.

**Data fiduciary responsibilities:** Schools collecting facial biometric data are Data Fiduciaries under the Act with obligations to implement appropriate security measures, retain data only as long as necessary, and delete it when the purpose is fulfilled or the student departs.

**Purpose limitation and data minimisation:** Facial biometric data collected for attendance cannot be used for other purposes (marketing, tracking, sharing with third parties) without fresh specific consent. Schools must collect only the data necessary for the stated purpose.

**Right to erasure:** Under the DPDP Act, individuals have the right to withdraw consent and request deletion of their personal data. For a student who withdraws consent for facial recognition, the school must be able to delete their faceprint from all systems, including the recognition device, the central database, and any backups.

## Why Privacy Advocates Are Specifically Concerned About Children

The concerns around facial recognition for minors are more significant than for adults:

**Children cannot fully comprehend the implications.** A 10-year-old enrolled in a facial recognition system at school cannot meaningfully consent to or understand what it means to have their biometric identity captured by a surveillance system.

**Faceprints follow a child indefinitely.** A student's faceprint captured at age 8 remains a valid biometric identifier for that person throughout their life. If the database is compromised, the child cannot change their face as they would change a compromised password.

**Normalisation of surveillance.** Children educated in environments with pervasive biometric monitoring may develop a reduced expectation of privacy that affects their relationship with surveillance technology throughout their lives.

**Mission creep risk.** A system installed for attendance may be expanded to track student movement patterns, identify students breaking rules, or share data with government databases. Each of these extensions requires fresh legal basis and consent.

## International and Indian Regulatory Context

Several countries have moved to restrict or prohibit facial recognition of students in schools. Sweden's data protection authority fined a school for using facial recognition for attendance in 2019, finding that consent was not freely given when provided as a condition of school attendance. UK's Information Commissioner's Office has provided guidance that facial recognition for student attendance is disproportionate given less privacy-intrusive alternatives.

In India, the Personal Data Protection Bill's discussion documents and DPDP Act guidance emphasise the heightened protection required for children's data. While no specific school facial recognition regulation has been issued, the framework clearly requires that processing of children's sensitive personal data be proportionate, consented, and for a legitimate purpose.

The Ministry of Education and NCERT have not issued endorsements or standards for facial recognition in schools. Schools implementing this technology are doing so without regulatory approval or clear national guidance.

## Less Privacy-Invasive Alternatives

Schools motivated by the genuine problems that facial recognition is presented as solving (proxy attendance, slow gate entry, unauthorised visitor access) can address these through less intrusive means:

- **RFID cards:** Fast, contactless, no biometric data required.
- **Teacher-marked digital attendance on a tablet:** Requires 3 minutes per class, no biometric collection, no privacy issues.
- **Visitor management with OTP verification:** Verifies visitor identity without collecting facial biometric data.
- **Controlled gate entry with security staff:** A trained gate guard recognising faces informally is not a biometric surveillance system and does not collect or process personal data in the regulated sense.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is facial recognition attendance legal for schools in India?**
A: There is no specific law prohibiting facial recognition in schools, but the DPDP Act 2023 imposes significant requirements: verifiable parental consent for minors' biometric data, strict purpose limitation, security requirements, and deletion rights. Meeting all of these requirements for a school population is technically and administratively demanding. Legal review before implementation is strongly advisable.

**Q: Does facial recognition work accurately for Indian school children?**
A: Accuracy depends on the system and the conditions. Research shows that many facial recognition systems have higher error rates for younger people and darker skin tones. Schools considering this technology should request independent accuracy data from vendors for comparable demographic populations, not just vendor-provided claims.

**Q: Can the school use facial recognition without parental consent?**
A: No. The DPDP Act 2023 requires verifiable parental consent for processing personal data, including biometric data, of students under 18. Making facial recognition a condition of school entry or attendance without valid consent is not legally permissible.

**Q: What happens if the school's facial recognition database is hacked?**
A: Faceprints of hundreds or thousands of students would be exposed. Unlike a password, a faceprint cannot be changed. The affected students carry that compromised biometric identity permanently. This is the fundamental reason biometric data requires significantly stronger protection than other personal data.

**Q: Is Nexli planning to add facial recognition?**
A: No. Nexli does not include and is not planning to add facial recognition functionality. Attendance is managed through manual entry, with optional support for RFID and fingerprint biometric integrations for schools that choose to implement them with appropriate consent and legal compliance.
