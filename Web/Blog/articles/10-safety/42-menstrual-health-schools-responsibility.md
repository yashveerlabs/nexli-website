# Menstrual Health: School's Responsibility to Students

Menstruation is a natural biological process, yet shame and lack of infrastructure turn it into an obstacle to girls' education. One in three Indian girls miss school during their periods due to lack of facilities, inadequate knowledge, or social stigma. Schools can transform menstrual health into a matter-of-fact wellness issue. This guide outlines your school's role.

## Why Menstrual Health Matters

**The Attendance Impact**
UNESCO reports that 1 in 10 African girls misses school during menstruation; in parts of India, rates are even higher. Period absences compound into learning gaps. When menstruation becomes normalized, attendance improves and academic outcomes follow.

**Health & Hygiene**
Poor menstrual hygiene practices increase UTIs, fungal infections, and reproductive health complications. Many students lack access to pads, reusable cloth, or proper disposal methods. School-provided facilities bridge this access gap.

**Policy Directives**
- **Menstrual Hygiene Scheme (MHMS):** Government of India provides free sanitary pads to students in government schools
- **CBSE Curriculum:** Health education (Classes VI-VIII) includes menstrual health topics
- **NEP 2020:** Emphasizes health and wellness as foundational learning outcomes
- **Period Leave Policy:** Some states allow leave; schools must create alternative—not exclusionary—policies

**Dignity & Destigmatization**
Period shame is learned. When schools normalize menstruation through education and facilities, girls develop confidence and boys develop respect.

## Creating a Menstrual-Health-Friendly School

**Physical Infrastructure**

1. **Sanitary Napkin Dispensers**
   - Place in girls' toilets (main building, hostels, sports area)
   - Stock with good-quality, absorbent pads
   - Easy access: students shouldn't feel embarrassed requesting
   - Ensure dispensers are discreet but accessible

2. **Disposal Facilities**
   - Separate, labeled bins in each girls' toilet
   - Incinerators (ideal) or sealed waste bins
   - Regular emptying (at least daily)
   - Hygiene standards per Biomedical Waste Management Rules, 2016
   - Educate students on proper disposal

3. **Washing Facilities**
   - Adequate water supply (often lacking in schools)
   - Soap for handwashing and body cleaning
   - Privacy for washing (curtained areas if traditional bathing)
   - Adequate toilet stalls for emergency needs

4. **Rest/Medical Area**
   - Private space in sick bay for menstrual discomfort
   - Heating pad or hot water access
   - Comfortable seating
   - Trained nurse available for consultation

**Policy Framework**

**Menstrual Leave Policy (If Allowed in Your State)**

Some states permit "period leave." If your school allows this:
- Frame as optional, not compulsory (respects student autonomy)
- Don't mark as "absence"—mark as "medically managed absence" or excused
- Provide option to attend school with accommodations (preferential seating, bathroom access)
- Avoid stigmatizing—don't announce publicly why student is absent
- Never exclude from exams, sports, or special events

Better approach: **Zero-barrier attendance**
- Provide all facilities/resources so students can attend comfortably
- Discourage leave, promote attendance
- Offer accommodations (rest breaks, bathroom access) without requiring disclosure

**Toilet Access Protocol**
- Allow bathroom access without permission during class (discreet signal system)
- No questions asked or required explanation
- Acceptable for all students (periods, anxiety, medical needs)

**Sports & PE Participation**
- All students can participate during menstruation
- Modify activities if student requests (water comfort, tampon/pad reliability concerns)
- Provide alternative activities only if student initiates request
- Never exclude based on period assumptions

**Uniform & Dress Code**
- Accommodate preferences during menstruation (dark pants allowed)
- Respect student dignity without forcing disclosure

## Menstrual Health Education Program

**Curriculum Integration**

**Grade V (Age 10-11):** Introduction
- What's menstruation? (Simple biology)
- Why does it happen? (Normal body process)
- Common myths vs. facts
- Basic hygiene practices

**Grade VI (Age 11-12):** Detailed Curriculum
- Menstrual cycle explanation (days, phases, duration variation)
- Puberty changes in boys and girls
- Emotional & physical changes during menstruation
- Hygiene: pad types, changing frequency, disposal
- Health: diet, exercise, pain management
- Addressing myths and social stigma

**Grade VII-VIII (Age 12-14):** Advanced Topics
- Menstrual disorders: heavy flow, irregular cycles, severe cramps
- When to see a doctor
- Reproductive health introduction
- Addressing gender stereotypes
- Supporting peers (boys' role in destigmatization)

**Teaching Approach**
- Gender-inclusive: boys learn too (understanding female health builds empathy)
- Evidence-based: use government health materials (Ministry of Health, NFHS data)
- Normalize language: use correct anatomical terms, not euphemisms
- Involve health professionals: school nurse or doctor conducts sessions
- Address myths: common misconceptions (menstruating women are "impure," can't exercise, etc.)

## Menstrual Product Supply & Management

**Sourcing Pads**

**Government Schemes**
- **Menstrual Hygiene Scheme (MHMS):** Free pads for government school girls
- Cost: ₹6-8 per pad subsidized by state
- Annual allocation: approximately ₹150-300 per girl per year

**School Procurement**
- Private schools: partner with pad manufacturers for bulk discounts
- Budget: ₹200-400 per girl annually
- Prefer eco-friendly brands (biodegradable pads) to reduce waste
- Offer choice: sanitary pads and/or reusable cloth pads

**Inventory Management**
- Track stock, expiry dates (pads do expire—adhesive weakens)
- Refill dispensers weekly or as-needed
- Maintain buffer supply (don't run out)
- Collect usage data (helps with budgeting)

**Alternative Products**
- **Reusable cloth pads:** Washable, economical (₹50-100 set), sustainable
- **Menstrual cups:** Durable (5 years), eco-friendly, but requires comfort/education
- **Tampons:** Available if school prefers (some girls prefer for sports/swimming)

## Real Example: Normalizing Menstruation Changes Engagement

**The Scenario**
Lakshmi School (girls' school, Grades VI-VIII, 400 students) observed declining attendance during months with higher typically-menstruating students. Teachers noted girls increasingly absent 1-2 days per cycle.

**The Intervention**
- Installed dispensers in every toilet
- Launched menstrual health curriculum (Grade VI)
- Trained teachers to normalize bathroom access
- Invited health worker for Q&A session (destigmatization)
- Communicated with parents: period is normal, school supports attendance

**The Outcome**
After 6 months: attendance during "expected menstrual days" remained stable. Girls reported feeling less embarrassed and more confident. Parents appreciated school's proactive support. Teachers noted improved focus and participation year-round.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should we teach boys about menstruation?**
Yes. Educated boys become empathetic partners, supportive friends, and respectful colleagues. Gender-inclusive education combats myths and builds solidarity.

**Is offering period leave discriminatory?**
Yes, potentially. While compassionate, period leave can reinforce stigma and limit girls' opportunities. Better: offer all students accommodations they need and let them choose attendance.

**What's the cheapest sustainable solution?**
Reusable cloth pads (₹100-300 per set) last 2-3 years. Cost per period: ₹2-5 (vs. ₹10-20 for disposable pads). Train students in washing/drying. Most sustainable and budget-friendly.

**Can schools require pads in hostels?**
Yes. Boarding schools should provide pads in hostel dispensers since girls can't easily access external purchases. Hostel fees should cover this essential health cost.

**What if a student is uncomfortable discussing periods?**
Respect privacy. Provide written materials (leaflets, QR codes linking to videos). Normalize discreet access: students can take pads without discussing. School nurse available privately if needed.

**How do we address period poverty (girls unable to afford pads)?**
This is school's responsibility. Government schemes cover most; schools should supplement for underprivileged students. Free pads for all from school funds = attendance guarantee.

**What about non-menstruating students (transgender boys)?**
Create inclusive policies: "menstrual products available for all who need them." Avoid gendering products; offer discreet access to anyone requesting.
