## How to Handle Medical Emergencies in School

**Medical emergencies (choking, allergic reaction, seizure, fainting, chest pain) require trained, calm response. This guide covers staff training, first aid protocols, emergency communication, and recovery procedures.**

---

## Essential Training & Certification

**All Staff Should Know:**
- How to call emergency services (911/local number)
- How to recognize common emergencies (fainting, choking, severe allergic reaction)
- When NOT to move an injured person (spinal injury risk)
- How to position an unconscious person (recovery position)

**Designated First Aiders (minimum 3 staff):**
- First aid certification (2-day course, renewed every 2 years)
- CPR certification (cardiac arrest response)
- AED training (defibrillator use)

**School Nurse (if budget allows):**
- RN or trained nursing assistant
- Available during school hours (24/7 for boarding schools)
- Medical clinic stocked with medications and supplies

## Common Medical Emergencies & Response

### Choking
- Heimlich maneuver (if trained)
- Call emergency services if unsuccessful
- Do NOT attempt if unsure (let professionals handle)

### Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis)
- Use EpiPen immediately (auto-injector)
- Call emergency services
- Lie student flat, elevate legs (unless vomiting)
- Wait for ambulance

### Seizure
- Do NOT restrain
- Clear area around student (remove hard objects)
- Cushion head with pillow
- Call emergency services
- Monitor breathing; if stops, CPR if trained

### Fainting
- Lie student flat, elevate legs
- Loosen tight clothing
- Check breathing
- Usually recovers in 1–2 minutes
- If doesn't recover: Call emergency services

### Severe Bleeding
- Apply direct pressure with clean cloth
- Elevate wound (if safe)
- Do NOT remove embedded objects
- Call emergency services if heavy bleeding doesn't stop in 5 minutes

### Chest Pain / Difficulty Breathing
- Sit student upright
- Call emergency services immediately (could be heart attack or severe asthma)
- Administer asthma inhaler if available and student has one prescribed

## Emergency Response Procedure

**Immediate (0–2 minutes):**
1. Identify emergency (loss of consciousness? difficulty breathing? severe pain?)
2. Call emergency services (911 or local ambulance)
3. Alert first aider / school nurse to come immediately
4. If first aider trained, start emergency response (CPR, Heimlich, etc.)

**2–30 minutes:**
- Stabilize patient (first aid)
- Alert Principal and parents
- Prepare for ambulance arrival
- Document: time, location, symptoms, response

**At Hospital:**
- Accompany student (Principal or designated staff)
- Contact parents (update on status, location, next steps)
- File incident report

**Follow-up:**
- Call parents daily (recovery status, visiting hours)
- File formal incident report within 24 hours
- Medical review: could this emergency have been prevented?

## Facility & Equipment Readiness

**First Aid Kit:**
- Bandages, gauze, antiseptic
- Pain reliever (aspirin, paracetamol)
- Antihistamine (allergic reaction)
- Thermometer
- Blood pressure monitor
- Scissors, tweezers

**Emergency Medications:**
- EpiPen (anaphylaxis)
- Asthma inhalers (if students with asthma enrolled)
- Blood pressure medication (if student prescribed)
- All stored in locked clinic cabinet, accessible to nurse only

**AED (Automated External Defibrillator):**
- Installed and functional
- Located in visible place
- Staff trained on use
- Battery/pads checked monthly

**Emergency Contact Information:**
- Accessible quickly (card in teacher's desk, digital in system)
- Parent/guardian emergency number for every student
- Student allergies and medical conditions listed

## FAQ

**Q1: Should we have a school nurse?**  
A: Highly recommended. At minimum, first-aid trained staff on-site during all school hours.

**Q2: What's the cost of first aid training?**  
A: ₹1,500–3,000 per person for 2-day course. Budget ₹10K–15K annually for staff training/recertification.

**Q3: Should parents know about medical emergencies?**  
A: Yes. Call immediately if serious (hospitalization, severe injury). For minor incidents (cut, headache), inform at end of day.

**Q4: What if we can't afford an AED?**  
A: Priority: first aid kits and staff training. AED is valuable but secondary to trained responders.

**Q5: Should we practice medical emergency drills?**  
A: Yes, annually. Simulate cardiac arrest, severe allergic reaction, etc. Staff practice response.
