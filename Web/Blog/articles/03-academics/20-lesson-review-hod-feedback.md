## Lesson Plan Review: How HODs Provide Meaningful Feedback

HODs (Heads of Department) review teachers' lesson plans to ensure quality and alignment to curriculum. Good feedback improves teaching. Poor feedback wastes time or demoralizes teachers. Effective feedback is specific, actionable, and growth-focused.

### Best Practices for HOD Lesson Review

**1. Establish Clear Criteria**
Before reviewing, HODs clarify: What constitutes a good lesson plan?
- Clear, measurable learning objectives
- Activities aligned to objectives
- Assessment methods included
- Differentiation for different learners
- Connection to curriculum standards

**2. Use a Standardized Rubric**
Rubric categories:
- Alignment (Does it align to curriculum?)
- Learning Objectives (Are they clear and measurable?)
- Instructional Methods (Are activities engaging and effective?)
- Assessment (Is there formative assessment?)
- Differentiation (Does it address diverse learners?)

Rate each: Exemplary / Proficient / Developing / Beginning

**3. Provide Specific, Actionable Feedback**
Not: "Good lesson plan"
Yes: "Your learning objective is clear. Consider adding a formative check-in after 15 minutes to catch misconceptions early. Also, your homework assumes all students grasp the concept; consider tiered homework for struggling learners."

**4. Balance Strengths and Growth Areas**
Start with: What's working well? (celebrate)
Then: What's one area to improve? (not everything at once)
Close with: How can I support you? (collaborative tone)

**5. Provide Professional Development**
If patterns emerge (e.g., all teachers weak on differentiation):
- Conduct department-wide training
- Share exemplar lesson plans
- Peer observations (see how excellent teacher differentiates)

**6. Track Progress**
Keep records:
- When did teacher receive feedback?
- What was the feedback?
- Did next lesson plan show improvement?
- Track: Is this teacher growing?

## FAQ

**Q: How often should HODs review lesson plans?**  
A: Weekly for new teachers. Monthly for experienced teachers. At minimum, before major units.

**Q: What if a teacher resists feedback?**  
A: Frame as partnership: "Help me understand your thinking. Here's what I'm seeing. How can we work together?" Resistance often fades with collaborative approach.

**Q: Should HOD feedback be part of teacher evaluation?**  
A: Partially. Feedback is coaching. Formal evaluation is separate. But evidence of growth from feedback supports positive evaluations.
