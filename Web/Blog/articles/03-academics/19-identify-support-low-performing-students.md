## How to Identify and Support Low-Performing Students Early

Early intervention is the most effective approach to supporting struggling students. Identifying problems in October (not December) gives months to intervene before year-end exams. Without systematic identification, struggling students fall further behind.

### The Problem

- **Late identification:** Problems discovered at report cards (too late to help much)
- **No root cause analysis:** "This student has low marks" without understanding why
- **Inconsistent support:** Support depends on whether teacher notices; some slip through
- **Widening gaps:** Student falls behind in one subject; soon behind in all subjects

### Solutions

**1. Use Multiple Data Points**
Don't rely on marks alone:
- Attendance patterns
- Homework completion
- Class participation
- Formative assessment results
- Behavioral changes

**2. Establish Benchmarks**
Define when a student needs support:
- "Below 60% on unit test"
- "Absent >2 days/week for 2 weeks"
- "Homework completion <80%"

**3. Assign Interventions**
Based on root cause:
- Low understanding → tutoring, reteaching
- Absent → investigate barriers, plan attendance support
- Low motivation → counselor involvement, engagement strategy
- Family issues → counselor, principal coordination

**4. Monitor Progress**
Weekly check-ins (not just end-of-term review):
- Is intervention helping?
- Adjust if not working
- Celebrate progress

**5. Communicate with Parents**
Early notice, not surprises:
- "Your child is showing early signs of struggling in Math. Here's our support plan"
- Engage family in solutions

## FAQ

**Q: How do we avoid stigmatizing students in intervention?**  
A: Frame it positively: "Extra support sessions" not "remedial." Include students proactively selected based on data (not shaming). Success-focused framing.

**Q: What if students don't respond to intervention?**  
A: Adapt: Try different interventions, involve counselor, involve family. Some students need specialized support (learning disabilities assessment).

**Q: When should a student be considered for repeating the grade?**  
A: After substantial intervention hasn't worked (6+ months of support) and objective data shows the student isn't ready for next grade. Rare decision, made with parents and principal.
