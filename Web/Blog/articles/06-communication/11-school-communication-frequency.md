## The Communication Paradox

Schools face a tension: parents want to know what's happening with their children, but they don't want to be overwhelmed with messages. Send too many updates and parents ignore them all (alert fatigue). Send too few and parents feel uninformed. The sweet spot is different for each school, depending on parent preferences, school priorities, and communication channels used.

The key is intentional, structured communication, not random bursts of messages.

## The Consequences of Imbalanced Communication Frequency

### Over-Communication
- **Alert Fatigue:** Parents stop reading emails/SMS when they receive 5+ per week.
- **Unsubscribes:** Parents opt out of email lists because they feel spammed.
- **Ignored Announcements:** Important messages (exam schedule, fee deadline) get lost in the noise.
- **Parent Frustration:** "Why is the school messaging me about minor things?"
- **Staff Time:** Composing and sending frequent messages consumes staff time.

### Under-Communication
- **Uninformed Parents:** Parents don't know when exams are, what homework is due, or how their child is performing.
- **Parental Anxiety:** "Why isn't the school telling me anything? Is something wrong?"
- **Missed Deadlines:** Fee payment, form submission, event registration deadlines pass without parent awareness.
- **Reduced Engagement:** Parents who are uninformed can't help their children effectively.
- **Compliance Risk:** Schools can't prove they informed parents of critical information.

## Recommended Communication Frequency by Category

### Weekly Communications (Regular)

**One School-Wide Newsletter:**
- Sent on a fixed day (e.g., every Friday at 3 PM).
- Contains top-priority school updates: exam schedule, upcoming events, policy changes, student achievements.
- Via email and parent portal.
- Typical length: 300-400 words, scannable with bullet points.

**Class-Specific Updates (Optional):**
- Class teacher sends 1 update per week (can be combined with the school newsletter).
- Content: Curriculum progress, homework summary, classroom news, behavior highlights.
- Via email or app notification (not WhatsApp, which feels too informal).
- Typical length: 150-200 words.

**Total weekly emails: 1-2**

### As-Needed / Transactional Communications

**SMS/WhatsApp for Urgent Alerts:**
- Attendance alerts: "Your child was absent today."
- Fee reminders: "Fee due today. Pay now in the parent portal."
- Event reminders: "PTM tomorrow. Your slot is 4:00-4:10 PM."
- Emergency closures: "School closed today due to weather."
- Frequency: 1-2 SMS per week on average.

**Email for Important Announcements:**
- Exam schedule released: Sent immediately when published.
- Holiday calendar: Sent at the start of each term.
- Policy changes: Sent with notice and explanation.
- Permission slips: Sent 1 week before the field trip.
- Frequency: 2-4 emails per month.

**Parent Portal for Reference:**
- All announcements archived in the parent portal (searchable).
- Parent portal is the "source of truth" that doesn't clutter the inbox.
- Frequency: Updated in real-time as needed.

### Periodic / Scheduled Communications

**Academic Report:**
- Mid-term progress (informal): Email/SMS with grades, feedback.
- Report card: Published after exams.
- Frequency: 1-2 times per term.

**Parent Meetings:**
- PTM: At least twice per year (after each major term).
- Parent forums / feedback sessions: Twice per year.
- Frequency: Varies, but scheduled in advance.

**Annual Compliance Communications:**
- CBSE bylaws summary: Once at year start.
- Fee structure: Once at year start.
- Data privacy/consent: Once annually.
- Frequency: Varies by policy.

## How to Determine the Right Frequency for Your School

### 1. **Survey Parents**

Ask parents directly:
- "How many emails/SMS should the school send per week?"
- "Which communication channels do you prefer?"
- "What information is most important to you?"
- "How do you prefer to be contacted about different topics?"

Example survey question: "Your child's school sends [frequency] communications per week. Is this too much, too little, or just right?"

### 2. **Track Parent Engagement**

- Email open rate: If emails have <20% open rate, you're sending too frequently or at the wrong time.
- SMS read rate: Most SMS are read within 3 minutes; if parents are opting out, you're sending too many.
- Parent portal logins: If logins drop, parents may be overwhelmed or information isn't valuable.
- Parent complaint tracking: "Stop sending so many emails" = too frequent.

### 3. **Start Conservative, Then Adjust**

Begin with minimal communication:
- 1 school-wide email per week
- SMS only for urgent items (1-2 per week)
- Everything else in the parent portal

Then, survey parents and add communication channels only if parents specifically request them.

### 4. **Match Frequency to Channel**

- **Email:** 1-2 per week is ideal (more feels like spam).
- **SMS:** 2-5 per week (SMS is less intrusive; parents expect transactional messages).
- **WhatsApp:** 2-3 per week (informal; more frequent is acceptable if it's high-value info).
- **App Notifications:** 1-2 per week (push notifications feel intrusive).
- **Parent Portal:** No limit (parents choose to check it).

### 5. **Be Consistent**

Whatever frequency you choose, stick to it. Parents stop checking email if messages arrive randomly; they start checking if messages arrive reliably (same day/time each week).

**Good:** "You'll get a school newsletter every Friday at 3 PM and attendance alerts the same day by 5 PM."

**Bad:** "You'll get emails whenever something comes up."

## Special Cases: When to Increase Frequency

### During Crisis
- Increase to SMS + email + phone call.
- Update every 30 minutes until crisis is resolved.
- Then return to normal frequency.

### During Exam Season
- More frequent updates about exam schedule, admit card details, results.
- Temporary increase is acceptable if parents understand it's time-bound.

### For High-Need Students
- Students with behavior or academic concerns may require more frequent communication.
- But communicate this expectation to the parent in advance ("We'll check in weekly about Arav's progress").

## FAQ

**Q1: Is one email per week enough?**
A: For most schools, yes. One well-written, substantive email per week (with links to more detailed info on the parent portal) is ideal. Add SMS for transactional alerts (attendance, fees, urgent events).

**Q2: Can parents adjust their own notification preferences?**
A: Ideally yes. Nexli and modern portals let parents choose: "I want SMS for urgent alerts but no weekly emails." This reduces over-communication for some parents while keeping others informed.

**Q3: What if parents request more frequent communication?**
A: They likely want more specific information (e.g., weekly homework summary, grade updates). Instead of sending more emails, improve the parent portal so they can check information anytime.

**Q4: Should weekends/evenings have no school communication?**
A: For routine messages, yes. But urgent alerts (emergency closure, accident) can go out anytime. Set expectations: "School communicates Monday-Friday, 7 AM-6 PM. Emergency alerts may be sent anytime."

**Q5: How do schools handle parents who want daily updates?**
A: Offer daily access via the parent portal instead of emails. Parents can check their child's attendance, homework, and grades daily without bombarding all parents with daily emails.

**Q6: Is it better to combine messages (one email with multiple topics) or send separate emails?**
A: Combine. One well-structured email with clear sections is better than 3 separate emails. It respects parents' inbox and reduces alert fatigue.
