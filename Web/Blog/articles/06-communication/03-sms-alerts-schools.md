## Why SMS Remains Critical for School Communication

In a world of apps and email, SMS is often overlooked. Yet SMS remains the most reliable channel for reaching parents, especially in India. SMS doesn't require smartphones, internet connectivity, or app installations. A simple text message reaches parents who lack email access, are offline, or have outdated phones. For urgent, time-sensitive information, SMS has the highest read rate: over 98% of SMS messages are read within 3 minutes.

Schools that integrate SMS into their communication strategy ensure critical information reaches parents reliably.

## When to Use SMS vs. Email vs. WhatsApp

Not every message deserves an SMS. Each channel has a purpose:

**Use SMS for:**
- Urgent alerts (school closure, emergency, accident)
- Time-sensitive reminders (exam tomorrow, fee due today)
- Attendance alerts (child absent today)
- One-time verification codes (for portal login)

**Use Email for:**
- Detailed information (policy updates, admission requirements)
- Documents and attachments (fee receipt, report card)
- Non-urgent announcements (holiday schedule, event details)

**Use WhatsApp for:**
- Class-level announcements (exam schedule, homework)
- Medium urgency (assignment due next week)
- Casual updates (event photos, thank-you notes)

**Use Parent Portal for:**
- Source of truth (grades, attendance, fees)
- Long-form information (detailed fee structure, school handbook)
- Compliance records (consent, feedback, documentation)

## SMS Compliance & Legal Requirements

### DPDP Act 2023

- **Consent:** Parents must explicitly opt in to SMS communication. Sending unsolicited SMS violates DPDP.
- **Purpose:** Clearly communicate why you're collecting their phone number and what types of SMS they'll receive.
- **Opt-Out:** Every SMS should include an opt-out mechanism (reply STOP or unsubscribe via portal).
- **Audit Trail:** Log every SMS sent, recipient, content, and delivery status.

### Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (TRAI) Rules

- **Registered Sender ID:** Schools should use a registered sender ID (e.g., "SCHOOL") not random numbers.
- **National Do-Not-Call Registry:** Respect parents' preferences if they've opted into the Do-Not-Call list for promotional messages (though transactional SMS like fee reminders are exempt).
- **Message Classification:** TRAI distinguishes between transactional (fee reminders, alerts) and promotional (event invitations) SMS. Transactional messages can be sent anytime; promotional messages are restricted.

### POCSO Act 2012

- **Child Safety:** Messages should never solicit responses from children without guardian consent.
- **Content:** SMS should be professional and appropriate; no personal messages to individual students.
- **Recording:** Maintain records of who sent what SMS when, for compliance if issues arise.

## Consequences of Poor SMS Strategy

**Opt-In Violations:** Sending SMS without consent violates DPDP Act. Regulators can impose penalties; parents can file complaints.

**Alert Fatigue:** Too many SMS messages (multiple reminders per day) cause parents to ignore them. Critical alerts get lost in the noise.

**Missed Information:** If SMS is unreliable (wrong phone numbers, provider issues), critical information doesn't reach parents, and disputes arise.

**Cost Overruns:** Sending SMS indiscriminately wastes school budget. A planned strategy reduces volume and cost.

**Privacy Breaches:** Sharing sensitive information via SMS (student grades, health data) violates DPDP. SMS is less secure than a parent portal.

## SMS Best Practices for Schools

### 1. **Build an Opt-In List Carefully**

- Include SMS phone number during admission or parent registration.
- Include a checkbox for SMS consent with a clear label: "I agree to receive transactional SMS alerts from the school (fees, attendance, emergencies)."
- Maintain documentation of who opted in when.
- Allow parents to opt out via the parent portal anytime.

### 2. **Use a Registered Sender ID**

- Register your school's sender ID with the SMS provider (e.g., "SCHOOL_A" or your school's abbreviation).
- Never send SMS from random numbers; parents won't trust them.
- Include the sender ID in every message so parents know it's from the school.

### 3. **Keep SMS Concise**

- SMS is limited to ~160 characters (one message). Keep it short.
- Essential info only: what happened, when, what to do next.
- Example: "Attendance Alert: Aarav absent today. Inform school if unwell. 📞 9876543210"
- Avoid: Multi-message chains; link to parent portal or website instead.

### 4. **Send SMS at the Right Time**

- Morning alerts (7-9 AM): Attendance (child absent), urgent school updates.
- Afternoon alerts (3-5 PM): Fee reminders, event notifications, pickup time changes.
- Avoid: Late evening, night, weekends (unless it's a true emergency).

### 5. **Automate Transactional SMS**

- **Attendance:** Auto-send when a student is marked absent.
- **Fees:** Auto-send on the due date and 3 days before.
- **Events:** Auto-send a reminder the day before (exam, PTM, field trip).
- **Assignment:** Auto-send when homework/assignment is overdue.
- **School Closure:** Auto-send when holidays are declared or school is closed unexpectedly.

### 6. **Use SMS for Verification, Not for Selling**

- One-time passwords (OTP) for parent portal login.
- Event registration links (brief, trackable).
- Avoid: Promotional offers, upselling, or selling school merchandise via SMS.

### 7. **Maintain an SMS Log**

- Date, time, sender, recipient (phone number masked), message content, delivery status.
- Use this log to prove compliance if regulators ask questions.
- Purge old logs after 1-2 years per data retention policies.

### 8. **Respect Preferences**

- Parents should be able to choose which types of SMS they receive (critical only, or all alerts).
- Maintain and honor the opt-out list.
- If a parent opts out, don't send promotional SMS, but you can still send critical safety alerts (define "critical" clearly).

## What NOT to Send via SMS

- **Student grades or exam results** → Parent portal only.
- **Medical or health information** → Parent portal or secure portal only.
- **Promotional content unrelated to school** → Violates DPDP and TRAI rules.
- **Personal messages to individual students** → Use parent portal or call instead.
- **Spam or repetitive reminders** → Causes opt-outs and erodes trust.
- **Financial details** → Fee amount OK; bank account numbers NOT OK.
- **Screenshots or images** → Use parent portal or email instead.

## FAQ

**Q1: How often should schools send SMS to parents?**
A: Depends on the school, but most schools send 2-5 SMS per month (attendance alerts, fee reminders, event reminders). Avoid more than one SMS per day on average; parents tune out if overwhelmed.

**Q2: Can schools send SMS without parental consent?**
A: No. DPDP Act requires explicit opt-in. Critical emergencies (school closure, accident) may be an exception, but even then, schools should have a clear policy documenting the emergency criteria.

**Q3: What's the best SMS service provider for schools?**
A: Look for providers that offer registered sender IDs, compliance with TRAI rules, and audit trails. Many work through school ERP platforms like Nexli, which handle integration and logging.

**Q4: How should schools handle SMS delivery failures?**
A: Wrong phone number, network issues, or carrier blocks cause delivery failures. Maintain a fallback list (email, parent portal, alternative number) and resend via email if SMS fails. Nexli logs failures so you can investigate.

**Q5: Can students see SMS sent to parents?**
A: Depends on the phone. If parents use shared family devices, students may see alerts. Schools should not include sensitive academic or health info in SMS to avoid unintended audience exposure.

**Q6: How long should schools keep SMS logs?**
A: Maintain SMS records for at least 2 years for compliance documentation. After that, you can archive or delete per your data retention policy (align with DPDP Act guidelines).
