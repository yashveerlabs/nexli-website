## The Gap Between What Schools Send and What Parents Want

Schools communicate a lot, but often not what parents want to hear. Schools send:
- "Your child must be on time."
- "Fee is due on [date]."
- "Exam will be held on [date]."

Parents want to hear:
- "Your child is making progress and here's specific evidence."
- "Here's how we support children with fees challenges."
- "Here's what your child should study to prepare for the exam."

The mismatch is striking. Schools focus on logistics and compliance; parents focus on their child's growth and well-being. When communication doesn't match expectations, parents tune out or feel unsupported.

## What Parents Want to Hear (Prioritized)

### 1. **Progress & Achievement (Top Priority)**

Parents want frequent, specific updates on their child's academic and personal growth.

**What to say:**
- "Arav scored 78% in Math this week. He's improved 5 points since last month."
- "Arav showed confidence speaking up in class this week."
- "Arav is struggling with division. Here's what we're doing and how you can help at home."
- "Arav made a friend and participated in group activity."

**What NOT to say:**
- "Arav is doing OK." (Vague; not useful.)
- "Arav needs to work harder." (Accusatory; no specific insight.)
- Comparisons to other students. (Inappropriate and demotivating.)

**Frequency:** Weekly or bi-weekly for struggling students; monthly for average students. High-achieving students also like progress updates, not just "You're doing great."

### 2. **Safety & Well-Being (Critical)**

Parents need to know their child is safe physically and emotionally.

**What to say:**
- "Your child eats well and plays safely at school."
- "Your child seems happy and engaged."
- "We noticed your child's mood has been low lately. Can we talk?"
- "Your child was involved in a conflict; here's what happened and how we resolved it."

**What NOT to say:**
- "School is safe." (Vague reassurance.)
- Delayed reporting of incidents. (Parents find out from their child or social media first.)

**Frequency:** Proactively share positive observations weekly. Report concerns immediately.

### 3. **Specific Behavior (Not Generalizations)**

When sharing concerns about behavior, parents want specifics, not vague complaints.

**What to say:**
- "On Monday, Arav spoke to the teacher without raising his hand. When asked to wait his turn, he responded with disrespect. We asked Arav to reflect on his tone."
- "Arav has been late to class 3 times this week. Is there a transportation issue we can help with?"

**What NOT to say:**
- "Arav is disruptive." (What does "disruptive" mean? When? How bad?)
- "Arav needs to be more respectful." (To whom? When?)
- "Arav is lazy." (Insulting and unhelpful.)

**Frequency:** Daily observation for serious issues; weekly for minor concerns.

### 4. **How to Support Learning at Home**

Parents want actionable suggestions for how they can help.

**What to say:**
- "Arav struggles with reading comprehension. At home, please read a short story together and ask him to retell it. This helps him practice summarization."
- "Arav is strong in Math and interested in robotics. You might explore coding apps or STEM camps in the summer."
- "Arav needs more sleep. Homework after dinner plus 9 hours of sleep will improve concentration in class."

**What NOT to say:**
- "Your child is struggling; you need to help more." (Blame without guidance.)
- Unsolicited advice about parenting. (Not the school's role.)

**Frequency:** For academic support, weekly or when new topics are introduced.

### 5. **Curriculum & Learning Goals**

Parents want transparency about what's being taught and why.

**What to say:**
- "This month, we're learning about fractions. We'll practice with real-world examples (cutting pizza, sharing). Your child can help in the kitchen!"
- "We're reading 'Charlotte's Web' to understand themes of friendship and loss. We'd love to hear if your child has favorite books."
- "We're studying water conservation. Your child will present a project. Can you help them research?"

**What NOT to say:**
- "We're covering the curriculum as planned." (Parents don't know what that means.)
- Unsolicited criticism of parents' educational choices. (e.g., "You let your child watch too much TV.")

**Frequency:** Once per term or when significant new topics begin.

### 6. **Appreciation & Recognition**

Parents love hearing that their child is noticed and valued.

**What to say:**
- "Arav helped a classmate with homework without being asked. That shows kindness."
- "Arav's artwork in the display looks beautiful. He put a lot of effort into it."
- "Arav was elected class monitor. We're proud of his leadership."

**What NOT to say:**
- Comparisons: "Arav is not as talented as Rohan." (Demoralizing.)
- Sarcasm: "Finally, Arav turned in homework on time!" (Defensive tone.)

**Frequency:** At least once per month per child.

### 7. **Challenges & Solutions (Not Blame)**

When there's a problem, parents want to be partners in solving it, not blamed.

**What to say:**
- "We're noticing Arav hasn't submitted homework twice this week. Rather than punishment, let's understand what's happening. Is the homework too hard? Too much? Is there a home situation affecting him?"
- "We'd like to recommend an assessment for attention. Here's why. Here's what we can do at school. What support can you offer at home?"

**What NOT to say:**
- "Your child is the problem." (Accusatory.)
- Punishment without trying to understand. ("Arav will lose 10% marks for late homework.")

**Frequency:** When issues arise, address them immediately and collaboratively.

### 8. **Practical Logistics (But Not Overwhelming)**

Parents do need logistical information, but it should be clear and timely.

**What to say:**
- "Exams are Oct 15-25. Exam schedule and admit cards will be sent by Oct 10."
- "Permission slip for field trip is due Oct 5. Submit via parent portal or print version."
- "Fee is due on Oct 15. You can pay online, by check, or cash."

**What NOT to say:**
- Vague deadlines: "Fees due soon." (When? How much?)
- Last-minute notices: "Field trip is tomorrow; permission slip due EOD today."

**Frequency:** Once per event; repeated reminders 1 week and 3 days before.

## Communication Content Checklist

Before sending any message to parents, ask:

- [ ] **Is it specific?** Or is it vague (e.g., "doing well" with no details)?
- [ ] **Is it actionable?** Or does it leave parents confused about what to do?
- [ ] **Is it timely?** Or am I telling them about something that happened weeks ago?
- [ ] **Is it balanced?** Or am I only complaining / only praising?
- [ ] **Is it respectful?** Or am I blaming the parent?
- [ ] **Is it necessary?** Or is it just noise?
- [ ] **Is it about the child?** Or am I discussing school policy that doesn't affect them?

If you answer "no" to any of these, revise before sending.

## FAQ

**Q1: Should schools share all concerns with parents or only serious issues?**
A: Share all concerns early and collaboratively. Minor concerns (small behavior lapse, single missed homework) become serious if ignored. Early, positive communication ("Arav struggled with this; here's how we're helping") prevents problems from escalating.

**Q2: How should schools balance positive and negative feedback?**
A: Aim for a 3:1 ratio of positive to negative. Even when sharing concerns, frame them as solvable: "Here's a concern. Here's what we're doing. Here's how you can help." This feels collaborative, not accusatory.

**Q3: Can schools send progress updates without grades?**
A: Absolutely. For young children (K-3), narrative feedback is often better than numbers. For older children, combine grades with context: "78% in Math. He's strong in multiplication but needs practice with division."

**Q4: What if parents give conflicting feedback (one wants daily updates, another finds them overwhelming)?**
A: Use a preference system. Let each parent choose their own communication frequency and channels. Nexli can support this.

**Q5: Should schools share information about other students?**
A: Never. Each parent should receive information only about their child. Don't compare: "Your child is ahead of Rohan" or "Most students got this; your child didn't." Focus on individual progress.

**Q6: How should schools respond if parents disagree with their assessment?**
A: Listen. "I understand you see it differently. Here's the evidence we have. Can we meet and discuss?" Parent input is valuable; sometimes there's context (medical issue, family stress) that explains performance.
