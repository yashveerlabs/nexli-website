A document retention policy specifies which records a school must keep, for how long, in what format, and when they can be destroyed. Schools that keep everything forever accumulate unnecessary storage burden. Schools that destroy records prematurely face regulatory non-compliance, inability to respond to legal claims, and inability to produce records when required by authorities.

## Legal Basis for Retention Requirements

Multiple laws specify minimum record retention periods:
- **Income Tax Act:** Financial records for 6 years from the end of the relevant assessment year
- **Companies Act (if applicable):** 8 years for some records
- **Trust/Society law:** Permanent retention for foundational documents (trust deed, registration, annual returns)
- **CBSE:** Board exam result records must be retained; specific periods vary by record type
- **DPDP Act:** Personal data not retained longer than necessary; consent records retained while relationship exists
- **Limitation Act 1963:** A 3-year general limitation period for civil claims; retaining records for longer than 3 years protects against late-filed claims

## Key Document Categories and Retention Periods

### Student Academic Records
- Admission applications, certificates submitted: Retain for the duration of enrollment + 5 years minimum
- Academic transcripts, report cards: Permanent retention (students may need records decades later)
- Attendance registers: 5 years (for CBSE classes; longer recommended)
- Board exam registration and results: Permanent
- Transfer certificates: 5 years minimum

### Financial Records
- Fee receipts, fee collection registers: 6-7 years
- Vouchers, invoices, payment records: 6-7 years
- Annual accounts and audit reports: 10 years (longer recommended)
- TDS records and certificates: 6 years from assessment year
- EPF/ESI contribution records: 5 years

### Staff Records
- Employment letters, service records: Duration of employment + 7 years
- Police verification certificates: Duration of employment + 3 years
- Qualification certificates: Duration of employment + 3 years (or permanently, they are originals)
- Leave records: 5 years
- Training records (POCSO, POSH, data protection): 5 years after last training

### Governance Records
- Trust deed, registration: Permanent
- Board meeting minutes: Permanent
- Annual returns filed with Registrar: 10 years minimum (copies)
- CBSE affiliation documents: Duration of affiliation + 5 years

### Compliance Documents
- Fire safety NOC, building safety certificate (expired): 5 years (for reference if inspection queries arise)
- UDISE+ data submitted: 5 years
- POSH ICC case files: 3-5 years after case closure
- POCSO complaint records: 5 years minimum after case closure
- DPDP consent records: Duration of student enrollment + 3 years

## Document Destruction

Records that have passed their retention period can be securely destroyed:
- Paper records: Shredded (not placed in general waste)
- Electronic records: Securely deleted (not just moved to recycle bin)
- Maintain a destruction log: what was destroyed, when, by whom, and the retention period that expired

**Do not destroy records that are subject to a pending regulatory inquiry, legal claim, or audit.** Put a legal hold on all records related to any pending matter.

## Digital vs. Physical Records

For records required to be maintained in original form (founding documents, certain legal certificates): physical originals must be kept.

For operational records, digital copies with proper backup are generally acceptable. Ensure:
- Backups are maintained and tested
- Records are stored in a format that remains accessible over time
- Access is controlled so records cannot be altered retroactively

## FAQ

**Q: Can a school store student records only digitally and destroy the paper originals?**

A: For most operational records (attendance registers, fee receipts), digital copies are acceptable if properly backed up and access-controlled. Originals of certain documents (trust deed, land documents, certificates issued by authorities) must be retained in physical form.

**Q: What if a student requests their academic records 20 years after leaving the school?**

A: Academic transcripts and board exam results should be retained permanently for this reason. Alumni seeking proof of education must be able to obtain it. CBSE maintains some records centrally, but the school's copies provide backup.

**Q: Does the DPDP Act require schools to delete student data after the student leaves?**

A: DPDP Act requires that personal data not be retained longer than necessary. Schools have legitimate reasons to retain some data (alumni records, legal claims, regulatory requirements). A retention policy that defines how long each category is retained, and deletes data after that period, satisfies the DPDP requirement.

**Q: Must the document retention policy be approved by the board?**

A: Yes. As a governance policy with legal and financial implications, the retention policy should be board-approved. It should be reviewed annually and updated when regulatory requirements change.
