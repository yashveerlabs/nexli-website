The RTE admission process is government-managed: parents apply to the district authority, a lottery allocates students to schools, and schools must accept the result. Understanding each step prevents compliance failures.

## Phase 1: Government Notification (January-February)

The state education department publishes EWS seat availability for all schools in the district. Parents receive notice to apply. Schools must have already declared their Class I intake to the district authority by this point.

## Phase 2: Parent Applications (February-March)

EWS-eligible parents submit applications to the district office with income proof, address proof, caste certificate if applicable, and child's birth certificate. Schools have no role in this phase.

## Phase 3: Lottery and Allocation (March-April)

The district authority conducts a computerized lottery. Results are published online and at district offices. Parents check allocations; schools receive a list of students allocated to them.

## Phase 4: School Admission (April-May)

Schools must accept all allocated students without additional screening, issue admission letters within 7 days, collect only documents needed for enrollment, and complete zero-fee enrollment with no charges of any kind.

## Phase 5: Ongoing Compliance

Maintain the EWS register updated monthly. Do not generate fee invoices for EWS students. Provide books, uniform, and stationery at school expense. File the annual compliance report with the district authority.

## FAQ

**Q: Can schools add their own admission criteria after lottery allocation?**

A: No. Once the district allocates a student, admission is mandatory. Any school-level criteria applied post-lottery is an RTE violation.

**Q: What if allocated students do not show up?**

A: Follow up with the district authority. Report no-shows. Do not fill seats with fee-paying students without district permission.

**Q: Can parents choose which school they prefer in the lottery?**

A: States vary, some allow preference ranking, others are purely random. Schools have no control over this process.
