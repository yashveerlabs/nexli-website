Serving as a trustee of a school trust is not an honorary role. Trustees have legal obligations that carry personal liability if breached. Many school trustees who take on the role as a mark of community standing do not fully understand what they have signed up for.

## Who Is a Trustee

A trustee is a person who holds and manages the trust's property and operations for the benefit of the trust's beneficiaries (in a school: students and the community). Trustees are appointed under the trust deed and are responsible for the school's governance.

Equivalent roles in different structures:
- Trust: Trustees (also called Managing Trustees or Board of Trustees)
- Society: Members of the Managing Committee
- Company: Directors of the Company

## Core Legal Duties

### 1. Duty to Act in the Best Interests of the Trust

Trustees must make decisions that benefit the school's beneficiaries (students), not themselves or their associates. Personal relationships, business interests, and community politics must not influence school decisions.

### 2. Duty to Act Within the Trust Deed

The trust deed is the foundational document. Trustees cannot take actions that contradict the trust deed. If the trust deed specifies the school is for a particular community or educational level, trustees cannot unilaterally change this.

### 3. Duty to Act Collectively

Individual trustees have no independent authority. Authority is vested in the board acting collectively. A trustee cannot bind the school to agreements or make decisions alone.

### 4. Duty to Maintain Confidentiality

Trustees receive confidential information about students (compliance incidents, special needs), staff (performance issues, disciplinary matters), and the school's finances. This information must not be disclosed outside the board.

### 5. Duty to Be Informed

The duty of care requires trustees to be informed. Trustees who approve decisions without reading the relevant information cannot claim they didn't know. They must attend meetings, read papers, and ask questions.

## Conflicts of Interest

A conflict of interest arises when a trustee's personal interests (financial, professional, familial) could influence a board decision. Common examples:
- A trustee's family business is proposed as a vendor to the school
- A trustee's family member is a candidate for the Principal position
- A trustee has a financial interest in property the school is considering purchasing

**What trustees must do:**
1. Disclose the conflict before the discussion begins
2. Abstain from the vote on the matter
3. Leave the room during discussion if appropriate (depends on severity of conflict)
4. Have the conflict and the process documented in the minutes

## Consequences of Trustee Failure

**Breach of fiduciary duty:** Trustees who breach their duties can be:
- Personally required to restore losses to the trust
- Removed from the board by the Charity Commissioner
- Subject to court proceedings
- Prohibited from serving as trustees in future

**Regulatory consequences:** The Charity Commissioner can investigate and take action against trustees for mismanagement, financial irregularities, or failure to follow the trust deed.

## FAQ

**Q: Can a trustee be paid by the school?**

A: Trust law generally prohibits payment of trustees from trust funds unless the trust deed explicitly authorizes it. Reimbursement of actual expenses (travel to meetings) is acceptable; payment for services requires careful legal structuring. Unauthorized payment to trustees is a breach of trust.

**Q: What is the trustee's liability for debt if the school is in financial difficulty?**

A: In a trust structure, trustees are personally liable for debts incurred through unauthorized actions. Properly authorized debts (within the board's authority) are typically the trust's liability, not personal liability. Legal advice is essential in financial distress situations.

**Q: Can a trustee resign mid-term?**

A: Yes. A trustee can resign by notice to the board. Resignation must follow the process in the trust deed and must not leave the trust with fewer trustees than the required quorum. The trust deed specifies how vacancies are filled.

**Q: What happens when a trustee's term expires?**

A: The trustee steps down (or is re-appointed if eligible). The process for term extensions and re-appointments should be in the trust deed. Holding office beyond an expired term without proper re-appointment creates governance irregularity.
