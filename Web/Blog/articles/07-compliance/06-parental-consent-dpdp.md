## What DPDP Says About Consent

Under the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, schools cannot collect personal data without the data principal's (parent/guardian's) consent. Consent must be:

1. **Explicit** (not implied or blanket).
2. **Informed** (parent knows what data is being collected and why).
3. **Specific** (tied to particular data types and purposes, not generic).
4. **Freely Given** (no coercion; refusal should not result in penalties).
5. **Documented** (proof of consent must be retained).
6. **Revocable** (parents can withdraw consent anytime).

Most schools think signing an "admission form" = blanket consent. It doesn't. Schools must obtain specific, documented consent for each data type or use case.

## Types of Consents Schools Need

### Consent 1: General Admission & Academic Purposes

Purpose: Collect data for admission, enrollment, and academic tracking.

**Data Included:**
- Name, DOB, address, phone number
- Academic records (attendance, marks, progress)
- Contact information

**Text:**
"I consent to the collection and use of my child's personal data for admission, enrollment, and academic tracking purposes. This includes name, date of birth, address, contact numbers, attendance, and academic performance. This data will be stored securely and used only for educational purposes."

**Retention:** Duration of enrollment + 2 years.

### Consent 2: Medical & Health Data

Purpose: Collect medical information for student safety and health emergencies.

**Data Included:**
- Medical allergies
- Chronic conditions (asthma, diabetes)
- Medications
- Vaccination status
- Medical emergency contact

**Text:**
"I consent to the collection of my child's medical and health information, including allergies, chronic conditions, medications, and vaccination records. This information will be used solely for health emergencies and ensuring a safe school environment. Only the school nurse, principal, and relevant staff will have access."

**Retention:** Duration of enrollment + 1 year.

### Consent 3: Photographs & Videos

Purpose: Use student photos/videos for school identification, records, or publications.

**Data Included:**
- Photographs for ID card
- Photographs for annual magazine
- Videos of school events
- Group photos for school website

**Text:**
"I consent to the collection and use of my child's photograph/video for the following purposes (check all that apply):
- [ ] School ID card
- [ ] Annual school magazine and publications
- [ ] School website and social media
- [ ] School events and internal documentation

I understand that photographs without consent will not be used for public display."

**Retention:** As needed for the specific purpose (ID card: current year; magazine: permanent).

### Consent 4: Biometric Data (Attendance, etc.)

Purpose: Use fingerprints or facial recognition for attendance or campus access.

**Data Included:**
- Fingerprint or facial biometric data
- Iris or voice recognition (if used)

**Text:**
"I consent to the collection of my child's biometric data (fingerprint, facial recognition, or iris scan) for attendance tracking and campus security purposes. This biometric data will be encrypted and stored securely. I understand that my child can use an alternative method (manual attendance, ID card) if I prefer not to provide biometric consent."

**Retention:** Duration of enrollment + 30 days after exit.

### Consent 5: Communication & Digital Platforms

Purpose: Use contact information for school communication via email, SMS, WhatsApp, apps.

**Data Included:**
- Email address
- Phone number
- WhatsApp contact
- Social media handles (if monitored)

**Text:**
"I consent to the school using my contact information (email, phone, WhatsApp) for communication regarding my child's academics, events, and administrative matters. I understand that I can withdraw this consent for any specific channel anytime."

**Retention:** As long as the contact relationship exists.

### Consent 6: Third-Party Sharing

Purpose: Share student data with external parties (government agencies, educational partners, vendors).

**Data Included:**
- Student data shared with government (UDISE+, CBSE, RTE authority)
- Data shared with education vendors or assessment companies
- Data shared with background verification agencies

**Text:**
"I consent to the school sharing my child's personal data with authorized government agencies (for reporting and scholarships), educational partners, and verified vendors only when necessary for legitimate educational purposes. Student privacy will be maintained, and data will not be sold or used for marketing."

**Retention:** As required for specific purposes.

### Consent 7: Data Deletion & Retention

Purpose: Clarify what happens to data after a student leaves.

**Text:**
"I understand that my child's personal data will be retained for [specific period] after enrollment ends, for record-keeping, verification, and dispute resolution purposes. After this period, data will be securely deleted or anonymized, unless required by law to be retained longer."

## How to Collect Consent (Paper vs. Digital)

### Paper Consent (Printed Form)

**When to use:** For parents uncomfortable with digital, or as a backup.

**Best practices:**
- Use clear, simple language.
- Checkbox format (not long paragraphs).
- Space for parent signature and date.
- Keep signed form for 2+ years.
- Difficult to track withdrawals or updates.

### Digital Consent (Via Parent Portal)

**When to use:** Preferred method; transparent, trackable, updatable.

**Best practices:**
- Use parent portal or OTP-based consent form.
- Digital signature (tick box + phone OTP verification).
- Timestamped automatically.
- Parent can view consent history and withdraw anytime.
- Easy audit trail for compliance.
- Parent can re-consent if consent expires.

### Hybrid Approach (Recommended)

- Collect digital consent via parent portal during admission.
- Print a copy for parent records.
- Email confirmation to parent for their records.
- Maintain digital records in a compliance system (like Nexli).

## Managing Consent Withdrawal

Parents have the right to withdraw consent anytime.

**School's Obligations:**
1. Allow withdrawal (don't create barriers).
2. Honor withdrawal within 7 days.
3. Stop using the data for that purpose immediately.
4. Don't penalize the family for withdrawal.

**Example:**
- Parent withdraws consent for biometric data.
- School immediately stops collecting fingerprints.
- School switches to manual attendance or ID-card based attendance.
- No negative consequences for the student.

## Consent Withdrawal Process

**How parents initiate withdrawal:**
- Email: "I withdraw consent for [data type]."
- School portal: Click "Withdraw Consent" for a specific data type.
- In-person: Signed request to office.

**School's response:**
- Acknowledge receipt within 3 days.
- Confirm withdrawal within 7 days.
- Provide alternative (e.g., "We'll track attendance manually instead of biometric").
- Document the withdrawal in the consent registry.

## Consent Renewal & Expiry

DPDP Act doesn't mandate regular re-consent, but good practice suggests:

**Annual Renewal:**
- At the start of each academic year, parents review consent status.
- If anything changed (new data types, new purposes), request updated consent.
- If everything is the same, send a confirmation email: "Your consent is still valid."

**Triggered Re-Consent:**
- Consent expires if a parent hasn't engaged with the school for 2+ years.
- School changes in data usage (new system, new vendor, new purpose).
- School needs consent for a new data type.

## Consent Audit & Compliance

**Track:**
- Who consented and when (timestamp).
- What specific data types were consented to.
- Which channels they consented to (email, SMS, photo, etc.).
- Any withdrawals or updates.

**Proof:**
- Consent form (signed or digital).
- Timestamp of digital consent.
- Email confirmation sent to parent.
- Record of parent's acknowledgment.

## Common Consent Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Blanket Consent**
- "I accept all school policies and data collection."
- Fix: Break into specific data types and purposes.

**Mistake 2: No Consent for New Uses**
- School starts using biometrics; no new consent asked.
- Fix: Request specific consent before starting new data collection or use.

**Mistake 3: Making Consent Mandatory**
- School withholds admission if consent is refused.
- Fix: Make consent optional for non-critical data. Offer alternatives for critical data (e.g., "Choose biometric or manual attendance").

**Mistake 4: No Consent Registry**
- School collects data but can't prove it had consent.
- Fix: Maintain a signed or digital record for every consent.

**Mistake 5: Ignoring Withdrawal Requests**
- Parent asks to withdraw consent; school continues using data.
- Fix: Honor all withdrawal requests within 7 days. Document them.

## FAQ

**Q1: Can parents refuse to give consent without losing admission?**
A: Depends on the data type. Non-critical data (photos, biometric): YES, refusal is allowed. Critical data (contact, address): NO, schools need minimum data to operate. Schools must offer alternatives for critical data.

**Q2: Is a blanket "I accept terms and conditions" consent valid under DPDP?**
A: No. Consent must be specific to each data type and purpose. Blanket clauses are not compliant.

**Q3: How long should schools keep consent documents?**
A: At least 2+ years. This proves schools had consent if regulators ask.

**Q4: Can a parent withdraw consent for medical data mid-year?**
A: Yes, but the school can then refuse to administer medication or monitor conditions, potentially affecting the student. School should discuss consequences with the parent before accepting withdrawal.

**Q5: What if a divorced parent refuses to give consent but the other parent agrees?**
A: Confirm guardianship. If both have joint custody, both must consent. If one has sole custody, only that parent's consent is required.

**Q6: Can schools make consent a condition of enrollment?**
A: Consent for essential data (address, contact) can be required. Consent for non-essential data (photos, biometric, optional communications) cannot be required; alternatives must be offered.
