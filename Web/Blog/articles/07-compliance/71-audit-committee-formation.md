An audit committee is a subcommittee of the school's governing board with specific oversight responsibility for financial controls, internal audit, risk management, and external audit liaison. While not legally required for all school structures, an audit committee is a governance best practice that strengthens financial accountability.

For school trusts and societies managing significant fee income, government grants, and development funds, an audit committee provides the oversight layer that protects the institution from financial mismanagement.

## Why Schools Need an Audit Committee

**Fee accountability:** Schools collect substantial fee income from parents. Without oversight, there is no independent verification that all fees collected reach the school's accounts.

**Grant compliance:** Government grants carry conditions. The audit committee monitors whether grant conditions are being met and utilization certificates are prepared accurately.

**External audit interface:** The committee interacts with external auditors independently of school management, ensuring auditors can raise concerns without management interference.

**Fraud prevention:** Regular internal review of financial controls reduces the opportunity for fraud or unauthorized expenditure.

**Board oversight:** Audit committee provides the full governing board with an independent financial summary, enabling board members who are not finance specialists to exercise meaningful oversight.

## Composition

An effective audit committee for a school includes:
- 2-3 trustees/governing board members (at least one with financial or accounting experience)
- External member (CA, retired banker, or finance professional, not the school's statutory auditor)
- Optionally: school's Finance Manager as a non-voting member for information provision

**Key principle:** The audit committee must be independent of school management. Members who are also involved in school operations cannot provide effective oversight.

## Core Functions

**Internal audit oversight:**
- Review internal audit findings
- Ensure management has addressed audit observations
- Track repeat findings (recurring issues indicate systemic problems)

**External audit oversight:**
- Review external auditor's appointment
- Receive and review audit report before board presentation
- Discuss audit qualifications and management responses with auditors directly (without management present)

**Financial control review:**
- Review fee collection and reconciliation procedures
- Review payment authorization processes
- Review development fund investment and utilization
- Review procurement and contract processes

**Risk management:**
- Identify financial risks (fee collection defaults, grant compliance risks, liability exposure)
- Ensure appropriate risk mitigation is in place

**Annual review of financial statements:**
- Committee reviews draft financial statements before they are presented to the full board

## Meeting Frequency

The audit committee should meet at minimum twice a year:
- Once before the external audit begins (to review audit scope and focus)
- Once after receiving the draft audit report (to review findings and management response)

Additional meetings may be needed if significant issues arise.

## FAQ

**Q: Is an audit committee legally required for school trusts?**

A: Not universally. Companies Act companies above a certain threshold have mandatory audit committee requirements. Trust and society law does not generally mandate audit committees. However, they are a governance best practice for any school managing significant financial flows.

**Q: How does an audit committee differ from the school's governing board?**

A: The audit committee is a specialist subcommittee that digs into financial controls, audit findings, and risk management in depth. The full governing board receives a summary from the committee and takes oversight decisions based on that summary. The committee handles the detailed financial scrutiny.

**Q: Can management be present during audit committee meetings?**

A: For information provision, yes. For the portion of meetings where auditors report directly to the committee, or where committee discusses concerns about management performance, management should not be present. The committee's independence requires the ability to meet without management.
