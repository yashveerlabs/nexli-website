Access to safe drinking water is a fundamental requirement for schools under the RTE Act 2009 and various state education regulations. CBSE affiliation norms also require schools to provide safe, potable drinking water to students. Water-borne illness outbreaks in schools are serious events, they affect student health and trigger regulatory scrutiny.

## Legal and Regulatory Basis

**RTE Act 2009:** Section 19 and Schedule mandates safe drinking water facility as a minimum infrastructure requirement for schools.

**State regulations:** Most states have specific norms for school water safety under their education department orders.

**CBSE affiliation:** CBSE inspections check for safe drinking water provision.

**BIS standards:** Drinking water must meet IS 10500 (Indian Standard for Drinking Water Specification).

## Types of Water Sources and Their Risks

**Municipal/corporation supply:** Generally treated, but can be contaminated in distribution. Requires storage and filtration at school level.

**Borewell/groundwater:** Higher risk of contamination (fluoride, arsenic, nitrates in various regions). Requires testing and appropriate treatment.

**Rainwater harvesting:** Requires treatment before consumption.

**Tanker supply:** High contamination risk due to tanker hygiene. Requires filtration and disinfection.

## Water Quality Standards

Drinking water for schools must meet IS 10500:
- Turbidity: max 5 NTU
- Nitrates: max 45 mg/L
- Fluoride: max 1 mg/L
- Coliform bacteria: absent in 100 mL

Schools should test water quality:
- Annually at minimum
- After any incident (flooding, pipeline damage, reports of illness)
- From an accredited laboratory

**Test parameters:** Physical (turbidity, color, taste), Chemical (pH, nitrate, fluoride, hardness), Biological (coliform, E. coli)

## Treatment Systems

**Reverse Osmosis (RO) systems:** Effective for chemical contaminants and total dissolved solids. Does not remove all bacteria if membranes are damaged.

**UV disinfection:** Kills bacteria and viruses. Does not remove chemical contaminants.

**RO + UV combination:** Most comprehensive treatment for school use.

## Maintenance Requirements

Treatment systems that are not maintained are not effective:

**RO systems:**
- Filter change schedule per manufacturer specification (typically every 6-12 months)
- Membrane replacement (typically every 2-3 years)
- Annual service by qualified technician
- Maintenance records maintained

**UV systems:**
- UV lamp replacement annually (or per manufacturer specification)
- Regular cleaning of quartz sleeve
- Lamp change records maintained

**Water storage tanks:**
- Clean every 6 months (more frequently in summer)
- Covered to prevent contamination
- Cleaning records maintained

## Access and Hygiene

- Sufficient water points for student population (RTE norms specify ratios)
- Water dispensers at appropriate height for younger students
- Cups/dispensers cleaned regularly
- Clear distance from toilets and waste disposal areas

## FAQ

**Q: How often must a school test its water quality?**

A: Annual testing is a minimum standard. Schools with groundwater sources, older plumbing, or any history of illness complaints should test more frequently (every 6 months). Testing must be from an accredited laboratory.

**Q: What if water test results show contamination?**

A: Immediate action: stop using that water source for drinking, investigate the contamination source, implement treatment or switch source, retest before resuming. Notify parents if there is any health risk.

**Q: Does an RO water purifier alone ensure compliance with IS 10500?**

A: Not necessarily. RO systems reduce TDS and chemical contaminants but a properly maintained RO + UV combination is more comprehensive. Water should still be tested from the outlet of any treatment system annually.

**Q: What records does the school need for water safety compliance?**

A: Water quality test reports (from accredited lab), maintenance records for water treatment systems, tank cleaning records with dates. These should be available for inspection.
