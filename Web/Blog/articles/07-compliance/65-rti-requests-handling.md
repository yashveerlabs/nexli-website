The Right to Information Act 2005 (RTI Act) enables Indian citizens to request information from public authorities. Understanding which schools are covered by RTI and what that means operationally is essential for school compliance.

## Which Schools Are Covered by RTI

**Government schools:** All government schools are public authorities under the RTI Act. They must respond to RTI requests within 30 days.

**Government-aided private schools:** Schools receiving substantial government aid are typically treated as public authorities for RTI purposes. The extent of the obligation depends on the quantum of government aid received and court interpretations applicable in the state.

**Private unaided schools:** Generally, private unaided schools (no government funding) are not directly subject to the RTI Act. However, they may be indirectly covered if they perform public functions, receive government concessions, or are subject to state education regulations that carry disclosure obligations.

Courts have periodically extended or restricted RTI coverage to aided private schools; schools should check the current applicable judicial position in their state.

## What Government and Aided Schools Must Disclose

Under Section 4 of the RTI Act, public authorities must proactively disclose (without waiting for a request) certain categories of information, including:
- School structure and management
- Powers and duties of officers
- Decision-making procedures
- Budget and expenditure
- Staff positions and salaries
- Service rules and policies

## Responding to RTI Requests

Government schools must:

1. **Designate a Public Information Officer (PIO):** Typically the Principal or a designated administrative officer

2. **Acknowledge receipt:** Within 5 days of receiving an RTI request

3. **Respond within 30 days:** Provide the information requested or explain the exemption that applies

4. **Fee structure:** RTI applications include a prescribed fee (typically Rs. 10 for central government; state governments set their own fees)

5. **Exemptions:** Certain information is exempt from disclosure under Section 8 of the RTI Act: information affecting national security, personal information of individuals, information held in fiduciary capacity. Schools may use exemptions where genuinely applicable, not as a default refusal.

## Common RTI Requests Schools Receive

- Staff salaries and service conditions
- Fee structure and collection details
- Infrastructure details
- UDISE+ data submitted
- Government grant utilization
- Student enrollment and category data
- Results of compliance inspections

## What Happens When Schools Don't Respond

Failure to respond within the statutory timeline results in:
- First appeal to the appellate authority (a senior officer in the same organization)
- Second appeal to the State Information Commission or Central Information Commission
- The Commission can direct disclosure and impose penalties on the PIO

## FAQ

**Q: Are all CBSE-affiliated schools subject to RTI?**

A: Not automatically. RTI applies to public authorities. Government CBSE schools are covered. CBSE affiliation alone does not make a private unaided school a public authority for RTI purposes.

**Q: Can a school refuse to disclose information about a student under RTI?**

A: Yes. Personal information about individual students is protected under Section 8(1)(j) of the RTI Act and, separately, under DPDP Act provisions. Schools should not disclose student personal data in response to RTI requests.

**Q: What is the penalty for a PIO who fails to respond within 30 days?**

A: The Information Commission can impose a daily penalty of Rs. 250 per day of delay, up to Rs. 25,000 total, on the PIO personally. The Commission can also award compensation to the applicant.

**Q: Can a private school be required to disclose information under RTI if ordered by a High Court?**

A: Yes. High Courts have jurisdiction to direct disclosure from private schools if the school performs a public function or receives public money. Schools in states with broad RTI case law interpretations should seek legal advice on their specific obligations.
