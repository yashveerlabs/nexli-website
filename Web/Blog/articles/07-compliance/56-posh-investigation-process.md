The POSH Act 2013 requires that the ICC complete its inquiry within 90 days of receiving a complaint. During this time, the ICC must follow a procedure that upholds natural justice for both the complainant and the respondent. An inquiry that cuts corners on procedure is vulnerable to legal challenge.

## Step 1: Acknowledge Receipt and Notify Respondent

Within 7 days of receiving the complaint:
- ICC acknowledges receipt in writing to the complainant
- ICC issues a written notice to the respondent with a copy of (or summary of) the complaint
- ICC gives the respondent an opportunity to submit a written reply within 10 working days

## Step 2: Consider Conciliation (Optional)

Before beginning inquiry, the ICC may offer the complainant the option of conciliation. Conciliation is a voluntary process where the ICC facilitates a resolution between the parties. It is used only if the complainant requests it.

**Important:** The ICC should not use conciliation to pressure the complainant into settling. If the complaint involves an act that amounts to a criminal offence, conciliation is not appropriate.

If conciliation is reached: ICC records the settlement and monitors compliance. If the respondent breaches the settlement: ICC proceeds with full inquiry.

## Step 3: Examine the Complaint and Evidence

The ICC reviews all material submitted with the complaint:
- Written complaint and supporting documents
- Messages, emails, screenshots submitted as evidence
- CCTV footage or other records relevant to the incident

## Step 4: Interview the Complainant

The ICC interviews the complainant in a safe, private setting:
- ICC members ask clarifying questions (not leading or adversarial)
- The process must not re-traumatize the complainant
- If the complainant requests support (a trusted colleague to accompany), accommodate this where appropriate
- Record the interview summary (not necessarily verbatim) for the case file

## Step 5: Interview Witnesses

ICC interviews witnesses named by the complainant and, where relevant, other witnesses identified during inquiry. For each witness:
- Interview conducted privately
- Witness informed of confidentiality obligations
- Summary recorded and signed by the witness

## Step 6: Notify Respondent of Evidence

ICC gives the respondent an opportunity to respond to specific evidence and witness statements. This is the natural justice requirement: the respondent must know what case they are facing.

## Step 7: Interview the Respondent

ICC interviews the respondent:
- Respondent may have a support person present (not a lawyer, unless both parties have legal representation)
- ICC members put evidence to the respondent and hear their response
- ICC asks questions to clarify facts
- Summary recorded and signed by the respondent

## Step 8: Deliberate and Prepare the Report

ICC members meet to deliberate:
- What evidence is credible?
- What is the balance of probabilities? (POSH uses civil standard, not criminal standard)
- Is the complaint substantiated?
- If substantiated: what action is recommended?
- If not substantiated: is there evidence of a false and malicious complaint?

The inquiry report contains:
- Summary of complaint
- Summary of evidence
- Findings of fact
- Conclusion (complaint substantiated or not)
- Recommended action

## Step 9: Submit Report and Action

ICC submits the inquiry report to school management within 90 days of the complaint. Management must implement the recommendation within 60 days.

**If complaint is substantiated:** Recommended action may include warning, fine, suspension, termination, or any combination.

**If complaint is not substantiated:** Case is closed. If the complaint was malicious, ICC may recommend action against the complainant (used cautiously; genuine but unsuccessful complaints must not be treated as malicious).

## FAQ

**Q: What standard of proof does the ICC apply?**

A: The POSH Act requires the ICC to apply the civil standard: balance of probabilities. This is lower than the criminal standard of beyond reasonable doubt. The ICC must decide whether it is more likely than not that the conduct occurred.

**Q: Can the ICC extend the 90-day timeline?**

A: With valid reasons, the ICC can extend the inquiry timeline. The extension and reasons should be documented. Indefinite extensions that prevent resolution are a problem; the Act intends for timely resolution.

**Q: Can the complainant be cross-examined by the respondent?**

A: The respondent does not have the right to directly cross-examine the complainant. The ICC manages all questioning. The respondent can submit questions through the ICC for the Presiding Officer to ask the complainant if the ICC determines they are relevant.

**Q: What if the respondent refuses to participate in the inquiry?**

A: The ICC can proceed with the inquiry on the basis of available evidence and draw reasonable inferences from the respondent's non-participation.
