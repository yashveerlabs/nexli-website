School canteens serve hundreds to thousands of students daily. Food safety in the canteen is both a health issue and a compliance issue. FSSAI has issued specific guidelines for school canteens, and CBSE has issued directions on restricting junk food in schools. Schools that operate canteens without following these standards face health risks, parental complaints, and regulatory action.

## FSSAI Guidelines for School Canteens

FSSAI's "Eat Right School" initiative and specific canteen guidelines cover:

**What should be available:**
- Fresh fruits and vegetables
- Whole grain options (rotis, rice, whole grain bread)
- Dairy products (milk, curd)
- Pulses and legumes
- Drinking water

**What should not be sold:**
- High-fat, high-sugar, high-salt (HFSS) foods
- Foods with artificial colors, flavors, or preservatives
- Carbonated soft drinks
- Energy drinks
- Foods with excessive trans fats
- Packaged foods with "red" traffic light labeling for fat, sugar, or salt (under FSSAI's display regulations)

## CBSE Guidelines on Junk Food

CBSE issued circulars directing affiliated schools to:
- Not allow sale of junk food (chips, candies, carbonated drinks, etc.) in school canteens
- Promote nutritious food options
- Discourage vendors near school gates from selling junk food to students

These are binding on CBSE-affiliated schools.

## Hygiene Requirements in the Canteen

**Food preparation area:**
- Clean, pest-free kitchen with smooth, washable surfaces
- Separate areas for raw and cooked food preparation
- Adequate ventilation

**Food handlers:**
- Personal hygiene: clean hands (washed before food handling), hair covered, no jewelry
- Health check: food handlers with illness (diarrhea, skin infections) must not handle food
- No smoking in food preparation areas

**Storage:**
- Cooked food stored at appropriate temperature (hot food above 60°C; cold food below 5°C)
- No reuse of cooked food after 4 hours at room temperature
- Raw foods stored separately from cooked foods in refrigerator
- No food stored directly on the floor

**Cleaning:**
- All utensils washed with hot water and detergent after use
- Surfaces cleaned and sanitized after each use
- Waste disposed in covered bins; emptied daily

## Model School Canteen Policy

Schools should have a written canteen policy covering:
- Approved food items and prohibited items
- Pricing policy (affordable for all students)
- Hygiene standards and enforcement
- Annual FSSAI registration renewal
- Periodic inspection by school administration

The policy should be shared with canteen operators and parents.

## Parent Communication

Parents should know:
- The school's canteen policy (what is sold and what is not)
- That unhealthy food is not sold on school premises
- That food safety standards are maintained and periodically inspected

Some schools share canteen menus with parents monthly.

## FAQ

**Q: Can a school earn revenue from allowing junk food vendors on campus?**

A: No. CBSE guidelines and FSSAI recommendations both discourage junk food sales in schools. Allowing vendors to sell junk food for school revenue creates regulatory risk and contradicts the school's duty of care for student health.

**Q: What if parents complain about the food quality in the canteen?**

A: Take complaints seriously. Investigate (taste the food, check preparation conditions), address the issue, and inform the parent. A formal complaint process for food quality is part of good canteen management.

**Q: Are vending machines in schools covered by FSSAI requirements?**

A: Yes. Vending machines selling food or beverages are food businesses. The school is responsible for ensuring the vending machine operator has FSSAI compliance and that the products sold comply with school food policy (no HFSS products).

**Q: How often should the school conduct a canteen inspection?**

A: Quarterly inspection by the Principal or designated administrator is a minimum. Surprise inspections are more effective than announced visits for checking hygiene conditions.
