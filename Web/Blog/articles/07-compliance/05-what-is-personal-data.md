## Understanding Personal Data Under DPDP Act

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act defines "personal data" broadly: any information that identifies or can reasonably identify an individual. For schools, this includes far more than most administrators realize. From a student's name and address to their medical history and grades, almost everything collected is personal data subject to DPDP protection.

Understanding what qualifies as personal data is the foundation for DPDP compliance.

## Categories of Personal Data in Schools

### Category 1: Identity Data

Information that directly identifies a person.

**Examples:**
- Full name
- Date of birth
- Photograph
- Video recording
- Signature
- Roll number or student ID
- Aadhaar number
- Passport number
- PAN (parent's)

**Why It Matters:** Identity data is the most sensitive; it can be used for fraud or impersonation. Schools must obtain explicit consent before collecting.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Store securely (locked file cabinet or encrypted digital).
- Limit access (only admission staff, not all teachers).
- Don't display student photos publicly without consent.
- Don't share with third parties without consent.

### Category 2: Contact Information

Information used to reach a person.

**Examples:**
- Phone number (student, parent, guardian)
- Email address
- Postal address
- WhatsApp number
- Contact person (emergency contact)

**Why It Matters:** Contact info is used for communication; if leaked, students/parents can be targeted by unsolicited calls or messages.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Collect only contact numbers for verified parents/guardians.
- Don't share phone numbers or email addresses with other students or parents.
- Use contact info only for school-related communication.
- Allow parents to update contact info anytime.

### Category 3: Family & Relationship Data

Information about family structure and relationships.

**Examples:**
- Parent names and occupations
- Sibling names and ages
- Grandparent contact information
- Marital status of parents
- Guardianship information
- Information about divorced/separated parents

**Why It Matters:** Family information can reveal socioeconomic status, family structure issues, or parental conflicts. Disclosure can embarrass families or lead to discrimination.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Collect only information necessary for emergency contact and guardianship purposes.
- Don't share family information with other students or staff unnecessarily.
- Store guardianship documents securely.
- When multiple parents exist, confirm which one has custody/guardianship.

### Category 4: Financial Data

Information about a family's financial status or transactions.

**Examples:**
- Fee amount paid or outstanding
- Parent's salary (for fee concession assessment)
- Bank account details (for fee payment)
- Credit card information
- Scholarship amount or concession granted
- RTE/EWS status (indicates economic weakness)

**Why It Matters:** Financial data is highly sensitive and often used to assess whether a family is "poor" or "struggling." Public disclosure causes shame and discrimination.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Collect minimum financial data (fee payment info only).
- Don't ask for parent's salary unless necessary for concessions.
- Don't display fee status publicly or in class.
- Store payment details securely; never share credit card info.
- Don't disclose concession amounts to the student (can be demoralizing).

### Category 5: Academic Data

Information about a student's academic performance.

**Examples:**
- Attendance records
- Test scores and exam marks
- Report card grades
- Homework completion status
- Class position or ranking
- Subject-specific performance
- Special education needs or learning disability diagnosis

**Why It Matters:** Academic data directly reflects a student's performance. Public disclosure (e.g., displaying toppers' names) can humiliate low performers or cause bullying.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Attendance and grades visible to parents only, not other students.
- No public display of low performers or defaulters.
- Learning disability information accessible only to relevant staff (teacher, counselor, principal).
- Don't share marks with other students or parents.
- Archive academic records; allow parents to request copies.

### Category 6: Health & Medical Data

Information about a student's health, medical history, or treatment.

**Examples:**
- Allergies (food, medicine, environmental)
- Chronic conditions (asthma, diabetes, epilepsy)
- Disabilities or special needs
- Immunization status and vaccination records
- Prescription medications
- Medical examinations and test results
- Mental health information or counseling sessions
- Injury or illness history
- Pregnancy or sexual health information

**Why It Matters:** Health data is highly sensitive. Disclosure of a disability or medical condition can lead to discrimination or bullying.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Collect only health data necessary for student safety and emergency response.
- Store in a confidential medical file (separate from academic file).
- Only school nurse, principal, and relevant teacher (e.g., PE teacher for asthma) should access.
- Don't disclose medical information to peers or other teachers.
- For infectious diseases (COVID, chickenpox), inform affected class only as needed.
- Never disclose mental health treatment or counseling to other students.

### Category 7: Behavioral & Disciplinary Data

Information about a student's behavior, conduct, or discipline.

**Examples:**
- Warnings or disciplinary action
- Incident reports (bullying, fighting, cheating)
- Behavioral observations by teachers
- Detention or suspension records
- Comments about attitude or conduct

**Why It Matters:** Disciplinary records can unfairly label a student as "bad" or "troublemaker." Public disclosure causes shame and affects future opportunities.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Store discipline records separately (confidential file).
- Disclose only to parents and relevant staff.
- Don't display on bulletin boards or class charts.
- Allow students to respond to allegations (due process).
- Expunge old records (e.g., delete after student leaves).

### Category 8: Biometric Data

Physical or biological measurements.

**Examples:**
- Fingerprints (for attendance or identification)
- Face recognition data (for entry/exit tracking)
- Height, weight, or body measurements
- Blood group

**Why It Matters:** Biometric data is unique and permanent. If leaked, it cannot be changed (unlike passwords). Misuse can lead to identity fraud.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Use biometric systems only when absolutely necessary (e.g., attendance).
- Store biometric templates securely (encrypted).
- Don't retain unnecessary biometric data.
- Get explicit parental consent before collecting biometrics.
- Don't share biometric data with third parties.

### Category 9: Location & Movement Data

Information about where a student is or travels.

**Examples:**
- Home address
- GPS data from school transport
- Entry/exit times (from RFID or facial recognition)
- Hostel or boarding location
- Travel route from home to school

**Why It Matters:** Location data reveals patterns and can be used for stalking or targeting.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Address is necessary; keep it secure.
- GPS tracking of buses is for safety; don't share real-time location with unauthorized parties.
- Entry/exit data is for security; retain only for a short period.
- Don't disclose hostel room numbers or dorm assignments.

### Category 10: Online & Digital Data

Information generated through digital interactions.

**Examples:**
- IP address and device ID
- Login information and activity logs
- Passwords (for parent portal or student email)
- Social media or online communication (if monitored by school)
- Digital footprints on school systems

**Why It Matters:** Digital data can reveal online habits, interests, or vulnerabilities. Breaches can lead to hacking or identity theft.

**How Schools Should Handle:**
- Use secure login systems (OTP-based, not just passwords).
- Don't store passwords in plain text; use encrypted systems.
- Don't monitor personal social media without parental consent.
- Secure all school digital systems (website, parent portal, student email).

## Special Category: Sensitive Personal Data

DPDP Act defines "sensitive personal data" as requiring extra protection:

**Sensitive Data:**
- Medical/health information
- Religious beliefs
- Caste or tribe
- Biometric data (fingerprints, face recognition)
- Sexual orientation or gender identity
- Genetic data
- Court proceedings or criminal history

**Enhanced Protection Required:**
- Extra-explicit consent (not just signing admission form).
- Separate consent for each sensitive data type.
- Encryption mandatory.
- Limited staff access.
- Data breach notification mandatory.

**Example:** A school cannot collect "student's religion" just for admission. If needed (e.g., for religious holiday accommodations), it requires separate, explicit consent.

## FAQ

**Q1: Is a student's class/section number personal data?**
A: No, class/section is not personally identifying (multiple students in each class). However, when combined with name, it identifies a student, so the combination is personal data.

**Q2: Can schools share a student's photo for a school magazine without consent?**
A: No. Photo is personal data. Consent required for publication. Exception: Blurred or anonymized photos might not require consent, but it's safer to get consent.

**Q3: Is a student's hobby or interest information personal data?**
A: Yes. Information about interests, preferences, or hobbies can identify and be used to profile a student. Collect only if necessary.

**Q4: Can schools share student data with government agencies (e.g., for UDISE+ reporting)?**
A: Yes, if data is necessary for government mandates and can be anonymized/aggregated. Individual student data requires consent.

**Q5: How long should schools retain student data after they leave?**
A: Minimum 2 years (for disputes, verification, certificate issuance). After that, securely delete or anonymize. Exceptions: RTE records (longer retention), legal holds, etc.

**Q6: Is a student's name on a class topper list personal data?**
A: Yes. Displaying a student's name publicly (even for achievement) is personal data disclosure. Get consent first, or use rankings without names.
