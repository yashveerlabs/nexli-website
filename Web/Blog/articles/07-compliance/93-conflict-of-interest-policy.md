A conflict of interest policy is a formal document that defines what constitutes a conflict of interest for school board members, requires disclosure of conflicts, and specifies how conflicted transactions are managed. This policy protects the school from decisions driven by board members' personal interests and protects board members from accusations of impropriety.

## Why Schools Need a Conflict of Interest Policy

School boards make decisions with financial implications: vendor selection, property transactions, service contracts, appointment of senior staff. When board members have personal financial or professional interests connected to these decisions, their objectivity is compromised.

Without a formal policy:
- Board members may not realize when they have a conflict
- Conflicts may not be disclosed
- Conflicted decisions may be challenged in court or by the Registrar
- The school's reputation and financial resources can be damaged

## What the Policy Must Define

### Types of Conflicts

The policy must clearly define what constitutes a conflict of interest:

**Financial conflicts:**
- Board member or their family member has a financial interest in a business that does business with the school
- Board member stands to benefit financially from a school decision

**Employment conflicts:**
- Board member's family member is employed by the school or seeking employment
- Board member is also an employee of the school (in a senior role)

**Professional conflicts:**
- Board member's professional firm (CA firm, legal firm, construction company) provides services to the school

**Indirect conflicts:**
- Board member is a director or significant shareholder of a company that has a commercial relationship with the school

### Disclosure Obligation

Board members must:
- Declare any conflict before discussions on the relevant matter begin
- Submit an annual written declaration of business interests and relationships that could give rise to a conflict

### Recusal Procedure

When a conflict is declared:
- The conflicted board member must not participate in the discussion of the conflicted matter
- The conflicted board member must abstain from any vote on the matter
- Depending on severity, the conflicted member may be asked to leave the room during discussion

### Record-Keeping

All conflict disclosures must be recorded in the board meeting minutes, including:
- The nature of the conflict declared
- By whom
- That the member was recused from discussion and vote
- The decision made by the non-conflicted members

## Arm's Length Transactions

If the school must do business with a connected party (perhaps the only qualified supplier), the transaction must be at arm's length, on terms no more favorable than the school would obtain from an unrelated party. This must be documented and approved by non-conflicted board members.

## Annual Disclosure

Board members should annually submit a written declaration disclosing:
- All businesses they have a significant financial interest in
- All professional relationships that could give rise to a conflict
- Any family member employed by or seeking employment from the school

## Enforcement

The policy must specify what happens if a conflict is not disclosed:
- The conflicted decision may be voided
- The board member may face removal from the board
- The Registrar may be informed

## FAQ

**Q: Is a conflict of interest automatically disqualifying from board membership?**

A: No. Having a potential conflict does not disqualify someone from board membership. The conflict must be disclosed and managed through recusal. Only if conflicts are so pervasive that the member cannot effectively serve does it become a membership question.

**Q: Must the conflict of interest policy be shared with parents or the public?**

A: The policy itself (not individual disclosure records) is a governance document that can be shared publicly. Some schools include it in their governance disclosure on the website. Individual disclosure records are board documents.

**Q: What if the entire board has a conflict on a particular matter?**

A: If all board members are conflicted, the decision may need to be made by an independent authority (e.g., seeking court approval for the transaction, or bringing in an independent advisor). Situations where the entire board is conflicted are a serious governance problem that should be addressed structurally.
