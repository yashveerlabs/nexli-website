Bullying is a serious welfare issue that causes lasting harm to students. CBSE has issued circulars requiring affiliated schools to have anti-bullying policies and mechanisms in place. Schools that ignore bullying or handle it inconsistently face parental complaints, student welfare failures, and regulatory scrutiny.

An effective anti-bullying policy prevents bullying where possible, addresses it when it occurs, and protects both the victim and other students.

## What Is Bullying

The policy must define bullying precisely so students, parents, and staff recognize it:

**Bullying is:**
- Repeated aggressive behavior (physical, verbal, or social) toward a person with less power or who cannot easily defend themselves
- Intentional and ongoing (not a single disagreement)

**Types of bullying:**
- **Physical:** Hitting, pushing, taking belongings, unwanted physical contact
- **Verbal:** Insults, name-calling, threats, spreading rumors
- **Social/relational:** Excluding from groups, turning others against the student, public humiliation
- **Cyberbullying:** Harassment through social media, messaging apps, or online platforms

**What bullying is not:**
- A single conflict between students of equal standing
- Mutual teasing where both parties participate willingly
- A disagreement or argument without power imbalance

## Reporting Procedure

Students must know how to report bullying:
- To any trusted teacher or staff member
- Directly to the school counselor or CPO
- Through an anonymous reporting mechanism (suggestion box, online form)
- Parents reporting on behalf of their child

Staff who witness bullying must intervene and report to the counselor or coordinator.

## Investigation

When a bullying complaint is received:
1. Incident is recorded with date, description, and names of parties
2. Separate interviews with the victim, the accused, and any witnesses
3. Assessment: is this bullying (repeated, power imbalance) or a single incident?
4. Interim protective measures for the victim while investigation is ongoing (seating change, supervision during break)

## Consequences for Bullying

Consequences must be:
- Proportionate to the severity and frequency
- Aimed at changing the behavior, not just punishing
- Clearly specified in the policy so students know in advance

Typical consequence ladder:
- First incident: formal warning, parental notification, counseling
- Repeated incidents: suspension from activities, parental meeting, counseling
- Severe or persistent bullying: suspension or exclusion

**Note:** Consequences must not further harm the victim (e.g., removing the victim from class rather than the perpetrator).

## Support for the Victim

The victim receives:
- Immediate support from the counselor
- Regular check-ins from a trusted adult
- Monitoring for ongoing bullying after intervention
- Academic accommodations if the bullying has affected performance

## Cyberbullying Considerations

Cyberbullying that occurs outside school can still be addressed by the school if:
- It involves school community members
- It significantly affects school environment or student welfare

The school should have a social media and technology use policy that covers student online behavior and bullying.

## Prevention Programs

Anti-bullying prevention is more effective than reactive management:
- Annual class sessions on what bullying is, how to report, how to support a victim
- Bystander education: students are empowered to report bullying they witness
- Peer mentoring programs
- Culture of reporting: students must believe reporting will help, not make things worse

## FAQ

**Q: Must the school report bullying incidents to any external authority?**

A: Bullying that constitutes a criminal offence (assault, extortion) must be reported to police. Bullying that involves a POCSO offence (sexual in nature, involving a minor) must be reported to police under mandatory reporting. Non-criminal bullying is managed internally but records should be maintained.

**Q: Can a student be expelled for bullying?**

A: Students in Classes 1-8 cannot be expelled under RTE Act. For secondary students, the school's disciplinary policy applies. Expulsion is a severe consequence reserved for extreme cases. The goal is behavioral change.

**Q: How should the school handle a situation where the bully's parent denies their child bullies?**

A: Document the incidents independently of parental acknowledgment. Present the documentation. Explain the investigation findings. Implement consequences based on findings. Parental denial does not prevent the school from acting on evidence.

**Q: Should cyberbullying between students outside school hours be addressed by the school?**

A: If the cyberbullying involves school community members and significantly affects the school environment, the school has both the authority and responsibility to address it. The school's technology use policy should clearly state that student online conduct that affects school community is within the school's jurisdiction.
