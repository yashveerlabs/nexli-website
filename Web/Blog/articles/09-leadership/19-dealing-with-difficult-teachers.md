## Dealing with Difficult Teachers: How Principals Support or Remove Underperformers

**Every principal faces difficult teachers: the one who chronically arrives late, the one whose teaching methods are outdated, the one who's disrespectful to parents, the one who doesn't follow school policies. Ignoring these issues damages school culture, student learning, and staff morale. But confronting them is uncomfortable, emotionally draining, and legally risky if not handled properly. This article teaches you how to distinguish between performance issues (fixable with coaching) and conduct issues (requiring formal action), and provides a clear framework for managing both.**

---

## The Problem: Types of Difficult Teachers

### Type 1: Performance Issues (Fixable)
- Low student learning outcomes
- Poor classroom management
- Outdated teaching methods
- Weak communication with parents

**Cause:** Skill gaps, knowledge gaps, need for feedback/coaching

### Type 2: Conduct Issues (Potentially Unfixable)
- Chronic absenteeism
- Disrespect to staff/parents
- Policy violations
- Safety violations

**Cause:** Attitude, willingness, character issues

### Type 3: Personality Issues (Often Unfixable)
- Negative, critical attitude
- Undermines decisions
- Sows discord among staff
- Resists feedback

**Cause:** Temperament; unlikely to change

---

## Root Causes

1. **You're avoiding the conversation:** Discomfort with confrontation
2. **You're unclear on expectations:** Teacher doesn't know what "good" looks like
3. **You're inconsistent:** You tolerate it in some teachers but not others
4. **You're unsupported by leadership:** Board/trust doesn't back you
5. **You're trying to fix everything:** You should focus only on what's fixable

---

## Solutions: The Framework

### Step 1: Assess (Is This Fixable?)

Ask yourself:
1. **Is this a skill/performance issue or a character issue?**
   - Skill: Can be taught/improved
   - Character: Rarely changes

2. **Is this teacher willing to improve?**
   - Willing: Coaching can work
   - Resistant: Formal processes needed

3. **How much time/investment is this worth?**
   - Invest in high-potential teachers
   - Don't waste time on those who can't or won't improve

### Step 2: Clarify Expectations

**Many teachers don't know they're not meeting expectations.**

1. **Document what "good looks like":**
   - Standards for lesson planning, classroom management, student learning, parent communication
   - Share with all staff; make it a benchmark

2. **Use data:**
   - Student learning outcomes
   - Class attendance
   - Parent feedback
   - Observation notes

3. **Show the gap:**
   - "Here's what good looks like."
   - "Here's where we are."
   - "The gap is [specific behaviors/outcomes]."

### Step 3: For Performance Issues, Coach & Support

**Step-by-step coaching:**

1. **Initial conversation:**
   - "I've noticed [specific behavior]. I want to help. What's going on?"
   - Listen without judgment
   - Identify the root (skill gap? resource gap? personal issue?)

2. **Create an improvement plan:**
   - Clear goal: "Student passing rate will improve from 60% to 75%"
   - Specific actions: "You'll attend PD on outcome-based teaching; I'll observe monthly"
   - Timeline: "3 months to show improvement; we'll review on [date]"
   - Support: "I'll pair you with Ms. X; we'll provide materials; I'll coach you weekly"

3. **Monitor closely:**
   - Weekly check-ins for 4 weeks, then bi-weekly
   - Observe classes; provide feedback
   - Celebrate small wins
   - Document progress

4. **Review at 3 months:**
   - Has improvement happened? Yes → Congratulate, maintain standards
   - Partial improvement? → Extend plan, add more support
   - No improvement? → Move to formal process (see Step 4)

### Step 4: For Conduct Issues, Formal Process

**Conduct issues require structured, documented responses.**

1. **Document the issue:**
   - Dates, times, specific behaviors
   - Email: "Ms. X arrived 45 min late on [date]. Policy requires on-time arrival."

2. **First conversation (informal warning):**
   - "The policy requires [X]. You've violated it on [dates]."
   - "This needs to stop. Here's what I expect going forward."
   - Document: Send email recap

3. **Second conversation (if issue persists):**
   - "We discussed this on [date]. The behavior continues. This is a formal warning."
   - "If this continues, further action will be taken."
   - Document: Written warning placed in file

4. **Third conversation (if issue still persists):**
   - Final warning or dismissal
   - Consult your board/trust and legal counsel
   - Follow your school's defined process for removal

### Step 5: Know When to Remove

**Some teachers should not be in the school.** Examples:
- Safety violations (inappropriate behavior with students)
- Chronic policy violations that persist after warnings
- Conduct that damages school reputation or culture
- Performance that significantly harms students

**Process for removal:**
1. Document thoroughly (dates, witnesses, specific incidents)
2. Consult board and legal counsel
3. Follow school's documented dismissal process
4. Offer transition support (severance, reference letter, if appropriate)
5. Handle the announcement professionally

## Best Practices

### 1. **Distinguish Early**
Is this a performance issue (invest in coaching) or conduct issue (formal process)? Don't treat one as the other.

### 2. **Be Clear on Expectations**
Don't assume teachers know what "good" looks like. Show them.

### 3. **Coach Before You Criticize**
Give teachers a chance to improve. Most will respond to clear expectations and support.

### 4. **Be Consistent**
If you tolerate behavior from one teacher, others notice and resent the double standard.

### 5. **Document Everything**
Dates, times, specific incidents, conversations, agreements. Protects you and makes the case clear.

### 6. **Support the Teacher While Setting Boundaries**
"I want you to succeed, and here's what needs to change. I'll help. I also have expectations."

### 7. **Know Your Legal/Contractual Obligations**
Consult your school's HR policies and legal counsel before removing anyone.

## FAQ

**Q: How do I balance supporting a difficult teacher with protecting students?**
A: Support their improvement for a set period. If they don't improve and students are harmed, student safety comes first. Remove them.

**Q: What if the difficult teacher has seniority/protection?**
A: Seniority doesn't protect misconduct. Follow due process, document thoroughly, consult legal counsel.

**Q: Should I tell other staff about a teacher's improvement plan?**
A: Confidentiality is important, but results become visible. Keep details private; share only outcomes.

**Q: How do I handle a teacher who complains publicly after I've given feedback?**
A: Address privately: "Feedback is confidential. I expect professionalism." Document if it continues; treat as conduct issue.

**Q: Can I remove a teacher without a formal process?**
A: No. Follow your school's documented process. Failure to do so creates legal liability.
