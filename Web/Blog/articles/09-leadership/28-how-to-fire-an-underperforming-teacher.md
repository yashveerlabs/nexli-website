# How to Fire an Underperforming Teacher

Terminating a teacher is one of the hardest decisions you'll make. It's also sometimes necessary. Approach it carefully, fairly, and with support.

## When Termination Is Appropriate

**Performance Issues**
- Repeated failure to meet job expectations
- Genuine effort made but still not meeting standards
- PIP completed but goals not achieved
- Performance affecting students significantly
- Clear that person is not capable of doing job

**Behavioral Issues**
- Violation of school policy or professional ethics
- Insubordination or refusal to follow procedures
- Harassment or mistreatment of students or staff
- Breach of confidentiality
- Dishonesty or fraud
- Safety violations

**Serious One-Time Issues**
- Criminal behavior
- Violence or threats
- Abuse of students
- Gross neglect
- Extreme insubordination
- May warrant immediate termination

## Before Pulling the Trigger

**Consult HR and Legal**
- Absolutely essential
- Understand all requirements
- Documentation review
- Due process adherence
- Union considerations
- At-will vs. contract status
- Severance obligations
- Reference policy
- Risk assessment

**Review All Documentation**
- Is the record clear and consistent?
- Is documentation thorough?
- Are observations documented?
- Are expectations clear?
- Is due process followed?
- Are there any gaps?
- Are there any mitigating factors?
- Is termination defensible?

**Honest Assessment**
- Is this really necessary?
- Have you genuinely supported improvement?
- Is person truly incapable or unwilling?
- Would more time help?
- Is performance really affecting students?
- Are you being fair and consistent?
- Are there any biases affecting decision?

**Consider Alternatives**
- Can person succeed in different role?
- Is different grade or subject area possibility?
- Would part-time work work better?
- Is leave of absence option?
- Is resignation with severance option?
- Is reassignment to different school option?
- Sometimes graceful exit is better than termination

## The Termination Process

**Planning and Preparation**

**Timeline**
- Usually after PIP or performance documentation
- Legal review of all materials
- HR confirmation and guidance
- Ensure all paperwork complete
- Arrange meeting
- Plan transition

**Location and Participants**
- Private, confidential location (usually off-campus)
- HR or legal representative present
- Principal or supervisor delivering
- Possibly district-level administrator
- Not in public or with students present
- After hours if possible (more private)
- Secure exit plan

**Documentation Prepared**
- Termination letter with reasons
- Final paycheck information
- Benefits continuation information
- Property return checklist
- Exit procedures
- Reference policy
- Severance details (if any)
- Legal information

**Security and Logistics**
- Campus safety notified
- Keys and access cards identified
- Personal belongings plan
- Communication plan
- Substitute teacher arranged
- Coverage for classes
- Announcement to staff
- Not shared with others beforehand

**The Termination Meeting**

**Opening**
- Be direct and kind
- "I've called you in today because we've made the difficult decision to terminate your employment effective [date]"
- Don't make it longer than necessary
- Provide written notice
- Explain basics calmly

**Key Points to Cover**
- Termination effective date
- Reason for termination (brief, factual)
- Final paycheck information
- Benefits continuation (COBRA, etc.)
- Property to return
- Exit procedures
- What happens to evaluations/references
- Legal information
- HR contact for questions

**What NOT to Do**
- Don't argue or debate
- Don't get drawn into conversation about decision
- Don't sympathize too much (can appear insincere)
- Don't make promises about references
- Don't discuss internal school matters
- Don't discuss other staff
- Keep it professional and brief

**Keep It Moving**
- Usually 10-15 minutes
- Provide written documentation
- Answer direct factual questions
- HR person can explain process
- Security escorts person from building
- Don't prolong goodbyes
- Professional tone throughout

**After Termination**

**Announce to School**
- Brief, factual announcement
- "Effective today, [name] is no longer employed by our school"
- Don't provide details about reasons
- Maintain person's dignity
- Address staff questions: "We've made this decision after careful consideration"
- Don't bad-mouth or over-explain
- Let it settle

**Communication to Parents**
- Brief, factual email
- "[Name] is no longer with our school"
- "We're committed to continuity in your child's education"
- Introduce replacement if available
- Transition plan for classes
- Offer to discuss concerns

**Support for Remaining Staff**
- Acknowledge disruption
- Reassure about continuity
- Explain coverage plan
- Welcome replacement
- Don't discuss details of termination
- Answer questions about immediate transition
- Allow time for people to adjust

**Handle Transition**
- Get replacement teacher quickly
- Cover classes with quality substitutes initially
- Brief new teacher fully
- Support continuity for students
- Don't bad-mouth former teacher
- Forward mail and communications appropriately
- Close out accounts and systems
- Archive files appropriately

## Legal Protections

**Documentation Requirements**
- Clear record of performance issues
- Specific examples with dates
- Observations and feedback
- Support provided
- Written notice of deficiencies
- Opportunity to improve
- Clear expectations
- Consistent with policies

**Due Process**
- Advance notice (unless for-cause termination)
- Opportunity to respond
- Fair and impartial process
- Consistent with how others are treated
- No discrimination
- No retaliation
- No violation of rights

**Union Considerations**
- Contractual requirements
- Grievance procedures
- Arbitration possibilities
- Severance or termination clauses
- Notice requirements
- Union representation rights
- Collective bargaining agreement adherence

**At-Will vs. Contract**
- At-will: Can terminate for almost any reason
- Contract: Limited grounds for termination
- Written contract review essential
- Legal counsel on contract interpretation
- Understand your specific situation
- Don't assume at-will

## The Emotional Toll

This is Hard Work
- Terminating someone is stressful
- You may feel guilt or doubt
- Fear of retaliation or lawsuit
- Worry about what's fair
- Concern about their wellbeing

Acknowledge the Difficulty
- This is one of hardest parts of leadership
- It's okay to find it difficult
- It doesn't mean you're heartless
- It means you understand the stakes
- Seek support from mentors or colleagues
- Process the emotion appropriately

Remember the Purpose
- You're protecting student learning
- You're protecting school reputation
- You're protecting other staff
- You're being fair and consistent
- You're modeling accountability
- Sometimes people aren't right for job
- It's kinder to end it than prolong it

## When It Goes Wrong

**If Terminated Wrongfully or Legally**
- Follows all your legal guidance
- Unexpected lawsuit still possible
- Expensive and stressful
- Maintain professional demeanor
- Let legal/HR team handle
- Document everything for legal team
- Don't discuss in public forums
- Learn and adjust future process

**If Person Doesn't Accept It**
- Some people don't accept termination
- Emotions run high
- Threats or complaints may follow
- Let HR and legal handle escalation
- Remain professional and kind
- Don't engage in debate
- Refer to HR for follow-up
- Document any concerning behavior

**If School Community Upset**
- Some may question your decision
- If confidential, limited explanation possible
- "We made this decision carefully"
- "We're committed to maintaining quality"
- Don't violate privacy
- Let community adjust over time
- New teacher may ease transition
- Continuity of high-quality teaching helps

## Supporting Yourself

**Self-Care**
- This is emotionally draining
- Take time to process
- Talk to trusted mentor or friend
- Exercise and stress relief
- Don't ruminate
- Let it go after
- Remember you did what was needed

**Reflection**
- What could you have done differently?
- Should you have intervened earlier?
- What did you learn?
- How will this improve your process?
- What will you do differently next time?
- Growth from difficult experience

**Moving Forward**
- Focus on new teacher or replacement
- Welcome them well
- Build positive culture
- Don't spend energy on what was
- Look forward
- Hope and optimism
- School will heal and move on

Firing someone is one of leadership's most difficult responsibilities. Do it carefully, fairly, and with support. Protect your school, support your students, and ultimately, you're doing the person a kindness by ending a situation that wasn't working for them either.
