# How to Set School Vision and Mission

Your school's vision and mission are its north star. They guide strategy, shape culture, and inspire stakeholders. Getting them right matters deeply.

## Vision vs. Mission: What's the Difference?

**Vision** (What you aspire to)
- What your school will look like when fully successful
- Inspirational and future-focused
- Answers: "What are we building toward?"
- Timeframe: 5-10 years out
- Example: "A school where every child discovers their potential and pursues meaningful purpose"

**Mission** (Why you exist)
- Your school's fundamental purpose
- Ground in the present
- Answers: "Why do we exist? What's our core work?"
- Timeframe: Ongoing
- Example: "We develop thoughtful leaders and engaged citizens through excellence in academics, character, and service"

## Who Should Create Them?

**Include**
- Principal and leadership team
- Board representative
- Teachers from multiple grades and subjects
- Parent representatives
- Student representatives (especially secondary)
- Community leader if relevant

**Don't**
- Create them alone and impose them
- Make them so large that no one can influence them
- Include so many voices that it becomes bland

## Process for Creating Vision and Mission

**Step 1: Inspire (1-2 weeks)**
- Read mission/vision statements from other schools
- Read research on excellent schools
- Reflect on why you became an educator
- Share inspirational stories
- Get staff thinking about possibilities

**Step 2: Assess Current Reality (2-3 weeks)**
- What's working now?
- What are we proud of?
- What frustrates us?
- What do we wish were different?
- What do students/families need from us?

**Step 3: Brainstorm (2-3 weeks)**
- What could we be?
- What unique contribution could we make?
- If we were wildly successful, what would that look like?
- Generate lots of ideas without critique
- Look for patterns and themes

**Step 4: Draft (1-2 weeks)**
- Synthesize ideas into draft statements
- Leadership team writes initial versions
- Keep them short and memorable
- Make them inspirational but achievable
- Ensure they reflect stakeholder input

**Step 5: Test and Refine (1-2 weeks)**
- Share drafts with broader school community
- Gather feedback
- Test: Do these capture what we're about?
- Refine based on feedback
- Repeat if needed

**Step 6: Adopt and Communicate (1 week)**
- Board formally approves
- Celebrate adoption
- Commit to living them
- Start using them in school communications

## Characteristics of Strong Statements

**Clear and Specific**
- Good: "We develop graduates who are college-ready and civically engaged"
- Weak: "We provide excellent education"

**Inspirational**
- Good: "Every student discovers their unique gifts and learns to contribute to the world"
- Weak: "We follow the national curriculum"

**Memorable**
- Short enough to remember
- Poetic enough to inspire
- Concrete enough to guide decisions
- Good: One sentence for mission, 1-3 sentences for vision

**Achievable but Ambitious**
- Good: Stretch beyond current reality but seem possible with effort
- Weak: Either boring and easy, or so pie-in-the-sky that no one believes it

**Connected to Core Purpose**
- Good: Reflects educational philosophy and values
- Weak: Could describe any business

**Inclusive and Affirming**
- Good: All stakeholders see themselves reflected
- Weak: Implies some students or families are more welcome than others

## Vision and Mission Examples

**Example 1: Academic Focus**
- Vision: "A school where rigorous academics and intellectual curiosity drive each student toward their highest potential"
- Mission: "We prepare students for success in college and beyond through challenging curriculum and personalized instruction"

**Example 2: Character and Citizenship Focus**
- Vision: "A community of learners who think deeply, act ethically, and contribute meaningfully to the world"
- Mission: "We develop thoughtful, ethical citizens through academics grounded in real-world impact"

**Example 3: Student-Centered Focus**
- Vision: "Each student discovering their passion, developing their voice, and preparing for purposeful adulthood"
- Mission: "We create a personalized learning environment where every student is known and challenged to grow"

## Using Vision and Mission

Once adopted, use them:
- Start faculty meetings with mission reference
- Include in school communications
- Use in hiring decisions
- Guide curriculum choices
- Frame discipline approach
- Reference in strategic planning
- Include in parent handbook
- Celebrate progress toward vision
- Use in admission conversations

## When to Revisit

- Every 5-10 years for full review
- Annually for relevance check
- Immediately if major environmental change occurs
- When considering major new direction

Your vision and mission should inspire you when you're tired, guide you when you're confused, and remind everyone why you're doing this demanding work.
