# How to Build Psychological Safety on Staff

Psychological safety—the belief that you can take interpersonal risks without fear—is foundational to high-performing schools. Without it, people hide problems, avoid innovation, and disengage.

## What Is Psychological Safety?

**The Definition**
- Shared belief that it's safe to take interpersonal risks
- Can speak up without fear of punishment or humiliation
- Can ask questions without being considered ignorant
- Can admit mistakes without consequences
- Can challenge ideas without being seen as disloyal
- Can be yourself without fear of rejection

**What It's NOT**
- Niceness or lack of accountability
- Avoiding all conflict or challenge
- Everyone agrees all the time
- No criticism or feedback
- Permissiveness or lack of standards

## Why Psychological Safety Matters

**For Learning**
- People take intellectual risks
- Experiment with new approaches
- Share ideas and feedback
- Learn from mistakes
- Innovation happens
- Continuous improvement

**For Performance**
- People speak up about problems
- Issues surface early
- Quality improves
- Mistakes get caught sooner
- Collaboration increases
- Collective intelligence accessed

**For Culture**
- People feel valued and respected
- Sense of belonging
- Relationships deepen
- Engagement increases
- Retention improves
- Community strengthens

**For Results**
- Better decisions (more input)
- Better execution (more commitment)
- Better innovation (more risk-taking)
- Better outcomes (better collaboration)
- Better sustainability (people stay)

## Building Psychological Safety

**1. Invite Input and Really Listen**
- Ask for ideas and feedback
- Listen without defensive interrupting
- Acknowledge good points
- Thank people for speaking up
- Act on input when appropriate
- Show that input matters
- Regular solicitation of views

**2. Respond Well to Mistakes**
- Don't punish mistakes
- Respond with curiosity: "What happened?"
- Treat as learning opportunity
- Ask what they learned
- Fix the problem together
- Help prevent future mistakes
- Model fallibility yourself

**3. Admit Your Own Mistakes**
- Apologize when wrong
- Don't defend or make excuses
- Take responsibility
- Show what you're learning
- Fix what you can
- Move forward
- Vulnerability builds trust

**4. Be Transparent**
- Share what you can
- Explain decisions
- Admit what you don't know
- Share reasoning
- Acknowledge constraints
- Honest communication
- No hidden agendas or secrets (when appropriate to share)

**5. Acknowledge Limits of Knowledge**
- You don't know everything
- Ask for expertise and help
- Collaborate on problems
- Use staff knowledge
- Defer to their expertise
- Learn from them
- Show humility

**6. Give Challenging Feedback**
- Feedback is sign of belief in people
- Can improve and should improve
- Specific and kind
- Focus on behavior, not character
- Forward-looking (how to improve)
- Private (when appropriate)
- Shows you care about growth

**7. Hold People Accountable**
- Clear expectations
- Consistent consequences
- Applied fairly
- Done respectfully
- Focused on behavior and results
- Growth mindset
- High standards with support

**8. Build Inclusive Culture**
- Everyone belongs
- Diverse perspectives valued
- Different backgrounds welcome
- Multiple ways to contribute
- Accessible participation
- Respected and included
- Sense of community

## Behaviors That Destroy Psychological Safety

**Avoid These**
- Humiliating people publicly
- Punishing questions or feedback
- Dismissing ideas without consideration
- Scapegoating or blaming
- Retaliating against dissent
- Making fun of people
- Showing favorites or bias
- Unpredictable anger
- Defensive when challenged
- Saying one thing, doing another

## Creating Structures for Safety

**Confidential Feedback Channels**
- Anonymous survey or suggestion box
- Third-party feedback mechanism
- Safe way to voice concerns
- Addressed seriously
- No retaliation
- Action on feedback
- Communication of changes

**Regular Check-Ins**
- One-on-one meetings
- Safe space to talk
- Confidentiality respected
- Open-ended questions
- Listen fully
- Address concerns
- Follow up promised

**Staff Forums**
- Regular meetings for input
- Group problem-solving
- Collective decision-making
- Respectful dialogue
- Different viewpoints honored
- Decisions explained
- Implementation feedback

**Peer Support**
- Colleague to talk to
- Trusted advisor
- Not reporting to boss
- Confidential
- Emotional support
- Sounding board
- Community of support

**Exit Interviews**
- For people leaving
- Understand why
- What could be better
- Honest feedback
- Non-defensive listening
- Learning for improvement
- Changes in response

## Addressing Safety Violations

**When Someone Violates Safety**
- Address directly
- Explain impact
- Clarify expectations
- Support change
- Consistency
- Don't let it slide
- Model holding accountable

**When Someone Tests Boundaries**
- Respond quickly
- Clear boundaries
- Consistent expectations
- Pattern of behavior addressed
- More serious consequences if needed
- Still respectful
- No public humiliation

**When Leaders Create Unsafety**
- Address with leader
- Specific examples
- Impact on culture
- Coaching and feedback
- Accountability for change
- If not changing: escalate
- Sometimes change in leadership needed

## Psychological Safety in Meetings

**Create Safe Meeting Norms**
- Phones away (everyone)
- Everyone's voice heard
- Questions and disagreement welcome
- Confidentiality respected (as appropriate)
- No side conversations
- Respect for different views
- Time for processing

**Techniques for Input**
- Small group discussion first
- Silent reflection time
- Round-robin speaking
- Written input option
- Anonymous suggestions
- Multiple ways to participate
- Inclusive of different styles

**Manage Dominant Voices**
- Politely redirect if someone dominates
- "Let's hear from others"
- Protect space for quiet people
- Build in reflection time
- Written input option
- Small group discussion
- No one person takes all air

## Psychological Safety and Performance Management

**Balancing Safety and Accountability**

**You Can Have Both**
- Clear expectations (accountability)
- Support to meet them (safety)
- Fair evaluation (accountability)
- Opportunity to improve (safety)
- Consequences for not improving (accountability)
- Respectful process (safety)
- High standards with belief in people

**The Message**
- "I have high expectations for you"
- "I believe you can meet them"
- "I will support you"
- "I will give you honest feedback"
- "Mistakes are learning"
- "You matter and your growth matters"
- "We're in this together"

## Measuring Psychological Safety

**Surveys**
- Regular climate surveys
- Question about safety
- Can speak up without fear
- Ideas are valued
- Mistakes treated as learning
- Supported and respected
- Trend over time

**Observations**
- Do people ask questions in meetings?
- Do people challenge ideas?
- Do people admit mistakes?
- Are meetings lively with input?
- Do people seem engaged?
- Do quieter people participate?
- Are there side conversations vs. engagement?

**Conversations**
- In one-on-ones: How safe do people feel?
- Can they speak freely?
- Do they hold back ideas?
- What would make them feel safer?
- Trust in leadership?
- Sense of belonging?
- Fear of consequences?

## Building Safety Takes Time

**Start Where You Are**
- If safety is low, building takes time
- Consistency matters more than speed
- Small wins build momentum
- Be patient with process
- Keep reinforcing
- Model constantly
- Don't give up

**Your Role Is Central**
- You set tone
- Your behavior matters most
- Your vulnerability enables others
- Your consistency builds trust
- Your responsiveness to feedback
- Your follow-through on promises
- Your genuine care

Psychological safety is foundational to school excellence. When people feel safe, they do their best work, contribute their best thinking, and commit most deeply. Build it intentionally and protect it fiercely.
