# Dealing with Difficult Teachers

Every school has at least one teacher who is talented but challenging to work with. Managing these relationships while maintaining school culture is one of your toughest leadership tasks.

## Types of Difficult Teachers

**The Brilliant But Arrogant**
- High academic credentials and strong results
- Dismissive of other approaches
- Creates tension with colleagues
- May not follow school protocols

**The Chronic Complainer**
- Always finds problems with school decisions
- Focuses on what's wrong, not what works
- Drains energy from meetings
- Influences other staff negatively

**The Boundary-Pusher**
- Tests limits on policies and procedures
- Questions authority publicly
- May be challenging school culture
- Forces you to clarify expectations

**The Checked-Out Teacher**
- Does minimum required work
- No longer engaged in teaching
- Affects student learning
- May be burned out or unhappy

**The Conflict Creator**
- Constantly in conflict with colleagues
- Creates drama in staff room
- Involves students in interpersonal issues
- Makes school feel divided

## Your Approach

**Start with Understanding**
- What's driving this behavior?
- Is this a skill problem or a will problem?
- Are they struggling with something personal?
- Have they always been this way?

**Distinguish Between Difficult and Harmful**
- Difficult: Hard to work with but not damaging
- Harmful: Affecting student learning or safety
- Your response differs based on this distinction

## Working with Difficult But Effective Teachers

**Set Clear Expectations**
- Specify exactly what behavior you need to change
- Explain how their behavior impacts others
- Make expectations non-negotiable
- Document the conversation

**Give Them a Valued Role**
- Channel their talents productively
- Give them leadership opportunities
- Recognize their contributions
- Help them feel invested in school success

**Create Boundaries**
- Limit their negative influence
- Don't let them control meetings or decisions
- Protect other staff from excessive negativity
- Remain professional but firm

**Regular Check-ins**
- Monitor behavior and attitude
- Provide feedback on improvements
- Address any backsliding quickly
- Show that you're paying attention

## Working with Underperforming Teachers

**Clarify Performance Gaps**
- Be specific about what's not working
- Use data (student learning, observation notes)
- Distinguish between poor performance and different style

**Develop a Support Plan**
- Provide coaching or mentoring
- Offer professional development
- Pair with a strong colleague
- Give specific timeline for improvement

**Monitor Progress**
- Regular observations
- Student feedback on learning
- Comparison to school standards
- Honest assessment of change

**Make Hard Decisions**
- If genuine effort shows no improvement, consider termination
- If they choose not to improve, follow formal processes
- Sometimes moving someone is better than forcing them out
- Be clear that improvement is necessary, not optional

## When Their Behavior Affects Students or School

**Performance Management Process**
- Document concerns thoroughly
- Provide written notice of expectations
- Give reasonable time to improve
- Follow all formal procedures

**Involve HR/Administration**
- Don't handle serious issues alone
- Get professional guidance on legal requirements
- Protect yourself and the school
- Ensure fairness throughout process

**Communication with Staff**
- Don't broadcast details
- Be clear about standards and expectations
- Show that you're taking action
- Maintain confidentiality of private matters

## Difficult Conversations

**When They're Defensive**
- Stay focused on specific behaviors and impact
- Don't get pulled into arguments
- Remain calm and professional
- Document what was discussed

**When They Deny Problems**
- Provide specific examples
- Reference student feedback or data
- Don't argue about perception
- State clearly what needs to change

**When They Threaten or Escalate**
- Stay professional
- End the conversation if needed
- Involve administration or HR
- Document everything

## Your Own Wellbeing

Working with difficult teachers can be draining:
- Remember you can't change people, only set expectations
- Some people will choose to leave if held to higher standards
- Your job is school culture, not individual satisfaction
- Seek support from mentors or coaches
- Don't take difficult behavior personally

## The Bigger Picture

Sometimes losing a difficult teacher—even a talented one—improves overall school culture. The cost of constant conflict often exceeds the cost of finding a replacement. Your responsibility is to the whole school community, not to individual preferences.

However, not all difficult people are problems. Some teachers who challenge you are pushing you to be better. Learn to distinguish between helpful challenge and harmful difficulty. The goal is creating a school where talented people want to work and where standards are clear and fair.
