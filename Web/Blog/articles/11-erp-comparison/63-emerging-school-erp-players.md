## Evaluating New School ERP Vendors: How to Assess Emerging Players

**Newer ERP vendors, companies that have been in the school ERP market for fewer than 5 years, can offer genuine advantages: modern architecture built with current compliance requirements from the start, better mobile experience, and responsive support from teams that still care about individual customer success. They also carry risks. Evaluating new vendors requires a different approach than evaluating established ones.**

---

## Advantages Newer Vendors Sometimes Offer

### Built for Current Requirements

An ERP built in 2022-2024 is designed with DPDP Act awareness, NEP 2020 requirements, and mobile-first use as foundational assumptions. ERPs built in 2010-2015 have these requirements bolted on, often less elegantly.

### Modern Technical Architecture

Newer products often use modern cloud infrastructure (Firebase, AWS, Azure) that provides reliability, automatic scaling, and built-in security features. Legacy on-premise systems require significant engineering to migrate to cloud equivalents.

### Responsive Support at Early Stages

At 50 or 100 customer schools, a newer vendor's team knows each customer personally. Support calls get answered by people who understand your specific configuration. This personal service is often better than what large vendors provide to their long-tail customers.

### Willing to Customize

Newer vendors are often more willing to implement specific requirements (a state board report card format, a specific compliance report) that established vendors have deprioritized.

---

## Risks Newer Vendors Carry

### Limited Track Record

A vendor with 50 school customers has less evidence of reliability, support quality, and product stability than one with 500 customers. The risk of undiscovered critical bugs is higher.

### Financial Vulnerability

Earlier-stage companies may depend on venture funding or have thinner margins. If they face financial difficulty, school operations are at risk.

### Incomplete Feature Set

Newer vendors often have excellent core modules (attendance, fees) but may not yet have transport, hostel, payroll, or analytics at the maturity of established vendors. Evaluate against your specific feature requirements, not the vendor's roadmap.

### Regulatory Gap Risk

A vendor that has not yet received a DPDP Act compliance audit may have architectural gaps that become apparent only when enforcement begins.

## How to Evaluate a Newer Vendor

### Verify the Technical Foundation

Ask technical questions directly:
- "Is your application cloud-native or hosted on a single server?"
- "How is student data isolated between schools?"
- "What is your uptime SLA? Can you share historical uptime data?"
- "How do you handle DPDP Act compliance, consent, access logs, encryption?"

A vendor with solid technical foundations can answer these specifically. A vendor that cannot has gaps.

### Talk to Reference Schools

Request 3-5 reference schools that have been using the system for at least 1 year. Ask specifically:
- "Were there any significant outages or data problems?"
- "How quickly does support respond when you have a critical issue?"
- "What features are missing that you wish the vendor had built?"

### Review the Contract Carefully

New vendors may have less standard contracts. Look for:
- Data export rights (what happens to your data if you leave?)
- SLA commitments (uptime, support response times)
- Termination terms (can you leave without penalty if the product fails to deliver?)

### Pilot Before Full Commitment

If possible, pilot with a single campus or a limited module before full school-wide commitment. A 3-month pilot reveals real-world issues that demo environments hide.

## FAQ

**Q: Should we always prefer an established vendor over a newer one?**
A: Not necessarily. The established vendor may have legacy technical debt and declining support quality. Evaluate each vendor on current capabilities, not historical reputation alone.

**Q: How many school customers is "enough" for a new vendor?**
A: 50+ paying schools with 1+ year of operation is a reasonable baseline. Fewer customers means less real-world validation.

**Q: What if a new vendor offers significantly lower pricing?**
A: Very low pricing from a new vendor can signal financial unsustainability. Ask how the vendor is funded and how they plan to maintain the product. Unsustainably low pricing often means services will either degrade or prices will spike at renewal.

**Q: Is newer technology always better?**
A: More recently built ≠ better. Evaluate the specific implementation. Some new vendors use modern frameworks but implement them poorly. Some established vendors have strong architectural foundations despite age.
