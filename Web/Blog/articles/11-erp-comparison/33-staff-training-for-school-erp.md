## Staff Training for School ERP: How to Do It Right

**The most common reason school ERP implementations fail is not poor software, it is poor training. Staff who do not understand the system avoid it. Teachers mark attendance in notebooks because the app seems complicated. Accounts staff use Excel because they never learned the fee module. Training is not a nice-to-have; it determines whether the investment succeeds.**

---

## Why Generic Training Fails

The biggest training mistake: one session for all staff. A 4-hour session that covers "the entire ERP" for the Principal, three HODs, twenty teachers, the accounts team, and the admin staff simultaneously teaches no one effectively.

Each role uses different parts of the system. A subject teacher needs to know attendance marking and marks entry, nothing else initially. The accounts team needs to know the fee module in detail and nothing else. Giving everyone the same training means everyone is overwhelmed by features they will never use.

**Role-based training is the only approach that works.**

---

## Training Plan by Role

### Principal and Vice Principal (2-3 hours)
**What they need to learn:**
- Dashboard: reading KPIs, understanding at-risk alerts
- Approval workflows: leave requests, fee concessions, compliance actions
- Compliance calendar: understanding deadlines and status
- Reports: running and interpreting standard school reports
- How to delegate and what each role sees in the system

**Format:** One-on-one or small group with senior admin. Focus on decision-making workflows.

---

### HODs and Academic Coordinators (2-3 hours)
**What they need to learn:**
- Department view: teacher attendance, lesson plan submission status
- Marks approval workflow: reviewing and approving subject teacher entries
- Curriculum coverage: tracking which topics are behind schedule
- Student performance reports: section averages, at-risk students in their department

**Format:** Small group per department. Use the HOD's actual department data during training.

---

### Class Teachers (2-3 hours)
**What they need to learn:**
- Daily attendance: marking present/absent/late/leave for their section
- Student profiles: where to find information about a student
- Homework assignment: publishing homework for their class
- Report card remarks: how to enter and submit class teacher remarks
- Parent communication: publishing class-level announcements

**Format:** Classroom of class teachers together. Practice with real student list from their section.

---

### Subject Teachers (1-2 hours)
**What they need to learn:**
- Period-wise attendance: marking subject attendance for their classes
- Marks entry: entering assessment marks for their subject
- Homework assignment: publishing subject homework
- Lesson plan submission

**Format:** Practice session with the teacher's own class roster and subject. Short and focused, subject teachers have the narrowest use of the system.

---

### Accounts Team (3-4 hours)
**What they need to learn:**
- Fee structure review: understanding how fees are configured
- Payment recording: entering cash/cheque payments, generating receipts
- Defaulter report: running and exporting defaulter lists
- Concession workflow: submitting concession requests for approval
- Fee reports: monthly collection reports, year-to-date status

**Format:** Dedicated session with the accounts team. Practice with real fee scenarios.

---

### Admin Team (2-3 hours)
**What they need to learn:**
- Student enrollment: adding new students, editing records
- Circulars: publishing announcements with audience targeting
- Visitor management: check-in/check-out, visitor log
- Transfer certificates: initiating and tracking TC workflows

**Format:** Admin coordinator leads, vendor trainer supports.

## Sustaining Adoption After Launch

Training does not end at go-live. Sustain adoption with:

**Quick reference cards:** One-page role-specific cards showing the 5 most common tasks with screenshots. Laminate and keep at desks.

**Video recordings:** Record training sessions. New staff joining mid-year can watch the recording relevant to their role.

**Champion designation:** Identify one "ERP champion" per department, a teacher who is comfortable with the system and can answer colleague questions. This reduces dependency on the vendor.

**Monthly check-in for first 3 months:** A 30-minute session covering common issues, questions, and new features. Keeps adoption momentum.

**Mandatory first-week tasks:** After training, assign mandatory tasks for the first week (mark attendance for 3 days, enter one assessment score). Completion creates habit.

## What to Ask Vendors About Training

1. "Is training role-based or one-size-fits-all?"
2. "Do you provide training recordings for staff who could not attend?"
3. "What is included in the standard training package? What costs extra?"
4. "Do you train our 'champion' staff to handle first-level questions?"
5. "What ongoing training is available for new staff joining after go-live?"

## FAQ

**Q: How long does staff training typically take?**
A: Plan 3-5 total days across all role groups. The accounts team and class teachers need the most time; subject teachers need the least.

**Q: What if key staff members miss the training sessions?**
A: Training recordings and one-on-one catch-up sessions are important. A missed HOD can block their department's adoption if they do not understand the system.

**Q: Should we train parents separately?**
A: Parents need a simple onboarding, a one-page guide or a 5-minute video explaining how to log in, check attendance, and pay fees. This is different from staff training and much shorter.

**Q: What if a teacher decides they do not want to use the system?**
A: This is a management issue, not a training issue. The Principal must set a clear expectation that the ERP is the official system, not optional. Identify the specific concern (too slow, confusing interface) and address it, but do not let one resistant teacher undermine school-wide adoption.

**Q: How do we measure training effectiveness?**
A: Track usage metrics, how many teachers marked digital attendance in week 1 vs. week 4. Improving usage rates over the first month indicate effective training. Stagnant or declining usage indicate a problem that needs attention.
