## School ERP Training Program: Best Practices That Drive Adoption

**Training quality directly determines adoption quality. A well-designed training program produces staff who can complete their ERP tasks independently within 2 weeks. A poor training program produces staff who are confused, overwhelmed, and revert to old habits. The design of the training program — not just its existence — is what creates adoption.**

---

## Best Practice 1: Role-Based Sessions

The most common training mistake is "one session for all staff." A 3-hour session covering the full ERP for teachers, accounts staff, admin, and the principal simultaneously teaches no one effectively. Each role spends 70% of the session watching features irrelevant to their work.

**Better approach:** Separate sessions per role group:
- Principal and VP (1 session, 2 hours): dashboard, approvals, compliance calendar, reports
- HODs (1 session, 2 hours): department view, marks approval, at-risk student list
- Class teachers (1 session, 3 hours): attendance, student profiles, homework, report card remarks
- Subject teachers (1 session, 2 hours): marks entry, homework, period attendance
- Accounts team (1 session, 3 hours): fee management, receipts, defaulter reports, concessions
- Admin team (1 session, 2 hours): enrollment, circulars, visitor management, TC

Each session covers only what that role actually uses. No one wastes time on irrelevant modules.

---

## Best Practice 2: Use Real Data, Not Demo Data

Train class teachers with their actual class list, not a generic demo list of 10 students. Train the accounts team with the real fee structure, not a simplified example.

Real data:
- Shows staff that the system already knows their context
- Identifies configuration issues before go-live (if the class list is wrong, it gets fixed during training)
- Builds confidence because staff recognize the information

Demo data creates confusion when real usage looks different from training.

---

## Best Practice 3: Hands-On Practice, Not Lecture

Training that involves watching a trainer click through the system produces passive learning. After the session, staff cannot reproduce what they saw.

**Better format:**
- Trainer demonstrates a task (2 minutes)
- Each participant completes the same task on their own device (3 minutes)
- Questions raised and resolved immediately
- Repeat for next task

For a class teacher's 3-hour session: demonstrate marking attendance, all teachers mark attendance for a test section. Demonstrate entering marks, all teachers enter marks for a sample assessment. Demonstrate publishing homework, all teachers publish a homework entry.

Hands-on completion during the session = competence entering the session.

---

## Best Practice 4: Provide Quick Reference Cards

After training, each role receives a laminated one-page reference card:
- Class Teacher card: 5 most common tasks with screenshots and step counts
- Accounts team card: fee receipt process, defaulter report generation, payment recording
- Admin card: new enrollment, circular publishing, TC initiation

Reference cards reduce dependency on re-calling everything from memory. They sit on desks. Staff consult them in the first 2 weeks while habits form.

---

## Best Practice 5: Record Training for New Joiners

Staff leave mid-year. New staff join. Without recorded training, new joiners either:
- Wait for the next scheduled training (losing productive time)
- Learn from colleagues (inconsistently)
- Learn by trial and error (slow and error-prone)

Record each role's training session. Store in a shared school drive. When a new accounts team member joins in October, they watch the accounts training recording on Day 1.

Recording quality does not need to be professional. A Zoom recording works. The content matters more than production value.

---

## Best Practice 6: Follow-Up Check-In at Week 2

Two weeks after training, the ERP coordinator schedules 15-minute check-ins (or a group session per department) to address:
- Questions that came up during real use
- Tasks staff are still unsure about
- Configuration issues discovered during real usage

Week 2 questions are more specific and actionable than Week 0 questions. Staff now know what they do not know.

---

## Measuring Training Effectiveness

Track by role:
- **Class teachers:** Percentage marking attendance digitally by Week 1, Week 2, Week 4
- **Subject teachers:** Percentage entering marks within expected timeframe after first assessment
- **Accounts team:** Payment recording accuracy (comparison with old ledger)

Below-target performance indicates a training gap. Identify which specific task is failing and run a targeted 30-minute refresher session — not a repeat of the full training.

## FAQ

**Q: How much total training time should we budget?**
A: 10-15 hours of vendor-led training time across all role groups for a 500-student school. Add 5-10 hours of internal practice time in the first 2 weeks. Small schools: proportionally less.

**Q: Should we train everyone before go-live or train in phases?**
A: Phase training to match the rollout schedule. Class teachers need attendance training before go-live. Accounts team needs fee training before the first installment due date. Subject teachers need marks training before the first assessment.

**Q: What if the vendor's training is poor quality?**
A: Flag this to the vendor and request role-specific sessions if they provided a generic one-size-fits-all session. If vendor training cannot be improved, supplement with a school-run practice session using the quick reference cards.

**Q: How do we handle staff with very low tech literacy?**
A: One-on-one sessions with these staff members, focusing only on their 2-3 essential tasks. Assign a peer champion to support them for the first 2 weeks. Do not expect them to attend the full role-group session and absorb all of it.

**Q: Does training quality differ between vendors?**
A: Significantly. Vendors who have implemented in many schools have refined their training approach. Newer vendors may provide technically accurate but pedagogically poor training. Ask for a sample training session during the vendor evaluation process.
