## School ERP Total Cost of Ownership: A 3-Year Calculation Framework

**Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) is the complete cost of an ERP over its useful life, not just the subscription price. For school ERP decisions, a 3-year TCO comparison is typically the most useful horizon: long enough to capture ongoing costs and benefits, short enough to remain realistic. This framework helps compare vendors on a complete cost basis.**

---

## Why Subscription Price Alone Misleads

Two vendors:
- Vendor A: ₹200/student/year subscription (per 500 students = ₹1,00,000/year)
- Vendor B: ₹350/student/year subscription (per 500 students = ₹1,75,000/year)

Vendor A appears 43% cheaper. But:
- Vendor A has ₹50,000 implementation fee
- Vendor A charges ₹0.25/SMS for notifications (₹15,000/year for typical usage)
- Vendor A's transport and payroll modules are extra (₹30,000/year combined)
- Vendor A's support is email-only; phone support is ₹25,000/year extra
- Year 2 price increases to ₹250/student/year; Year 3 to ₹300/student/year

3-Year Vendor A TCO:
Year 1: ₹1,00,000 + ₹50,000 + ₹15,000 + ₹30,000 + ₹25,000 = ₹2,20,000
Year 2: ₹1,25,000 + ₹15,000 + ₹30,000 + ₹25,000 = ₹1,95,000
Year 3: ₹1,50,000 + ₹15,000 + ₹30,000 + ₹25,000 = ₹2,20,000
**3-Year Total: ₹6,35,000**

Vendor B (all-inclusive, stable pricing):
Year 1: ₹1,75,000
Year 2: ₹1,75,000
Year 3: ₹1,75,000
**3-Year Total: ₹5,25,000**

Vendor B, which appeared 43% more expensive per year at Year 1, is actually 17% cheaper over 3 years.

## What TCO Does Not Include (But Should Consider)

**Internal staff time for implementation:** Your admin coordinator spending 2 weeks on data migration and configuration is a real cost (opportunity cost). Include it if comparing vendors with significantly different implementation burdens.

**Ongoing staff time for system maintenance:** Some ERPs require ongoing configuration work as the school year evolves. Include the estimated time your staff will spend on system administration annually.

**Switching costs:** After 3 years, switching to a different vendor costs money (implementation, migration, training again). This switching cost makes Year 4 and beyond costs asymmetrically in favor of staying, factor this when evaluating vendors.

---

## How to Get TCO Data from Vendors

Ask each vendor for:
1. Complete pricing sheet (all modules, support tiers, add-ons)
2. SMS pricing and typical school usage estimate
3. Storage limits and overage pricing
4. Year 2 and Year 3 pricing (explicitly)
5. Any one-time fees (implementation, setup, integration)

Build the TCO matrix with real numbers from each vendor before making the comparison.

## FAQ

**Q: Is a 3-year TCO the right horizon?**
A: For most schools, yes. Short enough that pricing is somewhat predictable. Long enough that subscription escalation and Year 2+ costs become visible.

**Q: What if we plan to switch ERP after 2 years?**
A: Use a 2-year TCO instead. Also include an estimate of switching cost (typically one additional implementation + migration). Total cost of switching then factors into whether staying vs. moving makes financial sense.

**Q: How do we account for inflation in Year 2 and 3 estimates?**
A: If the vendor has a price escalation clause, use the maximum escalation rate for your estimate. If locked in, use the contract price.

**Q: Does TCO apply differently for cloud vs. on-premise ERP?**
A: Significantly. On-premise has high Year 1 costs (servers, software licenses, installation) and lower Year 2-3 costs (maintenance). Cloud has lower Year 1 and consistent Year 2-3 costs. For most schools, cloud TCO is lower across 3-5 years once server and maintenance costs are included for on-premise.

**Q: Should we negotiate based on TCO rather than Year 1 price?**
A: Yes. Vendors who understand you are evaluating 3-year TCO are more likely to offer stable multi-year pricing or waive one-time fees to win the contract.
