## How to Evaluate School ERP Vendor Reputation

**School ERP vendors market themselves with case studies, testimonials, and impressive feature lists. Evaluating actual reputation — what schools genuinely experience over 2-3 years — requires going beyond vendor-provided materials. This guide covers practical due diligence methods that reveal what vendors do not show you in a sales presentation.**

---

## Why Vendor Reputation Matters More Than Product Features

Two vendors might offer similar features. The difference is whether those features work reliably, whether implementation was smooth, whether support is responsive when something breaks, and whether pricing remained fair at renewal.

A technically capable product with poor support can become a liability. A slightly narrower product with excellent support and fair practices can outperform the "better" product over a 3-year relationship.

Reputation evaluation covers the full lifecycle, not just the sales and onboarding phase.

---

## Method 1: Reference Schools — The Right Way

Most vendors provide references. Most references are cherry-picked success stories. To get useful information:

**Ask the vendor for schools that are 18+ months in, not just new customers.** Early-stage schools are in the honeymoon period. The support quality and pricing renewal behavior are only visible after 18 months.

**Ask for reference schools in your school type.** A CBSE chain school and a small private Montessori have different needs. Ask for schools comparable to yours.

**Call, do not email.** School principals and administrators will give you candid feedback on a phone call that they would not commit to in writing.

**Questions to ask reference schools:**
- How did the implementation actually go vs. what was promised?
- What happened the first time something went seriously wrong?
- How long did support responses typically take?
- Did pricing change at renewal? By how much?
- Would you sign the same contract again?

---

## Method 2: Online Reviews and Communities

Search for the vendor's name in school administrator forums, WhatsApp groups, and school management communities (Indian school networks are active on both). The schools who share there are not cherry-picked by the vendor.

Specific searches:
- "[Vendor name] school ERP review India"
- "[Vendor name] ERP problem issues"
- "[Vendor name] school management support"

Pay attention to patterns. A single negative review may be an outlier. Multiple reviews mentioning the same issue (slow support, billing disputes, data migration problems) indicate a systematic problem.

---

## Method 3: Evaluate the Vendor's Market Presence

**How long have they been operating?**
A vendor with 5+ years in the Indian school ERP market has navigated multiple regulatory changes (GST, NEP 2020, CBSE LOC updates, DPDP Act). Their product has been hardened by real-world problems.

**How many schools are they actively serving?**
Ask for a current customer count, not a cumulative "total schools served" number. A vendor with 500 current active schools has ongoing product and support obligations that incentivize quality.

**Do they have visible public activity?**
Vendors who publish product updates, regulatory guidance, and are active in the school administration community are more likely to be engaged partners. A vendor that went dark on their blog 2 years ago may have stopped investing in the product.

---

## Method 4: Examine Their DPDP and Compliance Posture

Ask the vendor directly: "How does your product handle DPDP Act 2023 consent requirements?"

A vendor with a strong compliance posture will describe:
- Parental consent collection workflow
- Consent withdrawal mechanism
- Data minimization (not collecting more than necessary)
- Data localization (data stored in India for Indian schools)

A vendor who is unfamiliar with DPDP or dismisses it is putting your school at regulatory risk. The DPDP Act 2023 applies now; schools are responsible for compliance.

---

## Red Flags in Vendor Reputation Evaluation

- References are all new customers (under 6 months)
- No willingness to provide any reference that was not pre-selected
- Vague support SLAs without numbers ("we're very responsive")
- Inability to explain data export rights under termination
- Multiple different answers from different vendor staff about pricing
- Active schools count significantly lower than "total schools served"
- No product update activity visible in the last year

## FAQ

**Q: What if the vendor is new and doesn't have many reference schools?**
A: Newer vendors present higher risk precisely because the track record is short. Evaluate the team's experience, request a longer free trial period, and negotiate contract protections (shorter initial commitment, data export rights, performance milestones).

**Q: Can we trust review sites for vendor research?**
A: Treat review sites as one input, not the primary input. Some reviews are incentivized. The pattern across multiple sources is more reliable than any single review.

**Q: How do we evaluate a vendor's financial stability?**
A: Ask about investor backing or profitability. A bootstrapped vendor with a stable customer base can be as stable as a VC-backed one. The risk is a vendor burning through funding with no path to profitability.

**Q: Is it worth paying a premium for a more reputable vendor?**
A: Often yes. An extra 20% in annual cost for a vendor with documented reliability, good support, and fair renewal practices is less expensive than the hidden cost of a troubled implementation, poor support, or mid-year vendor failure.

**Q: Should we use the same vendor other schools in our area use?**
A: It is useful information — you can ask those schools directly for their experience. But local adoption is not a substitute for due diligence. A locally popular vendor might have consolidated market share not because they are better, but because they entered the market first.
