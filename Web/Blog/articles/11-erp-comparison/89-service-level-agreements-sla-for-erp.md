## Service Level Agreements for School ERP: What Schools Should Require

**A Service Level Agreement (SLA) defines what the vendor commits to provide, uptime, support response times, resolution times, and what remedies apply if those commitments are not met. Most standard school ERP contracts contain minimal SLAs or none at all. Schools that negotiate SLAs into their contracts have a basis for holding vendors accountable. Schools that do not have no recourse when support is slow or the system is down during exams.**

---

## SLA Component 1: System Uptime

**What it means:** The percentage of time the ERP is accessible and functioning. 99.9% uptime means the system can be unavailable for a maximum of 8.76 hours per year.

**Benchmarks:**
- 99.9% uptime is a reasonable minimum for cloud-based school ERP
- 99.5% uptime allows up to 43.8 hours of downtime per year, acceptable only if downtime is scheduled during non-school hours
- 99.0% uptime (87.6 hours per year) is below acceptable for a production system

**What to look for in the SLA:**
- Uptime measured monthly (not annually), monthly SLA violations are more actionable
- Excluding scheduled maintenance windows (which should be during weekends and school holidays)
- Definition of what counts as "downtime" (inaccessibility vs. partial functionality)

**Red flag:** "We target 99% uptime" without a documented SLA means no commitment and no remedy.

---

## SLA Component 2: Support Response Times

**What it means:** How quickly the vendor acknowledges a support request after it is submitted.

**Realistic benchmarks for school ERP:**

| Issue Type | Definition | Target Response |
|---|---|---|
| Critical | System inaccessible, data loss, security breach | Within 2 hours |
| High | Major module not functioning (attendance, fees) | Within 8 hours |
| Medium | Significant function impaired (reports broken) | Within 24 hours |
| Low | Minor issue, configuration question | Within 48 hours |

**What to look for:**
- Response times defined by severity tier (not a single "we respond in 24 hours" for all issues)
- IST business hours definition (critical issues should have after-hours response)
- Escalation path if initial response does not resolve the issue

---

## SLA Component 3: Resolution Times

**What it means:** How long the vendor takes to actually fix the issue (not just acknowledge it).

**Benchmarks:**
- Critical issues: 8-hour resolution target
- High priority: 48-hour resolution target
- Medium priority: 5 business days
- Low priority: 10 business days

Note: Complex bugs may not be resolvable within these windows. The SLA should define a "workaround commitment" (providing a workaround within the target time even if the root fix takes longer) for complex issues.

---

## SLA Component 4: Maintenance Windows

**What it means:** When the vendor performs system maintenance (updates, upgrades, infrastructure work) that may cause downtime.

**What to require:**
- Scheduled maintenance only during defined windows (weekend nights, school holiday periods)
- Advance notice of at least 48 hours for scheduled maintenance
- Emergency maintenance allowed but must be communicated immediately
- Academic calendar consideration, no maintenance windows during board examination submission periods

---

## SLA Component 5: Remedies for SLA Breach

**What it means:** What the school receives if the vendor does not meet SLA commitments.

**Typical remedies:**
- Service credits toward next renewal period (e.g., 10% of monthly fee for each month the uptime SLA is breached)
- Right to terminate contract without penalty if SLA is breached for 3+ consecutive months

**What to avoid:** SLA with no remedy. A commitment without a consequence is not an enforceable commitment.

## FAQ

**Q: If the vendor's standard contract has no SLA, is it negotiable?**
A: Yes. SLA terms are among the most commonly negotiated additions to vendor contracts. Vendors with confidence in their product and support welcome documented commitments.

**Q: What if the vendor claims 99.99% uptime but won't put it in the contract?**
A: Verbal claims not in the contract are marketing, not commitments. Insist that any uptime claim the vendor makes verbally be reflected in the contract.

**Q: How do we verify if the vendor is meeting SLA commitments?**
A: Request a monthly service report from the vendor. Track your own support ticket open times. If tickets consistently take longer than the SLA, document and escalate.

**Q: Does SLA coverage matter more for some schools than others?**
A: Yes. Multi-campus chains, boarding schools (where the ERP runs welfare and roll call 24/7), and schools with heavy parent portal usage need more stringent SLAs than a single-campus day school with moderate ERP usage.

**Q: What is the most important SLA term for exam season?**
A: Zero unscheduled downtime during board examination LOC submission periods and examination date recording. Request an explicit blackout period (no maintenance, no updates) of at least 2 weeks before and during major exam submission deadlines.
