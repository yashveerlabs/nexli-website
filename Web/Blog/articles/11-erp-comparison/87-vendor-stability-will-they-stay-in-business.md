## School ERP Vendor Stability: Will They Still Be in Business When You Need Support?

**A school ERP is a long-term commitment. The vendor who sells you the system today needs to still be operating, investing in the product, and responsive to support requests in Year 3. Vendor instability, financial difficulty, acquisition, abandonment of the school ERP segment, is a real risk. Understanding how to evaluate it before signing is as important as evaluating the product itself.**

---

## Why Vendor Stability Is a School-Specific Risk

Unlike an individual consumer app, a school ERP holds critical institutional data: student records, fee history, attendance records, staff payroll, board examination data. If the vendor shuts down, this data does not automatically transfer elsewhere. Schools can be left without access to their own records and without ongoing support for a system that now runs their daily operations.

The risk is not hypothetical. Indian school ERP vendors have:
- Been acquired by larger companies and had their products deprecated
- Shifted focus from K-12 to other verticals (higher education, corporate)
- Run out of funding and reduced support to a skeleton staff
- Closed operations without adequate notice to schools

---

## Indicators of Vendor Stability

**Revenue diversity and customer count:** A vendor with 300+ active school customers has recurring revenue across a customer base that makes shutdown unlikely. A vendor with 30 customers is more vulnerable.

**Time in market:** 5+ years in the Indian school ERP market through multiple regulatory cycles (GST, NEP 2020, DPDP) indicates organizational durability. Surviving one bad year or one difficult regulatory change takes resilience.

**Product investment:** Is the vendor still releasing new features, compliance updates, and UI improvements? Product development requires investment. A vendor who stopped releasing meaningful updates 18+ months ago may be reducing investment in the product.

**Team stability:** Ask whether the founders and key technical staff are still in place. A vendor whose founding team has left and been replaced by an outsourced team is a different company, regardless of the brand name.

**Support responsiveness:** A vendor with financial stress will often reduce support staff first. If reference schools report support quality deterioration in the past 12 months, this may be a financial signal.

## What Happens When a Vendor Shuts Down

Without contractual protection, a vendor shutdown scenario:
1. You receive notice (if you are lucky) that the service is ending in 30-90 days
2. You need to immediately export all data (if the export tools still function)
3. You need to find and implement a replacement within that window
4. You absorb all migration costs, training costs, and disruption costs

With contractual protection:
1. Your contract includes data export rights with a defined 30-day export period
2. You receive a final data export in a documented standard format
3. You have a contract clause specifying vendor obligations in wind-down

The difference is what you negotiate before signing.

---

## Contract Terms That Protect Against Vendor Instability

**Data export rights:** The contract should state that you can request a complete data export at any time, in a standard format (CSV, Excel, or JSON), within 30 days of request. At termination (for any reason), this export must be provided.

**Escrow clause:** For large implementations, a source code or data escrow clause ensures that if the vendor closes, the school retains access to its data via an escrow agent.

**Service continuity clause:** If the vendor is acquired, the new owner must honor the terms of the existing contract for its full duration.

**30-day wind-down notice minimum:** If the vendor decides to cease operations, they must provide at least 30 days notice (more is better) and facilitate data export during this period.

## FAQ

**Q: How do we tell if a vendor is in financial difficulty?**
A: Signs include: support response times lengthening, key staff departures (LinkedIn is useful here), no product updates in 12+ months, changes in sales approach (unusual discounts, unusual urgency), and shifts in the team you interact with. None of these is definitive alone, but a pattern is concerning.

**Q: Is a larger vendor automatically more stable?**
A: Not necessarily. Large vendors can deprecate products or exit the school ERP segment. What matters is their specific commitment to the school ERP product line, not the size of the parent company.

**Q: What should we do if our current vendor is showing stability concerns?**
A: Document your concern. Request an executive meeting with the vendor to understand their product roadmap and company direction. Simultaneously, identify 2-3 alternative vendors and evaluate them so that you have an exit path if needed. Do not wait until the situation becomes urgent.

**Q: Is SaaS more stable than on-premise software in this regard?**
A: SaaS vendors can shut down just as on-premise vendors can. The difference is that with on-premise software, you retain a copy of the software (though not ongoing support or updates). With SaaS, cloud access ends when the vendor ends. This makes contractual data export rights more important for SaaS, not less.

**Q: How much weight should vendor stability get vs. product features in our evaluation?**
A: Give it significant weight. A great product from an unstable vendor is a worse choice than a slightly less impressive product from a stable, well-supported vendor. The 5-year total cost of an implementation disruption due to vendor failure is far higher than any short-term feature gap.
